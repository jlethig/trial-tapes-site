On the twenty-second of January, 1973, the Supreme Court of the United States decided a case called Roe versus Wade. Nine Justices had been handed the most divisive moral question in American life, and asked to answer it not with feeling, but with law.

But before the Court resolved anything, the Justice writing for the majority did something you rarely see in a Supreme Court opinion. He stopped to admit how hard the question was.

Justice Harry Blackmun opened not with the law, but with a kind of confession. He wrote, and these are his words:

We forthwith acknowledge our awareness of the sensitive and emotional nature of the abortion controversy, of the vigorous opposing views, even among physicians, and of the deep and seemingly absolute convictions that the subject inspires.

Deep and seemingly absolute convictions, on both sides. And still, the Court would have to choose. That was its burden: to settle a question the nation itself could not agree on, using a Constitution that never once names the right at the center of it.

On the twenty-second of January, 1973, nine men sat down to hand the country an opinion they already knew would satisfy no one. Not fully. Not on this. The question in front of them had divided pulpits and dinner tables, medical journals and state legislatures, for more than a hundred years.

Their job, in theory, was narrow. A single Texas statute. A single woman's claim. A single constitutional question. But everyone in that building understood that whatever they wrote would land far beyond Texas, and far beyond the page. The country was waiting to be told what its own founding document required of it.

And here is the difficulty the Justices could not write their way around. They were being asked to settle, with the tools of law, a disagreement that was not really about law at all. It was about conviction. About when a life begins. About what a woman owes the state, and what the state owes her. These are the kinds of questions that resist measurement.

So before the Court reached the statute, before it touched a single precedent, it stopped to define what it would, and would not, allow itself to do. It set a rule for its own conduct, and it stated that rule plainly. The line reads:

Our task, of course, is to resolve the issue by constitutional measurement, free of emotion and of predilection.

Constitutional measurement. Free of emotion. Free of predilection. It is a striking promise when you sit with it. The Court was telling the nation that it would approach the most charged question of the era as if emotion were something a judge could simply set down at the door.

Whether that is even possible is a tension the opinion never quite escapes. Because to measure something by the Constitution, you first have to decide what the Constitution measures. And on a question this old, and this contested, the Justices reached back for help, to a voice from an earlier fight over exactly that problem.

The voice belonged to Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes. Decades before, in a case called Lochner, Holmes had dissented from a Court that struck down a labor law simply because the majority disagreed with the economics behind it. Holmes thought that was the very trap a judge must avoid, mistaking his own preferences for the commands of the Constitution.

Blackmun reached for those words, and I'll read them as they appear in the opinion:

[The Constitution] is made for people of fundamentally differing views, and the accident of our finding certain opinions natural and familiar or novel and even shocking ought not to conclude our judgment upon the question whether statutes embodying them conflict with the Constitution of the United States.

A document made for people of fundamentally differing views. That is the whole burden of the Court in a single line. The Justices were not there to decide whose convictions were correct. They were there to decide whether a state could write its convictions into criminal law, and enforce them against a woman who did not share them.

That distinction, between what a judge believes and what the Constitution permits, is the ground the entire opinion is built on. And it is why, before we ever meet the people whose lives carried this case to Washington, it mattered so much that the Court drew that line for itself first.

The Court's burden was an abstraction. Nine people, one Constitution, a question the country could not settle among itself. But cases do not arrive as abstractions. They arrive as people, with names. Or, in this one, without them.

In March of 1970, in Dallas County, Texas, a pregnant single woman brought a lawsuit into a federal courtroom. The court would never learn her real name. To shield her, the record gave her a placeholder, the kind lawyers reach for when a true name would only cause harm. She would be known to history as Jane Roe. The opinion describes her in a single, spare phrase, and I'll read it exactly as written.

Jane Roe, a single woman who was residing in Dallas County, Texas.

That was nearly all the Court would say about who she was. What mattered in law was what she claimed. She was pregnant. She wished to end that pregnancy by a safe, licensed procedure. And Texas law made that impossible, while she could not afford to leave the state to find one. The name was a shield, not a person. The woman behind it is not ours to assume.

Roe was not alone. A second figure asked to join her case, and his stake in it was sharper, more immediate. He was a doctor, James Hubert Hallford, licensed and practicing in Dallas. He had already been arrested once under the Texas abortion statutes, and he was facing two more prosecutions under them. He asked the federal court for leave to intervene, to challenge the same laws that now hung over his own practice. The record states it plainly. Quote.

James Hubert Hallford, a licensed physician, sought and was granted leave to intervene.

How we describe his situation matters. Those prosecutions were pending. They were accusations, not convictions, unresolved on the record in front of the Court. He had not been found guilty of anything. What he had was a charge hanging over him, and a powerful reason to want these statutes struck down before they could be used against him again.

And then a third party. A married couple the opinion calls John and Mary Doe, another pair of pseudonyms standing in for real lives. They had no pregnancy at issue. They were childless, and they feared what a pregnancy might mean for them under a law that left them no lawful choice. The record notes their entry without ceremony. Quote.

John and Mary Doe, a married couple, filed a companion complaint.

So three cases moved together. And on June seventeenth, 1970, a panel of three federal judges in Texas handed down its ruling. They found the Texas criminal abortion statutes unconstitutional, void for being too vague and for reaching too far. It looked like a victory. And then the same court refused to do the one thing that would have given the ruling teeth. It declined to order anyone to stop enforcing the law.

A declaration without an injunction is a verdict without a remedy. The statutes were called void, and yet they stayed on the books. Still enforceable. Still hanging over Roe, and Hallford, and the Does. There was nowhere left to go but up.

But going up took time, and time created a problem the Court could not ignore. By the moment the case reached the highest court in the land, Jane Roe was no longer pregnant. The pregnancy that gave her the standing to sue had ended, one way or another, long before. Under the ordinary rules, a dispute that has already resolved itself is moot. And a moot case is dismissed.

The Court refused to let the calendar defeat the question. Pregnancy, it reasoned, is exactly the kind of condition that ends before the slow machinery of appeals can ever catch up to it. Blackmun put it in a line that has outlived the case itself. He wrote, quote.

Pregnancy often comes more than once to the same woman, and in the general population, if man is to survive, it will always be with us.

The legal phrase for it is, capable of repetition, yet evading review. A wrong that keeps recurring, but always finishes before a court can rule on it. If the law dismissed every such case as moot, the question could never be answered at all. So the Court kept it alive.

Which brings us to how long the Justices actually sat with this. The case was first argued before the Supreme Court on December thirteenth, 1971. And then, rather than rule, the Court held it over. It asked the lawyers to come back and argue the whole thing again. The reargument came on October eleventh, 1972.

The decision would not arrive until January of 1973. From first argument to final word, more than a year passed. This was not a question the Court was willing to rush. And to understand why they hesitated, you have to understand where the law they were judging had actually come from.

Before the Court could decide whether a woman had a constitutional right, it had to decide something less obvious. It had to decide where the law it was being asked to strike down had actually come from. And when the Justices went looking, they found a history at odds with the way most people imagined it.

There is a common assumption that laws against abortion are ancient, that they reach back through the centuries as a fixed and settled part of the moral order. The opinion treats that assumption as a question rather than an inheritance. And the Texas statute at the center of this case did not reach back through the centuries. Texas first wrote abortion into its criminal code in eighteen fifty-four.

The language settled quickly, and then it barely changed. The law Jane Roe challenged in nineteen seventy was, in its essentials, the same law Texas had written more than a hundred years before. Old, certainly. But not ancient. A creation of the nineteenth century, not of antiquity.

And the older law, the common law that came before these statutes, had been more forgiving than we tend to remember. Blackmun devotes long passages of the opinion to that history, to the attitudes of the ancient world and the rules of the early English courts. The picture that emerges is not one of timeless prohibition. The strict criminal ban was the new thing. The leniency was the old thing.

So if the ban was not an inheritance handed down from the distant past, where did the urgency to criminalize it come from? The opinion's answer points in an unexpected direction. Not to the pulpit, and not, at first, to the legislature, but to the medical profession itself.

The campaign had an institution behind it, and a date. In May of eighteen fifty-seven, the American Medical Association appointed a Committee on Criminal Abortion. That committee, the opinion records in the association's own words, was formed

with a view to its general suppression.

Suppression. Not regulation. Not study for its own sake. The organized physicians of the country set out to end the practice, and over the decades that followed, state after state rewrote its laws in response. The criminal prohibition that Jane Roe stood against was, in large part, their achievement.

Fourteen years later, in eighteen seventy-one, the same committee returned with its conclusions. The opinion calls the document a long and vivid report. And when Blackmun reaches for the clearest statement of the conviction on the other side of this case, the conviction that abortion is the taking of a human life, he does not summarize it or soften it. He lets that report speak for itself.

The committee did not reach for careful or qualified language. It spoke in the register of moral absolutes. And reading its words exactly as the opinion preserves them, this is what it said.

We had to deal with human life. In a matter of less importance, we could entertain no compromise. An honest judge on the bench would call things by their proper names. We could do no less.

Now consider what it means that those words appear where they do. They are quoted, at length, inside an opinion that would rule against the very law they helped to create. Blackmun did not bury the opposing conviction in a footnote. He gave it its fullest voice, in its own language, and let it stand on the page with all of its force intact.

That, in the end, was the weight the Court had taken on. Two histories, and two convictions, each sincerely held, each refusing the other any ground. On one side, a woman's claim over a decision about her own body and her own life. On the other, the claim stated here in eighteen seventy-one, and felt no less in nineteen seventy-three, that what was at stake was human life itself. The Court could not simply split the difference. It would have to find something in the Constitution large enough to hold them both, or honest enough to choose between them.

So the Court had walked the history. The common law, the doctors' crusade, the statutes that were younger than most people assumed. And now it arrived at the only question that finally mattered. If Texas could not simply do as it pleased with a pregnant woman's decision, then somewhere in the Constitution there had to be something that said so. The Justices had to find it. And finding it would not be simple.

Because the thing they were looking for was a right of privacy. The idea that there are decisions so personal, so close to the center of a life, that the government has no business making them for you. Marriage. Family. Whether and when to have a child. The Court believed such a zone existed. The difficulty was saying where, exactly, the framers had written it down.

And here, at the head of the most important section of the opinion, Justice Blackmun does something that still startles. He concedes the weakness in his own foundation. He writes, and these are his exact words, quote.

The Constitution does not explicitly mention any right of privacy.

Sit with that for a moment. The right at the very center of this case, the right the whole decision will stand or fall upon, is a right the document never names. Not in the Bill of Rights. Not in any amendment. The word simply is not there.

And yet, the Court says, an unnamed right can still be a real one. Blackmun begins tracing a line through the Court's own past. Cases about marriage, about contraception, about the raising of children and the privacy of the family. In each of them, the Justices had protected a freedom the Constitution does not spell out by name, because the freedom lived in the space the named rights were built to guard.

He reaches back toward an older case, Palko against Connecticut, and the question it framed: which liberties are so fundamental, so deeply rooted, that no free government could deny them. And it is precisely here that the text in front of me stops. The opinion I have been reading to you breaks off mid-thought, the reasoning still unfolding. So I will not pretend to carry you past where the record goes. We follow the argument exactly as far as the page allows, and not one sentence further.

But even with the page cut short, the shape of the collision is clear, because it has been clear from the very first sentence of this case. Two convictions, each held with the kind of certainty that leaves no room for the other.

On one side, the conviction the Court had already given voice to through the doctors of the last century. That this is human life. That a matter of this gravity admits no compromise, and that an honest mind is bound to call it by its proper name.

On the other, the woman the record calls only Jane Roe. A single claim, quiet beside the weight of the statute books. That the decision was hers. That it belonged to her life, her body, her future, and not to the State of Texas.

And so the question the Court carried, the question it had reargued and held and turned over for more than a year, came down to this. Could a right the Constitution never names be strong enough to take that decision out of the hands of the state, and place it in the hands of the woman herself. In nineteen seventy-three, nine Justices answered. And the answer they gave would not stay settled.

The opinion you and I have been following breaks off before its conclusion. But history records where it landed. When the writing was done, the Court affirmed that Jane Roe had the right to bring her case at all, and it agreed with the heart of the lower court's ruling: that the Texas criminal abortion statutes could not stand under the Constitution.

The decision was not a clean sweep. The Court reversed the relief that had been granted to Doctor Hallford, the physician, sending him back to face his pending prosecutions in the Texas courts. And it let stand the dismissal of John and Mary Doe, the married couple whose claim it found too speculative to decide. Affirmed in part. Reversed in part. The law is rarely as tidy as the headlines that follow it.

That contested answer would govern American law for nearly half a century. And then, in June of two thousand twenty-two, in a case called Dobbs against Jackson Women's Health Organization, the Supreme Court reversed itself, and Roe was no longer the law of the land. So hear this clearly. What we have walked through tonight is history, not the rule that governs today.

Which leaves a harder question for another night. The case that undid this one, and what it means when the same Court that can find a right can later decide to take it away.

Justice Blackmun had set out to resolve the most divisive question in the country by constitutional measurement alone, free, as he put it, of emotion and of predilection. Whether any Court could ever truly manage that, with a question like this one, is something the nation is still arguing about. And perhaps always will.
