The numbers grew so large they stopped feeling like numbers. Cities overwhelmed, hospitals stretched thin, families hollowed across two decades. When the Delaware Supreme Court sat down to write its opinion here, it did not begin with insurance fine print. It began with the size of the wound. Chief Justice Seitz opened with a single sentence, quoting a court that had come before him.

The opioid epidemic is "one of the largest public health crises in this nation's history."

But notice what this case is not about. These justices were not asked to decide whether CVS, the pharmacy chain named in thousands of opioid lawsuits, actually bore responsibility for that suffering. The question of blame was never before them. What they had to decide was narrower, and just as consequential. When the lawsuits arrived by the thousands, who would pay to fight them?

CVS had spent decades buying insurance for catastrophes exactly like this one. And so the tension at the heart of this case came down to a single, uncomfortable question. If the opioid epidemic truly was one of the largest public health crises in the nation's history, how could CVS's own insurers walk away owing nothing toward its defense?

By the time these cases reached Delaware, CVS was not facing a single lawsuit. It was facing thousands. Cities had sued. Counties and states had sued. Hospitals had sued. And so had the third-party payors, the health plans that foot the bill for prescriptions. They came at the company from every direction, and they all wanted the same thing. They wanted CVS to help pay for the opioid crisis.

Now, it is important to be precise about what these lawsuits actually claimed, because that precision turns out to be the whole case. The governments and the hospitals alleged that companies like CVS had helped fuel the epidemic, that pharmacies dispensed opioids they should have questioned, that the public was left to absorb the wreckage. None of that was ever proven here. CVS's responsibility for the epidemic was never decided in this litigation. What mattered, for our purposes, was the shape of the bill each plaintiff handed over.

And the bills were enormous. According to the complaints, the plaintiffs had spent staggering sums responding to the crisis. The cost of treating addiction. The cost of reversing overdoses. The cost of policing, of social services, of autopsies. And what the plaintiffs described as the cost of burying the dead. These were the damages the governments said they had suffered. These were the damages they wanted CVS to cover.

Different plaintiffs, different stories, but underneath them all, the same structure. Each one was asking to be reimbursed for what the epidemic had cost it. And one complaint, more than the others, shows you exactly what that looked like on the ground.

Consider the City of Philadelphia. Its complaint did something the others mostly didn't. It got specific. It described, in plain administrative detail, what the epidemic had done to a single city office. The medical examiner's office. The place that handles the dead.

Philadelphia told the court that the rising tide of overdose deaths had pushed that office to its breaking point. The surge in cases, the city wrote, and here I am quoting the complaint word for word,

required a doubling in the budget for supplies and materials (body bags, safety equipment, gowns, etc.) and the hiring of a new assistant medical examiner.

Read that again, and notice what it is. And what it isn't. Body bags. Safety equipment. Gowns. A new hire. These are line items. They are the budget of a city office stretched past its limits. The human catastrophe behind them is real, the deaths were real, but the way Philadelphia framed its claim, the injury it asked the court to recognize, was its own. It was the city's expense. It was not the bodily injury of any one person who died.

Hold onto that distinction, because it is about to become the most expensive distinction in the entire case. Every one of these lawsuits, no matter how vividly it described human suffering, was built to recover money the plaintiff itself had spent. And that, it would turn out, was a problem. Not for the plaintiffs. For CVS's insurance companies.

When the lawsuits started arriving, CVS did what any large company does when it gets sued. It turned to its insurers. For decades, CVS had paid premiums to two groups of insurance companies. The opinion refers to them simply as Chubb and AIG. CVS handed these opioid cases to them and said, in effect, this is what we pay you for. Defend us.

These were not new relationships. The policies stretched back to 1993. Dozens of them, more than sixty in all, written across a quarter of a century. And buried in every one of them was a single phrase that almost no one would think twice about. The policies promised to cover the sums CVS became legally obligated to pay as damages because of bodily injury.

Because of. Two small words. We are going to spend most of the rest of this story on them, because the insurers read these lawsuits and reached a conclusion CVS did not expect. They said no. These suits, the insurers argued, were not really claims for bodily injury at all. They were claims for economic loss. And economic loss, they said, was simply not what the policies covered.

So the insurers went to court first. Rather than wait to be sued for refusing to pay, they filed what is called a declaratory-judgment action in Delaware, asking a judge to declare, formally, that they owed CVS nothing. Not a dollar toward a settlement. Not a dollar toward a defense. The question was now in front of a court. And the answer would turn entirely on what those two small words were meant to mean.

To understand why this case came out the way it did, you have to go back three years, to a decision this same court had already made. Because by the time CVS and its insurers arrived in Delaware, the central question was not really open anymore. It had, in large part, already been answered.

In 2022, the Delaware Supreme Court decided a case called ACE American Insurance Company versus Rite Aid Corporation. Rite Aid, like CVS, was a pharmacy chain. Rite Aid, like CVS, had been named in the flood of opioid lawsuits. And Rite Aid, like CVS, had turned to its insurers and asked a simple question. Will you defend us, and will you pay?

And you can understand why a company in that position would expect the answer to be yes. CVS had carried liability insurance for decades, paying premiums on policy after policy, going back to 1993. The whole purpose of that coverage, as CVS saw it, was to stand behind the company when lawsuits came. And now the lawsuits had come, by the thousands.

But insurance is a creature of its exact words, and the words here mattered enormously. The policies promised to cover sums the company became legally obligated to pay, quote, because of bodily injury, or because of property damage. The entire dispute would turn on those two small words. Because of.

In the Rite Aid case, the court had already wrestled with what those words demand. And it drew a line that this case would come to rest on. There is a difference, the court held, between damages that are merely connected to a bodily injury somewhere along the chain, and damages a policy will actually pay for. Connection alone, it said, is not enough.

The justices distilled it into a single sentence. And it is worth hearing exactly as they wrote it, because every argument that came after had to survive it. Quoting their own earlier ruling, they put it this way.

[t]here must be more than some linkage between the personal injury and damages to recover 'because of' personal injury.

More than some linkage. In a sense, that is the entire case, compressed into four words. Because linkage there certainly was. The cities, the counties, the hospitals, the health plans, every one of them could trace their losses back to human beings in genuine pain.

But trace is the crucial word. According to their own complaints, these plaintiffs did not sue on behalf of the people who were injured. They sued for their own budgets. For the money spent on police and paramedics, on treatment and recovery programs, on autopsies and overburdened morgues. The harm they pleaded was financial. The bodily injuries, by and large, belonged to other people.

And so under Rite Aid, the link between CVS's alleged conduct and any individual's suffering, however real that suffering was, did not transform these economic claims into claims for bodily injury. The policies covered damages because of bodily injury. These lawsuits, the insurers argued, sought something different. They sought reimbursement for the cost the epidemic imposed on the public.

There is something genuinely uncomfortable in that distinction, and it is worth sitting with. The suffering described in these complaints was not abstract. And yet the law of insurance asked a narrower question than the law of conscience might. Not who was harmed, and not even who was responsible, but whose injury the policy was actually written to cover.

That argument went first to the Superior Court of Delaware, the trial court that would hear it long before it reached the justices. And the Superior Court sided with the insurers. Not once, but twice.

In August of 2023, it granted partial summary judgment for Chubb and AIG on the first wave of government suits. A year later, in August of 2024, it did the same again, this time sweeping in the government, hospital, and third-party payor cases together. By then, the parties had stipulated that a single ruling effectively resolved more than two thousand additional lawsuits.

And the trial judge did not hedge. He could not imagine, he wrote, that anything would ever, quote.

come about that will transmute or transform the various governmental claims into those for bodily injury or property damage covered by the [p]olicies.

Transmute. It is a striking word for a court to reach for, the vocabulary of alchemy, of turning one substance into another. And the judge's point was precisely that no argument, however clever, could perform that transformation. A claim for the cost of running a city, he was saying, is simply not a claim for a broken body, no matter how tightly the two are bound together.

But CVS was not out of arguments. The company still had theories it had not fully tested, and one court above the trial judge that had not yet spoken. Whether any of those theories could do the very thing the trial judge said could not be done was the question now waiting at the top of the appeal.

By the summer of 2025, the dispute had climbed about as far as it could go within the state of Delaware. CVS had lost in the Superior Court, not once but twice. And so the company brought its consolidated appeal to the Delaware Supreme Court, which agreed to hear it sitting en banc. All five justices. Every member of the court.

Arguments were submitted on the twenty-fifth of June. Jeffrey Schulman rose for CVS. Daniel Sullivan answered for the insurers, for Chubb and AIG. And the question in front of those five justices was, in one sense, narrow, and in another sense, enormous.

Narrow, because the controlling precedent was already on the books. Rite Aid had been decided three years earlier, and it pointed against CVS. Enormous, because billions of dollars in defense costs and possible settlements turned on whether CVS could find daylight between its own case and that precedent. To win, CVS did not need to overturn Rite Aid. It needed to show that these particular lawsuits were different.

CVS's strongest remaining theory went something like this. Yes, the governments and the counties had framed their lawsuits as claims for their own economic losses. But strip away the framing, CVS argued, and what were those losses, really? They were the downstream cost of residents who had been harmed. People who, according to the underlying complaints, had overdosed, had needed treatment, had died. If the suits were, at bottom, derivative of those bodily injuries, then the damages would flow because of bodily injury, and the policies would have to respond.

It was not a careless argument. If you accept that a county's swollen budget is simply the arithmetic of its residents' suffering, the logic has a real pull. The trouble for CVS was not the logic. The trouble was the text of the complaints themselves.

Take Summit County, Ohio. Its complaint did not leave the derivative question to inference. The County and the other plaintiffs wrote, in their own words, that they,

are asserting their own rights and interests and the [p]laintiffs' claims are not based upon or derivative of the rights of others.

Read that again. Not based upon or derivative of the rights of others. Summit County had spelled it out, in its own pleading, and it had said almost the opposite of what CVS now needed it to mean. The County was not suing on behalf of its injured residents. It was suing for itself. And so the very feature that might have pulled these claims under the policies was the feature the plaintiffs had deliberately disclaimed.

That left CVS with one more avenue. The policies covered damages because of bodily injury, yes, but they also covered damages because of property damage. And property damage, CVS suggested, might be read more loosely. Perhaps the contamination of communities, the strain on public systems, could qualify as harm to property in a way that opened the door the bodily-injury language had closed.

The Court was not persuaded, and it disposed of the idea in a single, even-handed sentence. Chief Justice Seitz wrote, quote,

We see no reason to treat "bodily injury" and "property damage" differently for coverage purposes.

The same two words governed both. If because of demanded a real, direct link between the injury and the damages, then it demanded that link whether the injury was to a body or to a piece of property. CVS could not slip through the second door simply by relabeling what it had failed to fit through the first.

There was one more matter the Court had to address, because a sister state had looked at nearly identical words and reached the opposite result. In Arkansas, a court had ruled in a case involving Walmart that this kind of policy language did reach opioid claims. The Delaware Supreme Court considered that ruling, weighed it, and declined to follow it. Delaware had Rite Aid, and Rite Aid controlled.

And so, on the eighteenth of August, 2025, the court affirmed. All five justices. The insurers, Chubb and AIG, owed CVS no duty to defend the opioid lawsuits, and no duty to indemnify whatever those lawsuits might one day cost. The decades of policies, the dozens of contracts stretching back to 1993, would not answer here.

It is worth being precise about what that holding means, and what it does not. The court had decided who pays the lawyers. It had not decided whether CVS bore responsibility for the epidemic itself. That deeper question, the one sitting underneath all the others, was never the question on the table.

On August 18th, 2025, the Delaware Supreme Court, sitting en banc, all five justices, delivered its answer. It affirmed the rulings below. CVS's insurers, Chubb and AIG, owe no duty to defend the opioid lawsuits, and no duty to pay.

It is worth being precise about what that means, and about what it does not. This was a coverage case. It decided who pays the legal bills, the defense lawyers and any settlements, under policies written as far back as 1993. It did not decide whether CVS bears responsibility for the opioid epidemic.

That second question, CVS's actual liability for the harm those complaints describe, was never placed before this court, and it was not answered here. The figures in those complaints, the autopsies, the body bags, the cost of burying the dead, remain allegations made by the governments and hospitals who sued.

What the court decided instead came down, in the end, to two words. Because of. The plaintiffs had pleaded their own economic losses, the cost of responding to a crisis. And under Delaware's Rite Aid precedent, that was not the same thing as damages because of a specific bodily injury, which is what these policies actually promised to cover.

Reasonable people can disagree about whether that is the right line to draw. The words were written long before anyone imagined a public health crisis of this scale, and they were asked to carry a weight their drafters never had in mind.

And here, the story does not quite close. A court in Arkansas, reading the very same policy language in a case against Walmart, came out the other way, and found that coverage was owed.

So the question Delaware has now answered is, across the rest of the country, still very much open. Which states' courts, and which insurers, will follow this reasoning, and which will follow Arkansas, is a story still being written.

For now, in Delaware, the matter is settled. The crisis was real. The cost was staggering. And the policies, the court held, simply never promised to pay for it.
