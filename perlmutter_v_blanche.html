In May of 2025, the official who ran the United States Copyright Office released a long-awaited report, still in prepublication form. It took up a question worth billions. Could artificial intelligence companies train their models on copyrighted work for free? Some uses, the report concluded, were likely fair use. Others likely required a license.

The next day, a Saturday, an email arrived from the White House, sent on the President's behalf. It was, in essence, a single sentence. It read, in full, that her

position as the Register of Copyrights and Director at the U.S. Copyright Office is terminated effective immediately.

Her name was Shira Perlmutter. She contends the firing was retaliation for that report. The government disputes it. But the Copyright Office is no ordinary agency. It sits inside the Library of Congress, an arm of the Legislative Branch.

And so the question headed for a federal courtroom was this. Can a court stop the President from reaching into Congress's own library to remove its chief copyright official?

To understand what happened to Shira Perlmutter, you have to understand where she worked. Because the office she ran is not where most people would guess. The United States Copyright Office, which registers the rights to every book, song, and film in America, does not sit inside the executive branch. It sits inside a library.

In 1897, Congress placed the Copyright Office within the Library of Congress, its own institution, an arm of the legislative branch. That can sound like trivia. Hold onto it. By the end of this story, it becomes the entire case.

The person who runs that office holds the title Register of Copyrights. Since October of 2020, that job had belonged to Shira Perlmutter, appointed by the Librarian of Congress, Carla Hayden. For nearly five years she did the quiet, technical work the role demands. She advised Congress. She oversaw registration. And in the spring of 2025, the hardest question in her field had a name. Artificial intelligence.

On May ninth, 2025, the Copyright Office released part three of a major report. The subject was generative AI, and one question above all. When a company feeds millions of copyrighted books and images into a machine to train it, is that free, or does the company have to pay?

The report did not give the tech industry the clean answer it wanted. It was a prepublication version, not a final rule. And its conclusion was carefully split. Some uses of copyrighted works in AI training, it said, are likely fair use. But some uses likely require a license. In plain terms, the office was telling the most powerful companies in the world that they could not always train on other people's work for free.

Now, here is the timing, and the timing is why we are talking about this case at all. Two days before that report, the President had fired Carla Hayden, the Librarian of Congress who had hired Perlmutter. Her longtime deputy stepped in as interim Librarian under the existing rules.

Then came the report. And the day after it, a Saturday, an email arrived in Shira Perlmutter's inbox. It came from Trent Morse, a deputy in the White House Presidential Personnel Office, writing on the President's behalf. Not a phone call. Not a meeting. A single line. It told her that her

position as the Register of Copyrights and Director at the U.S. Copyright Office is terminated effective immediately.

That was it. The day after she told the tech industry it could not always train for free, the chief copyright official of the United States was told her job was over. Perlmutter contends the two events are connected, that she was fired for the advice in that report. The government disputes that, and whether it is true is something a court would still have to decide. But the sequence, the report, and then the Saturday email, is not in dispute.

What came next was a question of who would replace her, and the answer ran straight through the Justice Department. The President invoked a statute called the Federal Vacancies Reform Act, and used it to designate his Deputy Attorney General, Todd Blanche, as Acting Librarian of Congress.

And Blanche, in turn, purported to install another Justice Department official, Paul Perkins, as Acting Register of Copyrights, the very job Perlmutter had just been told she no longer held. Notice the words the courts would later use. Purported. Designated. Because whether the President could lawfully reach into a legislative branch library and install his own people at the top was, and still is, the central unanswered question of this case.

Except Shira Perlmutter did not accept that she was gone. In a sworn declaration, she said she remained the Register. She said no official inside the Library of Congress had recognized Todd Blanche as Acting Librarian. And she said the Copyright Office was still operating, as it always had, under her.

The government disputes the legal effect of all of that. But picture the situation it created. Two people, each claiming to run the same office. Two chains of authority, each insisting it was the real one. And no one with the power to settle it except a court. That is the standoff Shira Perlmutter was about to carry into a federal courtroom.

Twelve days after that Saturday email, Shira Perlmutter went to court. On May twenty-second, twenty twenty-five, she sued. And she challenged two things at once. First, her own removal. And second, the chain of appointments that had installed Todd Blanche as Acting Librarian, and beneath him, a new Acting Register sitting in her chair.

What she wanted first, and most urgently, was an emergency order. In the language of the courts, a temporary restraining order. Think of it as pulling the brake. You ask a judge to freeze everything in place, right now, before either side has fully argued the merits, because waiting itself would cause damage that no later ruling could repair.

On May twenty-eighth, a week after she filed, the district court denied that request from the bench. The judge did not rule that her firing was lawful. He ruled that she had not shown the kind of harm that justifies pulling the brake. That kind of harm has a name in the law. Irreparable harm. And it would become the hinge on which this entire case would turn.

The fight then moved to the larger question of a preliminary injunction. And on July thirtieth, the judge handling the case, Timothy Kelly of the federal district court in Washington, gave his answer. He denied her again. And here is the part that matters most. He rested that denial on a single ground. Not on whether the President had the power to fire her. Only on irreparable harm. He found, again, that she had not shown it.

To get there, Judge Kelly leaned on a decision the Supreme Court had handed down only months earlier, in a case called Trump versus Wilcox. The Court there had set out how to weigh the competing harms when a removed government officer asks to be put back, and the balance it struck cut hard against people in Perlmutter's position. Judge Kelly quoted that standard directly. I'll read it as he wrote it.

the Government faces greater risk of harm from an order allowing a removed officer to continue exercising the executive power than a wrongfully removed officer faces from being unable to perform her statutory duty

Sit with that for a moment, because it tells you exactly how the deck was arranged. The harm to the government, from letting a removed officer keep wielding power, was treated as the weightier harm. Heavier than the harm to the officer herself, shut out of the job she says is lawfully hers. By that arithmetic, the person fighting to keep her position begins the contest already a step behind.

And this is where the whole case quietly turns. The Wilcox standard rests on one assumption, stated almost in passing. That the officer in question wields executive power. Power that belongs, in the end, to the President. Under that view, removing such an officer is presumptively the President's call, and a court should be slow to stand in the way.

So everything came to rest on a question that sounds almost academic, until you see what rides on it. Is the Register of Copyrights an executive officer at all? Because the Copyright Office does not sit in the Executive Branch. It sits inside the Library of Congress, on the Legislative side of the ledger, where it has lived since eighteen ninety-seven. If the Register answers to Congress rather than to the President, then the Wilcox math, built for executive officers, might not fit her at all.

But Judge Kelly was not yet persuaded of that. On August twentieth, after Perlmutter signaled she would appeal, she asked him for one more thing. An order holding her in place while that appeal played out. He denied it too, for the same reason as before. No irreparable harm.

Twice now, the same judge had turned her away on the same narrow ground, without ever deciding whether the firing itself was legal. To get that larger question in front of anyone, Perlmutter would have to climb one level higher, to the Court of Appeals. And there, the cramped argument about harm would crack open into something far larger. A question about the separation of powers itself.

By August of 2025, Shira Perlmutter had lost twice. Judge Kelly had turned her away in July, and again on the twentieth, both times on the same narrow ground. She had not shown irreparable harm. So she took the fight one level up, to the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit. The appeal was docketed on August fifth, case number 25-5285.

On its face, the question before the appellate court was small, almost technical. Not whether the President could fire her. Not whether Todd Blanche had been lawfully installed. Just this. Had Perlmutter shown the kind of harm that a court must act to prevent, before the larger questions could even be heard? But underneath that narrow question sat a much bigger one, about the separation of powers itself.

Perlmutter's lead counsel was Donald Verrilli, a former Solicitor General of the United States, a man who had argued some of the largest cases of his generation before the Supreme Court. In his reply brief, he went straight at the government's central claim. The government had argued that even if Perlmutter were wrongly removed, there was no real harm, because if she won, she could simply be paid for the time she lost. Backpay, in other words, would make her whole.

Verrilli's answer was to follow that logic all the way to its end, to a place the government surely did not want to go. He wrote this.

But if backpay were sufficient redress, then the President could temporarily replace the Senate parliamentarian before she issued a ruling that would prevent a bill that the President supported from becoming law.

Think about what that means. The Senate parliamentarian advises Congress on its own rules. If a president could temporarily remove her to clear the way for a favored bill, and the only remedy was a paycheck after the fact, then the damage would already be done. The interference with a coequal branch could not be undone by money. That was Verrilli's point. Some harms cannot be measured in backpay.

The argument landed. When the panel issued its order, the controlling opinion came from Circuit Judge Pan, joined by Circuit Judge Childs. And Judge Pan reached for an analogy of her own, one that captured the whole structural stake of the case in a single sentence.

She wrote, and I am quoting her directly.

The President's purported removal of the Legislative Branch's chief advisor on copyright matters, based on the advice that she provided to Congress, is akin to the President trying to fire a federal judge's law clerk.

A law clerk works for a judge. The judge sits in a branch of government separate from the President. And however powerful the President may be, he has no business reaching across that line to fire the people who serve the courts. Judge Pan's point was that the Register of Copyrights, sitting inside the Library of Congress, was that same kind of figure. An advisor to the legislative branch. If the firing happened as Perlmutter alleged, in retaliation for advice she gave Congress, then it crossed a line the Constitution was built to defend.

On September tenth, 2025, the panel ruled. By a vote of two to one, it granted an injunction pending appeal. Judge Pan and Judge Childs in the majority. Circuit Judge Walker in dissent. The order barred the officials from interfering with Perlmutter's service as Register while the case moved forward.

Now, it is worth being precise about what that ruling was, and what it was not. This was not a final judgment. The court did not decide that the firing was unlawful, or that Blanche's appointment was void. An injunction pending appeal is a holding action. A decision that Perlmutter should keep her post while the deeper questions are argued. And the hardest of those questions, the one Judge Walker would seize upon in dissent, was still waiting. Whose power was this, really?

By the time the divided panel issued its order, the case had stopped being about a single email and a single official. It had become an argument about the architecture of the federal government itself. Two judges had sided with Perlmutter. One had not. And the reasons they gave reveal exactly how much was riding on a question that sounds, at first, almost technical. It came down to a single question. Who does the Register of Copyrights actually work for.

Judge Pan wrote the controlling opinion, joined by Judge Childs. She had already compared the firing to the President reaching across the branches to remove a federal judge's law clerk. But underneath that image was a principle she stated plainly, and it is worth hearing in her own words.

In a system of checked and balanced power, the Executive has no authority to punish a Legislative Branch official for the advice that she provides to Congress.

That is the whole theory of the majority in a single sentence. The Copyright Office sits inside the Library of Congress, an arm of the Legislative Branch. If the Register's job is to advise Congress on copyright law, then punishing her for that advice, Pan reasoned, is the executive reaching into a branch that is not his to discipline. Backpay could not repair that kind of injury, because the injury was structural, not personal.

But Judge Walker saw the same facts and reached the opposite conclusion. And he did not bury his disagreement in qualifications. He rejected the majority's premise in his very first line.

Strip away the institutional geography, Walker argued, and look at what the Register actually does. Registering copyrights, setting fees, issuing regulations. To him, that was the exercise of one kind of power, and one kind only.

The Register of Copyrights exercises “executive” power.

If that is true, the rest follows almost mechanically. Officers who wield executive power generally serve at the President's pleasure, and the Supreme Court's recent decisions have leaned hard in that direction. Under the Wilcox standard the district court had already applied, a removed executive officer does not get to keep exercising that power while the courts sort out whether the firing was lawful. By Walker's logic, the majority had it backwards.

The government agreed with Walker, and it was not prepared to let a two to one order stand. It asked the full D.C. Circuit to step in, petitioning for rehearing. The opening of that petition framed the panel's decision as something close to a constitutional trespass by the courts themselves.

A divided panel of this Court has taken the extraordinary step of granting an injunction pending appeal to reinstate the Register of Copyrights, an Executive Branch officer who exercises executive power and who was removed from office at the President’s direction.

Note the framing. To the government, this was not a legislative official being protected. It was an executive officer being forced back into a job the President had lawfully ended. On October first, the D.C. Circuit declined to rehear the case, both as a panel and en banc. The injunction stood.

But standing in the D.C. Circuit is not the same as standing for good. On October twenty-seventh, the government carried its fight to the Supreme Court, filing an emergency application to stay the injunction. And in November, the D.C. Circuit did something telling. It put the appeal on hold, in abeyance, to wait and see what the Supreme Court would do. Which means the central question, the one this whole case turns on, has not been answered. No court has finally decided whether the President could remove the Register, or whether Todd Blanche was lawfully installed in the first place.

So here is where things stand as of the latest filing. Shira Perlmutter is still serving as Register of Copyrights. Not because a court has ruled she was wrongly fired, but because a court has ordered that she keep serving while the larger questions are resolved. That order is real, and it is in force. It is also, potentially, temporary.

The Supreme Court could lift the injunction at any time. If it does, the standoff between two claimed chains of authority resolves in the executive's favor, at least for now. If it does not, Perlmutter stays, and the case grinds on toward a decision on the merits that no court has yet reached.

Strip the case down and what remains is a question older than copyright, older than any report on artificial intelligence. It is the question of whether one branch of government can punish an official for the honest advice she gives another branch. The day Shira Perlmutter warned that AI companies cannot always train on copyrighted work for free, she could not have known her own job would become the test case. But it did. And the answer, when it finally comes, will reach far beyond the Copyright Office.
