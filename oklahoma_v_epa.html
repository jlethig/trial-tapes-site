In early 2023, the Environmental Protection Agency did something it rarely does all at once. In one rule, printed across the pages of the Federal Register, it rejected the clean-air plans of twenty-one states.

The states did what you would expect. They went to court. But the case that climbed all the way to the Supreme Court was not, in the end, a fight about ozone, or smokestacks, or whose air was being fouled. It was a fight about a courthouse.

When the decision came down in June of 2025, Justice Clarence Thomas opened the Court's opinion with a single, spare sentence.

Petitioners seek judicial review of the Environmental Protection Agency’s (EPA’s) disapprovals of two state emissions-control plans.

Notice what that sentence is about. Not whether the plans were good or bad. Only the right to be reviewed, and the question hiding inside it. When the federal government rejects your state's plan, where do you get to fight back. On your home turf, or on Washington's?

To understand where this fight ended up, you have to start with the air. In 2015, the Environmental Protection Agency tightened the national standard for ozone, the pollutant behind summer smog. And under the Clean Air Act, a stricter standard sets off a chain of obligations that runs all the way down to the states.

The law gives each state the first move. Every state has to write its own plan, showing how it intends to meet the federal standard. But there's a second requirement folded into that plan, and it's the one that matters here. It's called the Good Neighbor provision.

The idea is simple. Pollution doesn't stop at a state line. Emissions from a power plant in one state can drift downwind and foul the air in another. So the Good Neighbor provision requires each state to show that its own emissions won't interfere with its neighbors. Twenty-one states submitted plans. And that is where the trouble began.

The EPA reviewed the submissions and reached a decision. The agency concluded that all twenty-one of those Good Neighbor plans fell short. According to the EPA, each one failed to do enough to control emissions crossing state lines. So the agency rejected them.

Now, here's the choice that would come back to haunt the agency. The EPA didn't issue twenty-one separate rejections. It packaged all of them together into a single rule, published as one entry in the Federal Register, and built the whole thing around what it called its four-step framework. One rule, twenty-one states. That packaging decision would become the center of the case.

Two of those states would carry the fight all the way to the Supreme Court. Oklahoma, and Utah. And it's worth pausing on what the EPA actually said about each, because its reasoning was specific to each state.

In Oklahoma's case, the EPA said the state had tried to disclaim responsibility for certain emissions affecting Texas, and the agency wasn't persuaded. It faulted Oklahoma for what it called an insufficient evaluation of additional emissions-control opportunities. That was the EPA's assessment. Whether it was correct was a question for another day.

Utah got similar treatment. The EPA found that Utah had not adequately justified its attempt to discount certain emissions affecting Colorado, and it pointed to what it called technical and legal flaws in the state's arguments. Again, these were the agency's findings, not proven facts. The lawfulness of the rejections had not been decided by anyone. Hold onto that, because it matters to how this story ends.

The states did what you'd expect. They went to court. And so did the energy companies whose plants were on the hook, companies like PacifiCorp. But the question of which court turned out to be anything but simple.

The Clean Air Act contains a venue rule, a provision that decides where these challenges get heard. Some EPA actions can only be challenged in one place, the Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit, in Washington. Others can be challenged out in the regional circuit where the affected state sits. And the two sides disagreed, sharply, about which kind of action this was.

So the challenges scattered across the country, and the appeals courts split. Of the five circuits that ruled on the EPA's motions to move or dismiss these cases, four kept them at home, in the regions where the states were. Only one court went the other way. The Tenth Circuit sided with the EPA and shipped Oklahoma's and Utah's challenges off to Washington. That disagreement among the courts is what pulled the Supreme Court in.

The Tenth Circuit's reasoning was, on its surface, a kind of deference. The EPA had labeled its rule a single, nationally applicable action, and the court took the agency at its word. A nationally applicable rule, it concluded, belonged in the national court. When the case reached the Supreme Court, Justice Clarence Thomas, writing for the majority, had a blunt answer. The lower court, he said, had never wrestled with what the statute itself meant by its key word.

His verdict on that approach came down to a single sentence. I'll read it exactly as he wrote it.

But, that reasoning fails to grapple with what §7607(b)(1) means by that word.

That word. The entire case, twenty-one states, a sweeping federal rule, billions in compliance costs hanging over power plants across the country, would turn on a question of definition. What does the law mean when it sorts the EPA's actions into national and local? Everything that follows is the Court working out that answer.

To understand how this case was decided, you have to understand the single piece of federal law that everything turned on. It lives in the Clean Air Act, at section 7607(b)(1). And it says nothing about ozone, or emissions, or downwind neighbors. It is a venue rule. A map of which courthouse hears which dispute.

The map has two roads. If the EPA takes an action that is, in the statute's own words, nationally applicable, the challenge goes to one place and one place only. The Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit, in Washington. But if the action is only locally or regionally applicable, it goes to the regional circuit that covers the affected state. Home turf.

Then comes the wrinkle. Even a local action can be pulled back to Washington if it rests on what the statute calls a determination of nationwide scope or effect, and if the EPA finds and publishes that fact. So the Court faced two questions, and the order mattered.

Were these disapprovals national, or local. And if local, were they nevertheless built on a nationwide determination. Congress, in other words, made local review the default, and carved out a narrow exception for the genuinely national. The whole fight came down to which box twenty-one rejected plans belonged in.

The EPA's answer was that the box was obvious. It had taken twenty-one states, applied one analytical framework, and issued one rule in the Federal Register. One document, national in reach. To the agency, that made the action nationally applicable, and Washington the only proper forum.

Justice Thomas, writing for the Court, tested that claim with a comparison. Picture the opposite outcome. When the EPA approves a state's plan, no one doubts that it is a local action, a judgment about one state's submission. So what, the Court asked, is a disapproval. Thomas put it in a single line.

Thus, EPA's approvals and disapprovals are opposite sides of the same coin.

Opposite sides of the same coin. An approval and a disapproval examine the very same thing, one state's plan, and simply arrive at opposite conclusions. The verdict differs. The nature of the act does not. And if an approval is a local action, then so is its mirror image.

Which left the EPA's packaging argument with nothing to stand on. The agency could have issued twenty-one separate rejections, one per state, and no one would have called any single one of them national. Stapling them together into one omnibus rule did not transform twenty-one local decisions into a single national act.

But the EPA had a deeper point, and it is worth taking seriously. Every one of those disapprovals, the agency said, flowed from the same nationwide reasoning. The same framework, applied the same way, across the whole country. Look at the basis for the action, not just its label, and you would see something national underneath.

Justice Thomas refused to let that reasoning migrate into the first question. The basis for an action, he held, is the business of step two, not step one. Mixing the two, he warned, would unravel the whole design. In his own words,

We would make a hash of the CAA's venue framework if we looked to the basis for EPA's actions at the first step too.

Step one asks what an action does, whether its reach is national or local. Step two asks why the agency did it. Collapse the why into the what, and the careful order Congress wrote simply disappears.

So the first question had its answer. These were local actions, properly heard in the states' own regional courts. But the EPA was not finished. It still had that second step waiting, the argument that even a local action can belong to Washington if it rests on a determination of nationwide scope. And that argument would rise or fall on a single concession.

So the states had won the first round. The Supreme Court agreed that rejecting a single state's clean-air plan is a local act, the kind of decision that belongs in a regional courthouse. But the Environmental Protection Agency was not finished. It had a second argument waiting, and it was built into the very statute the states were trying to use.

The Clean Air Act's venue provision does not stop at asking whether an action is local or national. It has a second step. Even a locally applicable action, the kind that would normally stay in a regional circuit, gets routed to Washington's D.C. Circuit if it rests on a determination of nationwide scope or effect, and if the agency says so when it issues the rule.

And the EPA had something to point to. Buried in its analysis was a screening tool, a nationwide threshold. If a state contributed less than one percent of the pollution reaching a downwind area, the agency could screen it out. That one-percent line was the same for every state in the country. So the EPA argued that this nationwide determination was what drove the disapprovals, and that it should carry all of these cases to Washington.

It was a clever fallback. If the Court bought it, the states would lose on the second step what they had just won on the first. The whole fight over where these cases belonged came down to a single question. Was that one-percent threshold actually the thing that decided the outcome, or was it just the opening move?

Here is where the EPA's own words came back to haunt it. The screening threshold was never the end of the analysis. It was a filter at the front of a long process. After a state cleared or failed that one-percent cut, the agency still had to do the hard, specific work of examining that state's emissions, its controls, and its plan. And at oral argument, the EPA conceded as much.

Justice Thomas seized on that concession and put it at the center of the opinion. Quoting the transcript of the argument directly, he wrote:

As EPA does not dispute, even after it made its threshold determination, “there was still a lot of work to be done” before EPA could issue its disapprovals.

Think about what that admission gives away. If there was still a lot of work to be done after the nationwide threshold, then the threshold did not decide anything. The decisions turned on each state's own facts, evaluated one at a time. The one-percent line was a guidepost, not the driver. And with that, the EPA's second argument collapsed alongside its first.

So the holding landed where the states had hoped. The Supreme Court reversed the Tenth Circuit and held that Oklahoma and Utah were entitled to litigate their challenges in their home regional circuits, not in Washington. The cases were sent back down, closer to home, to the courts the states had chosen in the first place.

But it is worth being precise about what the Court did and did not do. This was a ruling about place, not about pollution. The Justices decided where the fight belongs. They said nothing about whether the EPA was right to reject these plans. The agency's disapprovals were not struck down. The question of whether those twenty-one state plans were actually deficient was left untouched, waiting for the regional courts to take it up on remand.

The states had won the forum they wanted, and on paper the result looked clean. Every participating Justice agreed on the outcome. But the decision that delivered that win was not nearly as united as a single, unanimous result might suggest.

On its face, the decision read like a clean sweep. The states had asked to fight closer to home, and the Court agreed. But the vote beneath that result was not as tidy as the outcome suggested. Justice Samuel Alito had recused himself, taking no part in the case, which left eight justices to decide it.

And all eight of them agreed on the bottom line. The states would get their day in their own regional courts. But agreement on the result is not the same as agreement on the reasoning. Justice Neil Gorsuch, joined by Chief Justice John Roberts, read the majority's logic, and declined to put his name to it. He wrote that while he reached the same destination, he could not travel Thomas's road to get there. His conclusion was careful, almost diplomatic. He wrote, and I am quoting him directly here:

Accordingly, I am unable to join the Court’s opinion, but I am pleased to concur in its judgment.

Pleased to concur in its judgment. Not in its opinion. In the careful grammar of the Supreme Court, that single word, judgment, carries enormous weight. Gorsuch agreed the states should win. He simply refused to endorse the path Thomas took to get them there.

So here is the arithmetic of the decision. Eight justices agreed on the result. Only six of them signed on to the reasoning behind it. Gorsuch and the Chief Justice peeled off, concurring in the judgment alone.

That distinction matters more than it might seem. A unanimous result makes a strong headline. But the rule that lower courts must actually follow lives in the reasoning, and that reasoning commanded a narrower majority. The states won decisively. The logic that delivered the win was left a little less settled than the vote count implied.

And so the case that began as a fight over air pollution ended as a fight over geography. Twenty-one states had their plans rejected in a single sweeping rule. Two of them, Oklahoma and Utah, had been told to take their grievances to Washington. The Supreme Court said no. They could fight on their home turf, in their own regional circuits.

But it is worth being precise about what the Court did and did not decide. It did not strike down the EPA's rule. It did not rule that the states' clean-air plans were fine, or that the agency had overreached. It decided one question only. Where the fight happens. Not who wins it.

The merits, whether the EPA was right to reject those plans in the first place, were sent back down to the regional courts. That question, the one everyone actually came for, is still waiting for an answer.

And when those cases land back in the courts closer to home, the real argument finally begins. Twenty-one states. Twenty-one plans. And a single question the Supreme Court left for someone else to settle. Were those plans truly deficient, or not.
