In 2019, a data breach exposed the payment-card numbers of Wawa's customers. The settlement that followed offered most of them a single thing. A five dollar gift card.

The lawyers who negotiated that settlement asked to be paid three point two million dollars.

When the case reached a federal appeals court, the judge who wrote the opinion opened with an admission. He wrote, in his own words.

Although we conclude that fairness and justice have been achieved by the settlement ultimately reached by the parties in this consumer class action, whether it was accomplished through the types of efficiency envisioned by the drafters of the rules probably rests in the eye of the beholder.

Fairness, the court conceded, had been achieved. Whether it was reached the way the rules intended was, in its own words, in the eye of the beholder. When customers get five dollars and their lawyers get millions, someone has to decide whether that is fair. And whether the man who stood up to object was protecting them, or just standing in the way.

To understand the fight over the fee, you have to start with the breach. And the breach started quietly. On March fourth, 2019, malware began running on Wawa's payment-processing systems. Not at one store. Across the chain. Every register, every fuel dispenser, potentially feeding card data to someone who had no business seeing it.

It ran for nine months. Wawa says it discovered the intrusion on December tenth, contained it two days later, and on December nineteenth, the company's CEO went public and told customers what had happened. By then, the malware had been collecting credit and debit card numbers, expiration dates, and cardholder names since the previous spring.

The lawsuits came almost immediately. The day after the disclosure, plaintiffs began filing class claims in the Eastern District of Pennsylvania. Within weeks, fifteen separate actions had been filed, and the court consolidated them into three tracks, one for financial institutions, one for employees, and one for consumers. It's that last group, the customers, whose story we're following.

And then, on January twenty-seventh, 2020, the thing every breach victim dreads actually happened. The stolen card data showed up for sale. It appeared on a dark-web marketplace with an almost cartoonish name, Joker's Stash, the kind of bazaar where stolen payment information is bought and sold in bulk. The harm was no longer hypothetical. It was listed, priced, and available.

The consumer plaintiffs filed their consolidated complaint in July of 2020. Their theory was negligence and breach of an implied contract. The argument, as they framed it, was that Wawa had a duty to safeguard the card data its customers handed over at the register, and that it failed to do so. It's worth being precise here. Wawa's legal liability for the breach was never decided by any court. The case settled before a judge or jury ever ruled on whether the company did anything wrong. What follows is a story about the settlement, not a verdict on the breach.

On September fifteenth, 2020, the lawyers sat down to make a deal. They met before a mediator named Diane Welsh, a former federal magistrate judge, and they stayed at it for nearly twelve hours. By the end of that long day, they had a settlement in principle. An arm's-length deal, hammered out by experienced lawyers on both sides, with a neutral in the room.

The settlement they built sorted customers into three tiers. If your card had merely been exposed, you could claim a five-dollar Wawa gift card, compensation for the time you spent watching your statements. If you had actually seen a fraudulent charge, you could claim a fifteen-dollar gift card. And if you could document real out-of-pocket losses, you could claim cash, up to five hundred dollars.

So that is the deal on the table. Gift cards for most, cash for the few who could prove they had been hurt. Each tier capped. It is, in many ways, a perfectly ordinary data-breach settlement. The kind that gets approved by courts across the country without anyone outside the courtroom ever noticing. And it might have been approved quietly here too, except for one number that hadn't been mentioned yet. The fee.

In November of 2021, a class member named Theodore Frank filed an objection. Now, Ted Frank is not an ordinary customer who happened to read the fine print. He is one of the best-known professional objectors in the country, a lawyer who has built a career out of showing up in class-action settlements and arguing that the deal favors the lawyers over the people they are supposed to represent. To his admirers, he is a watchdog. To class counsel, he is something else entirely.

Frank's objection wasn't a single complaint. It was a list. He attacked the way customers had been notified. He pointed to the low rate at which they had actually claimed their gift cards. And he went after the fee, the roughly three-point-two million dollars that class counsel asked the court to award them. His central charge was that the class lawyers were, in his words,

attempting to seize an excessive portion of the settlement proceeds.

Think about what that accusation actually means. Frank wasn't just saying the fee was too big. He was saying the lawyers had structured the deal to maximize what they took, while most of the customers they represented walked away with a five-dollar gift card, if they claimed anything at all. Whether that is a fair reading, or an unfair one, is the question the courts would spend the next four years trying to answer.

To understand why this case went on for years, you have to understand a single question of arithmetic. Class counsel asked the court to approve a fee of roughly three point two million dollars. The question was not whether they had done work. The question was what number that fee should be measured against.

Counsel pointed to the total value the settlement had made available to Wawa's customers. Add up every gift card that could be claimed, every dollar of potential reimbursement, and you arrive at a large figure. Measured against that pool, three point two million looked modest, even reasonable.

Frank pointed somewhere else entirely. He argued that the honest yardstick was not what the settlement made available, but what customers actually claimed. And here the two numbers diverged sharply, because in a data-breach settlement built largely on five-dollar and fifteen-dollar gift cards, most eligible customers never filed a claim at all.

Think about what that gap means. If you measure the fee against money offered, the lawyers look like they captured a small slice of a generous deal. If you measure it against money that real people actually took home, the same fee suddenly looms much larger over a much smaller pile. Same settlement, same fee, two completely different stories.

Counsel's answer was that they could not control how many customers filed claims, and that a defendant's willingness to put money on the table is itself a real benefit they negotiated. Frank's answer was that fees untethered from actual payouts invite lawyers to build settlements that look generous on paper and pay out little in practice. Both positions, the court would acknowledge, had force.

While that argument was unfolding, something else was happening in the background. Frank's objections were not just being argued against. They were changing the deal.

In the weeks after Frank filed, class counsel rewrote the settlement, not once, but three times. The first change made the Tier-1 gift cards automatic for roughly five hundred and sixty thousand customers who had used Wawa's mobile app, so they would receive their five dollars without lifting a finger. The expiration date on those cards was removed.

Then came the change that mattered most to the fee fight. Under the revised deal, if the court awarded counsel less than the full three point two million dollars, the difference would no longer flow back to Wawa. It would be redirected to the customers holding gift cards.

Now, in civil debate there are two honest ways to read a string of last-minute amendments. One reading says the system worked exactly as designed. An objector raised concerns, and the parties responded by making the deal better for the class. The other reading is more skeptical. It asks why those improvements only appeared once a watchdog was standing over the table, and what the original draft would have looked like if no one had objected at all.

That skeptical reading had two specific targets, and the courts gave them a name. Red flags. Not proof of wrongdoing, but features of a settlement that experience has taught judges to examine closely.

The first was a so-called clear-sailing provision. In plain terms, that is an agreement by the defendant not to contest the lawyers' fee request. The worry is subtle. If a defendant promises not to fight the fee, counsel may have less reason to push hard for the class, because their own payday is no longer at risk.

The second flag was the fee reversion, the very feature the amendments had tried to fix. In its original form, any money the court trimmed from the fee would have reverted to Wawa, the defendant. The concern there is even sharper. It can give a defendant a quiet incentive to support a high fee request, because whatever the lawyers do not collect comes back home.

So the question on the table was stark. Were these red flags evidence that class counsel and Wawa had quietly cooperated to protect the lawyers' fee at the customers' expense? Or were they, as counsel insisted, the ordinary furniture of a complicated settlement, present by accident rather than design?

On the reversion, Wawa's lead attorney, Gregory Parks, gave a sworn explanation for how it ended up in the agreement. His account was not that the reversion was a clever lever. It was that no one had thought about it at all. He wrote, and these are his own words from the declaration.

I did not anticipate any possibility that the District Court would not approve a $3.2 million attorney’s fee award. There was therefore no discussion of what would happen in that event.

In other words, the reversion was an oversight born of confidence. The lawyers were so sure the full fee would be approved that they never drafted for the alternative. Frank's response cut the other way. A red flag, he argued, does not stop being a red flag just because the people who created it say they never meant anything by it.

And that is the knot the case had tied itself into by the time it reached the appeals court a second time. Everyone agreed the structures looked suspicious. The dispute was over what they actually proved. Resolving it would come down to something a courtroom is rarely comfortable resting on. Whether to believe the lawyers.

To understand how this case finally ended, you need to know that the fight you have been following was not its first trip to the appeals court. It was its second. Back in 2023, in a decision the lawyers now call Wawa One, a panel of the Third Circuit had looked at this very fee, and it had not been satisfied.

That first panel vacated the roughly three million dollar award and sent the case back down to the trial court. Take another look, it said. Scrutinize those side agreements. Ask hard questions about whether a fee this size was truly proportional to what the class actually received. It was, in effect, an instruction to show your work.

The judge who received that instruction was Gene Pratter, who had sat on the federal bench in Philadelphia for nearly twenty years. And she did not treat the remand as a formality. Through the winter of 2023 and into the early months of 2024, she held more hearings, demanded more sworn declarations, and walked back through the record piece by piece.

By the time she was finished, Judge Pratter had written more than a hundred pages of opinions and presided over many hours of proceedings on a single question. Was a three point two million dollar fee fair, in a settlement that handed most customers a five dollar gift card. In April of 2024, she gave her answer. She re-approved the fee, finding no secret clear-sailing agreement, and concluding that the troubling reversion clause had been an oversight rather than a design.

Ted Frank, unpersuaded, filed his second notice of appeal that May. And here the story takes a turn that has nothing to do with the law, and everything to do with the people inside it.

On the seventeenth of May, 2024, only days after that appeal was filed, Judge Gene Pratter died. She had given the better part of two years to this settlement, and more than a hundred pages to defending her reasoning. When the Third Circuit at last ruled on whether that reasoning would stand, the judge who had done the work was no longer there to hear the answer.

So the case arrived at the Third Circuit a second time, in the spring of 2025. By now the dispute had narrowed to something almost philosophical. Frank's argument depended on reading the red flags, the clear-sailing clause and the fee reversion, as the fingerprints of a quiet bargain between lawyers who were supposed to be on opposite sides of the table.

But the record held something that theory had to overcome. Sworn statements. Gregory Parks, the lawyer who led Wawa's defense, had told the court under oath exactly what he believed had happened during the negotiations. And he put it about as plainly as a lawyer can. He swore, and I am quoting his declaration directly:

[t]here was no ‘coordinated’ advocacy, no collusion, and nothing other than honest[,] hard-fought negotiations throughout a long process of discussing, negotiating, and executing this sophisticated Settlement of a large matter.

That, of course, is what Wawa's own lawyer said. The real question was whether the judge who had watched the entire process unfold believed him. And on that point, Judge Pratter left no doubt. After weighing the declarations from every lawyer in the room, she made a finding, the kind of finding that sits at the very center of a trial judge's authority. She wrote, and the choice of words matters here:

the Court unreservedly credits their declarations regarding how the fee reversion came about through omission and was ultimately removed from the Settlement Agreement.

Unreservedly. That is not a hedged word. And it carries real weight, because an appeals court never gets to sit in the room and watch the witnesses for itself. When a trial judge looks the lawyers in the eye and decides that she believes them, the court above her disturbs that judgment only rarely, and only for the strongest of reasons. To win, Frank would have to show that Judge Pratter's conclusion was not merely debatable, but clearly wrong.

On the twenty-fifth of June, 2025, the Third Circuit gave its answer. It affirmed. The fee would stand. But the court was careful not to wave the concerns away. It agreed that clear-sailing clauses and fee reversions are genuine warning signs, the sort of provisions that should make any conscientious judge slow down and look harder.

What the court would not do was treat a warning sign as a confession. There is, the judges acknowledged, a structural risk built into every class action, because the lawyers can come to have interests of their own. But Judge Smith, writing for the court, drew a careful line under what that risk does, and does not, mean. He wrote:

But our recognizing the potential for conflict arising out of the unique nature of the class action device does not require us to view every action the lawyer takes in representing a class as inherently nefarious or even suspicious.

And so the arithmetic held. The lawyers kept their fee, a little over three million dollars, close to the three point two million the deal had contemplated from the start. The customers kept their gift cards. And a case that had climbed to the appeals court twice, and consumed more than a hundred pages of one judge's life, finally came to rest.

But notice what the court did not decide. It found that the settlement was fair. It did not find that the system had worked efficiently, or cheaply, or in the way the people who drafted these rules once imagined. That harder question the court had set aside at the very beginning, in a single phrase. Whether justice here was done well, or merely done, it said, rests in the eye of the beholder.

On the twenty-fifth of June, 2025, the Third Circuit gave its answer. After two appeals. After more than a hundred pages of opinions from the district court. After the gift cards, the amendments, and the accusations of self-dealing, the panel affirmed. The fee would stand.

And yet the court refused to call the objections frivolous. It agreed that a clear-sailing provision, where a defendant promises not to contest the lawyers' fee, and a reversion, where leftover fee money flows back to the defendant, are precisely the kind of terms that should make a judge look twice. The court called them legitimate red flags. The real dispute was never whether the flags existed. It was about what they meant.

Judge Smith acknowledged the danger plainly. A class action, by its very design, hands enormous power to lawyers who answer to people they will never meet, people who may never even know the case exists. The potential for conflict is built into the machine. And then, having admitted all of that, he drew a line.

But our recognizing the potential for conflict arising out of the unique nature of the class action device does not require us to view every action the lawyer takes in representing a class as inherently nefarious or even suspicious.

In other words, suspicion is not proof. The district court had looked the lawyers in the eye, weighed their sworn declarations, and believed them. It found no collusion, only a deal that was awkwardly drafted and then repeatedly improved. An appeals court does not undo that kind of credibility finding lightly, and this one declined to.

There is a quiet postscript to all of this. The judge whose work was affirmed, Gene Pratter, had sat on the federal bench for nearly twenty years. She held the hearings, read the declarations, and wrote those hundred pages. She died in May of 2024, more than a year before the opinion that vindicated her ever issued. She never read the words affirming what she did.

So what, in the end, was actually won? The customers who filed a claim kept their gift cards. Five dollars, fifteen if a fraudulent charge appeared, up to five hundred for documented losses. And the lawyers kept their fee. Just over three million dollars. The full amount they had asked for from the start.

It would be easy to call that a defeat for Ted Frank. It is not that simple. Frank lost the fight over the fee, but along the way he changed the deal. His pressure made the gift cards automatic for hundreds of thousands of customers. It stripped out the expiration date. It sent leftover money to cardholders instead of back to Wawa. The class was better off for his having shown up.

And yet his critics have another name for him. Not watchdog, but toll collector. A man who stands at the end of every settlement and asks what it will cost to make him go away. Both descriptions can be true at once. That is the uncomfortable center of this story.

The court said at the outset that fairness had been achieved, but that whether it was reached efficiently rests in the eye of the beholder. That was not coyness. The court genuinely could not say whether a system that pays customers five dollars and their lawyers three million is working as intended, or quietly failing. It left the question where it found it.

Next time, we sit with that question a little longer. When does a professional objector cross the line from watchdog to toll collector. And whether the rules of our courts should reward him for standing there at all.
