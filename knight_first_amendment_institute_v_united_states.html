Knight First Amendment Institute v. DHS | 2017 | The Border Search Files | CourtRoom Ep 0

A press-freedom institute sues for the border search files

Full Transcript

There is a promise written into American law, and it is a simple one. Because the government works for the public, the public is entitled to see what it does. That promise has a name, the Freedom of Information Act, and a court in Washington once put it about as plainly as a court can.

The fundamental principle animating FOIA is public access to government documents.

So in March of 2017, a press freedom group at Columbia University, the Knight First Amendment Institute, set out to test that promise. It asked Homeland Security a straightforward question. What are the rules when your officers search a traveler's phone or laptop at the border. The answer was silence. No records, no denial, nothing at all. And within days, the institute sued.

What followed was not a fight about whether to release the files. It was a fight about which ones could stay sealed. And beneath it lay the question the court would have to answer. When the public's right to know collides with privacy and national security, which one gives way, and who decides what stays hidden.

To understand this case, you have to go back to 2009. That August, the Secretary of Homeland Security announced a revised set of policies. They governed how two of the department's agencies, Customs and Border Protection and Immigration and Customs Enforcement, could search the electronic devices that travelers carry across the border.

The policies gave officers broad discretion. A phone, a laptop, a tablet, the kind of device that holds your photographs, your messages, your contacts, the record of your whole life, could be inspected at a port of entry. And the Secretary, aware that this touched something delicate, directed the department's Office for Civil Rights and Civil Liberties to study what these searches meant for the rights of the people subjected to them.

What that machinery produced, over the years that followed, was a trove of internal records. Search policies. Officer guidance. Internal assessments. A report generated each time a device was examined. Paper, in other words, that documented exactly how the government was using a power most travelers never knew it had.

Enter the Knight First Amendment Institute at Columbia University. It is a press-freedom organization, built to defend the freedoms of speech and of the press in the digital age, and it had been following the reporting on these border searches with growing concern.

The institute's interest was not abstract. Reporters cross borders for a living, and a device search can expose a journalist's sources, an attorney's privileged files, a traveler's entire private world. If the government had built a system for inspecting all of that, the institute believed the public had a right to see how that system worked.

So on the fifteenth of March, 2017, it filed a request under the Freedom of Information Act with the Department of Homeland Security and with Immigration and Customs Enforcement. It asked for the records behind the border searches of travelers' electronic devices. One week later, on the twenty-second, it sent an identical request to Customs and Border Protection.

The Freedom of Information Act is a simple instrument with a large ambition. It lets any person ask the federal government for its records, and it puts the burden on the government to justify keeping anything back. The institute was not asking a favor. It was invoking a right.

And then, nothing. The agencies did not produce the records. They did not formally deny the request. They did not, so far as the institute could tell, respond at all.

So the institute did not wait. On the twenty-seventh of March, 2017, just days after sending its requests, it filed suit in federal district court in Washington. Weeks later, on the nineteenth of April, it amended its complaint, sharpening the case it meant to bring.

And here is the thing to understand about the fight that followed. It was not, in the end, about whether the government would release anything at all. The agencies would eventually turn records over. The battle was over what they could keep back, and on what authority.

Because the Freedom of Information Act, for all its openness, contains exceptions. Nine of them. Carve-outs that let the government withhold records to protect personal privacy, or sensitive law enforcement techniques, or the confidential deliberations that go into shaping policy. The institute wanted the files. The Department of Homeland Security, Customs and Border Protection, and Immigration and Customs Enforcement each reached for those exemptions. And the question of who was right would land on the desk of a United States district judge.

To understand what the Knight Institute was fighting to see, you have to understand what a border device search actually produces. When a Customs and Border Protection officer selects a traveler for inspection and examines their phone or their laptop, the search does not simply end when the device is handed back. It generates a record. The agency calls it an Electronic Media Report.

And that report is not a thin thing. According to CBP's declarant, an official named Patrick Howard, an Electronic Media Report can capture a traveler's country of birth, their ethnicity, their citizenship, their occupation, and more. One document, holding the rough contours of a person's life, generated at a border crossing.

Now, not every search is random. Some are what the agency calls targeted, meaning an officer chose that particular traveler for a reason. But Howard was careful to explain that such a choice could rest on any number of grounds, national security, law enforcement, or the safety of the officers themselves. The fact that a device was searched, in other words, tells you nothing certain about the person who carried it.

And that, the agency argued, was exactly why even fragments of these reports had to stay sealed. Release a piece here, a detail there, and you risk exposing the very travelers whose devices were searched. CBP's position was that privacy does not live only in the whole file. It lives in the bits.

But the Knight Institute had an answer to all of this, and it went to the very reason the institute had filed suit. The public, it argued, had a pressing need to see these records. Because there were reports, troubling ones, about who exactly was being searched.

In its motion for summary judgment, the institute put the claim before the court in its own words. It pointed to, quote...

widespread reports that border officers were targeting journalists and Muslims, including Muslim Americans, in exercising their broad discretion to conduct these invasive searches.

It is worth being precise about what that was, and what it was not. This was the institute's contention, drawn from press accounts and traveler complaints. It was not a finding of fact. No court had concluded that border officers had singled out reporters, or people of a particular faith. The institute's point was that the public could not even begin to weigh the charge without the records that might prove it, or lay it to rest.

CBP's reply turned the institute's logic on its head. The agency did not deny that these records touched a matter of public concern. It argued instead that the privacy cost of releasing them was severe. And to show the court just how severe, it offered a hypothetical. A small story, really, about a man who does not exist.

Judge TSC set the agency's example down in the opinion. Imagine, CBP said, a single, innocent-looking detail pulled from one of these reports. Here is how the court captured the argument. Quote...

CBP posits that releasing information that an Italian accountant, whose electronic device was searched, just returned from a two-week trip to Japan creates a substantial likelihood that his “friends, neighbors, and clients” would recognize the description of someone they know.

Think about what the agency was really saying. Not a name. Not a passport number. Just a profession, a nationality, and a recent trip. Three ordinary facts. And in the right small circle, those three facts point to exactly one person. That was CBP's entire theory of privacy. Re-identification does not require a name. It requires only enough pieces.

So the court was left holding two competing truths. The public's interest in knowing how its officers wield this power at the border, and the searched traveler's interest in not being named, or recognized, in a government file. And that collision, it turned out, was only half the fight. Because the agencies had a second argument, one that had nothing to do with privacy, and everything to do with what they warned would happen if the wrong people ever learned how these searches really worked.

There's a second front in this case, and it has nothing to do with privacy. It has to do with secrecy of a different kind. Among the records Knight wanted was an internal ICE handbook. The document that tells agents at the border how to decide when a traveler's phone or laptop gets searched.

ICE refused to hand over key sections of it. The agency leaned on a provision of the law known as Exemption 7(E), which lets the government withhold law enforcement techniques when disclosure could help someone evade the law. And to make that case, ICE turned to a declarant named Toni Fuentes.

Fuentes filed a sworn declaration explaining exactly what was at stake if those handbook sections became public. The language is bureaucratic, a little rough around the edges. But the warning underneath it is blunt. Here is what she wrote, quoting directly.

The withheld information in this internal handbook provide law enforcement techniques and guidelines to special agents at the border to determine when a search of an [sic] electronic devices are [sic] warranted. Disclosure of the content of these sections would allow the human traffickers or drug smugglers [to] know the circumstances under which they may be subjected to a search at or near the border, allowing them to change their tactics because they know the techniques in which the agents conduct searches at the border.

Strip away the rough grammar, and the argument is clean. The handbook tells agents when to search. Publish it, and the people the agents most want to catch, the smugglers, the traffickers, learn precisely where the tripwires are. Give them the map, and they simply walk around it.

This is the hard edge of the whole FOIA bargain. The same transparency that lets the public watch its government can, in the wrong hands, become a manual for defeating it. And so the law carves out room for the agencies to keep certain operational secrets, even from the citizens they answer to.

But Knight had an answer to all of this. Remember the institute's central claim, that border officers had singled out journalists and Muslims, including Muslim Americans, for invasive searches. If that were true, the public interest in these records would be enormous. Enough, perhaps, to outweigh the government's secrecy. The whole argument rested on the idea that something at the border had gone badly wrong.

The court was not persuaded. At least, not on the record in front of it. Knight had pointed to specific complaints, individual travelers who said their devices had been searched and believed they'd been singled out. But a complaint, the judge reasoned, is not the same thing as proof.

On the question of whether these searches revealed a pattern of unlawful conduct, the judge wrote:

The fact that a CBP border search of electronic devices resulted in a specific complaint, without more, fails to provide compelling evidence that the search was conducted unlawfully or that the agency is engaging in a pattern of unlawful searches.

It's a careful line, and it's worth sitting with for a moment. The judge was not saying the searches were lawful. The judge was saying that a single complaint, standing alone, does not prove they weren't. The burden was on Knight to bring compelling evidence of wrongdoing. And on this record, the court found it hadn't.

That mattered, because it weakened the public-interest side of the scale. If you cannot show the government did something wrong, it is harder to argue the public urgently needs the files to find out. But Knight had one move left. And it went to the heart of what FOIA is even for.

By now, two kinds of secrets had been fought over in this case. The privacy of a single traveler, and the methods an officer uses to decide whom to search. But there was a third secret at issue, and it was the most abstract of the three. It was the government's claim to a quiet space in which to deliberate before it decides.

To understand it, you have to go back to 2009, the year this story really begins. After the Department of Homeland Security revised its rules for searching travelers' devices, the Secretary asked an internal office, the Office for Civil Rights and Civil Liberties, to study what those rules were doing. That office gathered the concerns of advocacy groups, weighed them, and drafted recommendations and legal analyses for the Secretary to consider.

When Knight asked for those documents, the Department refused to hand them over whole. Its declarant, James Holzer, invoked one of FOIA's narrower exemptions, the one that shields the government's internal deliberations. The argument was that these were not decisions. They were drafts of advice, the back and forth of officials still making up their minds.

And on this point, the court largely agreed with the government. The recommendations, the judge found, were predecisional and deliberative. They did not purport to state the current position of the Department. They were the thinking that comes before a policy, not the policy itself, and FOIA has long protected that kind of candor.

Knight had an answer, and it reached back to an older fight over government secrecy. In a case called ACLU versus the Department of Justice, a court had confronted the same instinct, the same official reluctance to expose how a law enforcement policy actually works. The principle that came out of that case sat at the very center of what Knight was arguing here.

The judge quoted that earlier decision directly.

Whether the government’s tracking policy is legal or illegal, proper or improper, is irrelevant to the case . . . Matters of substantive law enforcement policy are properly the subject of public concern, whether or not the policy in question is lawful.

Read that again, because it is the hinge of the whole dispute. The public's right to examine what its government does is not conditioned on first proving the government did something wrong. The policy itself is the public's business. Whether it is lawful is a separate question, and not the one that decides whether the people get to see it.

But a principle, however clear, does not by itself decide a case. The court still had to weigh it against the privacy of travelers and the operational secrets of border enforcement, exemption by exemption, document by document. And when the ruling came, it did not hand a clean victory to anyone.

The Department of Homeland Security and Immigration and Customs Enforcement prevailed on the withholdings they had defended. Their declarations, the court held, did enough to justify keeping those records sealed.

Customs and Border Protection did not fare as well. The court denied its motion, in part without prejudice, on the deliberative-process claims and on the privacy exemption covering personal information. Knight's own cross-motion was denied too. No one had won outright, and so the judge ordered the parties to brief the privacy and public-interest questions all over again.

As for the heart of Knight's case, the claim that officers had singled out journalists and Muslims for these searches, the court was unmoved on this record. A single complaint about a border search, it found, standing alone, was not compelling evidence that any search had been unlawful, or that the agency was engaged in a pattern of them.

So the larger question, the one the judge had named at the very beginning, the principle that FOIA exists to give the public access to what its government is doing, remained exactly that. A principle. Stated, honored, and still unsatisfied.

In the end, no party fully prevailed. The court sealed some files and reopened others. It found no proof that anyone had been targeted. And it sent the privacy and public-interest questions back for another round of argument.

Which means that the thing Knight had gone to court to learn, what its own government does with the phones and laptops of people crossing the border, remained, in large part, unknown. The public's right to know had pried some of the files loose. Privacy and national security had kept the rest in the dark.

On rebriefing, Customs and Border Protection would have to justify its withholdings all over again, exemption by exemption. But here the record runs out. The opinion that tells this story breaks off mid-analysis, in the middle of the government's case for its operational secrets. The rest of the fight is, for now, untold.

What remains is the principle the judge began with. That a free society is one whose government can be made to show its work. The argument over how far that goes, at the border, with a traveler's device in an officer's hands, was not settled here. It was only adjourned.
