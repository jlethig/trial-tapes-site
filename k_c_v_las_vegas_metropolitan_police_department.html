On the last day of 2015, in a quiet corner of Las Vegas, a man lay on the ground, and the officers who had just fired on him moved in to search his body, certain they would find the gun he had been carrying. The court later recorded what they found in one plain sentence.

They found a black phone in Childress's right front pocket but no gun.

His name was Keith Childress Jr., a fugitive wanted in Arizona on charges of armed robbery and kidnapping. But the threat the officers believed was in his hidden right hand had never been a gun. It was a phone. And Childress was the father of three children who would now grow up without him.

That is the wound at the center of this case. Not whether a man had died, which was never in dispute, but whether the deadly mistake that killed him was, in the eyes of the law, a reasonable one. And whether the men who made it owed his children anything at all.

Before the lawsuits, before the appeals, before a panel of federal judges would spend a winter trying to decide what the law had required of three police officers, there was a young man named Keith Childress Jr. He was twenty-three years old. And on the last day of 2015, he was a fugitive.

Two years earlier, in March of 2013, a grand jury in Arizona had indicted him on two charges: armed robbery, and kidnapping. He did not stay in Arizona to answer for them. He crossed the state line into Nevada, and for a while, the warrants simply followed his name through the system, waiting for someone to find him.

Think about what two years of running actually means. It is not a single dramatic escape. It is a slow, grinding way of living. Always moving, always looking back, always one knock at the door away from the thing you are trying to outrun.

And there is one more fact worth holding onto before the story moves, because it is the fact this whole case will eventually turn on. Keith Childress was a father. He had three young children. All three of them were about to outlive him.

On December 30th, 2015, United States Marshals found him. He was staying at his uncle's house in Las Vegas, an ordinary residential block near the edge of the city. The Marshals did not move in that night. They watched the house, and they waited for morning.

This is what a federal fugitive task force does. It does not improvise. It assembles, it coordinates, it plans the approach. By the time those Marshals were ready to knock, a great deal of information about Keith Childress had already been gathered, and was already being shared.

Morning came on December 31st, the last day of the year. The Marshals moved toward the house to make the arrest. And Keith Childress did what he had done for two years. He ran.

Now, here is where it gets complicated, and where the real tragedy of this case begins. Because information travels ahead of a fleeing man. Over the radio, from one agency to another, a picture of Keith Childress was being built in the minds of the officers who were about to chase him. And part of that picture was simply wrong.

A United States Marshal relayed what Childress was suspected of. But the words that went out over the air were not the words written on the Arizona indictment. The court that later reviewed all of this singled out that description, and pointed out that it had been made, in the court's own word, incorrectly. The label that was passed along was this. I'm quoting it directly.

attempted homicide

Attempted homicide. But Keith Childress had never been charged with trying to kill anyone. The indictment he was running from was for armed robbery and kidnapping. Serious, yes, but not that. And there was more layered into the picture. The officers were told that a firearm had been found in the uncle's car, and that Childress himself might have access to a gun.

So sit with what these officers believed as they converged on that quiet neighborhood. In their own understanding, they were not chasing a man wanted for a robbery. They were chasing someone they had been told had tried to kill, someone they had been told might be armed and dangerous. That belief was built, in part, on a single wrong word. And in the few minutes that were left, that belief would decide everything.

Pick the story back up on the last morning of the year. The Marshals have located Keith Childress at his uncle's home, and when they move to take him into custody, he runs. On foot, through a residential neighborhood in Las Vegas, a man trying to disappear into the streets of a city that was closing in around him.

The Marshals could not hold him alone, so they called for help. And here is where the local police enter the story. Officers from the Las Vegas Metropolitan Police Department converged on the neighborhood, and as they came, they carried with them a picture of the man they were chasing. A picture assembled in fragments, over the radio, in the rush of a foot pursuit.

In that picture, Childress was armed and dangerous. They had been told a firearm was found in his uncle's car, that he might have access to a gun, and, mistakenly, that he was wanted for attempted homicide. None of the officers converging on that street had searched him. None of them knew what was, or was not, in his hands. They knew only what they had been told.

And then the chase narrowed to a single confrontation. Childress came toward the officers. Not running away now, but walking, quickly, in their direction, with his right hand held where they could not see it. In that hand was a black cell phone. The officers did not know that. What they saw was a man they believed to be armed, closing the distance.

Sergeant Robert Bohanon called out to him. A final warning, plain and unmistakable, the last words spoken before the shooting began. He said, and these are his words as the court recorded them, quote:

If you advance on us, you will be shot.

Childress kept moving. And at a distance of roughly forty-five feet, Sergeant Bohanon and Officer Blake Walford opened fire. This was the first volley. Childress was struck, and he fell.

He was not armed. There was no gun. The officers had fired on a man holding a telephone, in the genuine but mistaken belief that they were facing a weapon. Hold onto that fact, because the law would later take it up directly. It did not ask whether the officers were right. It asked whether, in that moment, with what they believed they knew, their belief was reasonable. And on the first volley, a court would later answer that it was.

But the shooting did not end when Childress fell. After he went down, the officers paused. The record measures that pause in seconds, somewhere between two and five. And then Bohanon and Walford fired again. A second volley.

Then a third officer, James Ledogar, released a police dog. The animal reached Childress and held him for about fifteen seconds, until the officers got to him, handcuffed him, and finally searched him. Through all of this, it is worth noting, the two U.S. Marshals who had started the chase never fired a single shot.

When the officers searched the body, this is what they found. The court's own words, the plainest sentence in the entire opinion, quote:

They found a black phone in Childress’s right front pocket but no gun.

A phone. In the pocket of a man who was now dead at the scene. The second volley and the police dog would not be judged as cleanly as the first. Those claims would survive, and they would one day go to a jury. But that reckoning was still years away. For now, there was only the street, the silence after the shots, and a black phone where everyone had been so certain a gun would be.

By the time this case reached the Ninth Circuit, one fact was no longer in dispute. Keith Childress Junior had been holding a phone. There was no gun. The officers had been wrong. And so the question the court had to answer was not whether a mistake had been made. The question was whether that mistake was reasonable.

To answer it, the court reached for a standard that governs nearly every police use of force case in America. It comes from a 1989 Supreme Court decision called Graham versus Connor, and it asks judges to do something that runs against every human instinct. It asks them to set aside what they know now.

The Ninth Circuit quoted the rule directly. And as you listen to it, notice what it deliberately leaves out.

The “reasonableness” of a particular use of force must be judged from the perspective of a reasonable officer on the scene, rather than with the 20/20 vision of hindsight.

From the perspective of a reasonable officer on the scene. Not from the perspective of the medical examiner, who would later find a phone in his pocket. Not from the calm of a courtroom, years removed from the noise and speed of that morning. The law freezes time at the instant the trigger was pulled, and it asks only what a reasonable officer would have believed in that instant.

And in that instant, the officers believed they were facing an armed and violent fugitive. They had been told, incorrectly, that Childress was wanted for attempted homicide. They had been told a firearm was found in his uncle's car. They watched him advance with his right hand hidden, after a warning that he would be shot. Through that lens, the court concluded, the first volley was not just understandable. It was reasonable as a matter of law.

That phrase, as a matter of law, carries enormous weight. It means the Ninth Circuit affirmed summary judgment for the officers on the Fourth Amendment claim arising from that first volley. No reasonable jury, the court held, could find the shooting unconstitutional. The case would not even go to trial on that question, because the answer, in the law's eyes, was already clear.

There was a second claim, brought by Childress's three children. It rested on the Fourteenth Amendment, on the right of a child to the companionship of a parent. To win it, they had to show more than a mistake. They had to show that an officer acted with a purpose to harm, unrelated to any legitimate law enforcement aim. The court found nothing in the record to meet that bar, and it affirmed summary judgment against the children too.

That might have been the end of it. The federal claims were resolved, and the officers had prevailed. But one claim survived, and it came not from the Constitution but from the ordinary law of Nevada. The family argued that the officers had been negligent. Careless. That whatever else you called the shooting, it fell below the standard of care the law demands.

The defense had an answer, and it was an old one. You cannot be careless about something you chose to do. A shooting is a deliberate act. Negligence is about accidents, about inattention, about the harm you did not mean to cause. And Nevada's courts had said as much, in language going back more than sixty years.

In a 1962 decision, the Nevada Supreme Court put the principle this way.

Willfulness and negligence are contradictory terms. If conduct is negligent, it is not willful; if it is willful, it is not negligent.

Contradictory terms. If the officers chose to fire, the argument goes, then their conduct was willful, and willful conduct cannot be negligent. The two ideas cancel each other out. It is a tidy piece of logic. And it sat directly in the path of a grieving family's last surviving claim, raising a question that Nevada's own courts had never squarely answered.

By the time the case finally reached a jury, the great constitutional questions had largely been settled. What remained was a claim grounded not in the Constitution at all, but in something older and far more ordinary. The simple law of negligence. The question of whether these officers had, in the end, simply been careless.

But the trial judge drew a careful line around what the jury would be permitted to consider. The shootings themselves, the first volley and the second, were kept entirely off the table. They had already been judged, and judged reasonable. What the jury could weigh was something far narrower.

The question put to them was confined to a single window of time. Not whether the officers were wrong to fire, but whether, in the seconds after the first volley, when Childress had already fallen, they were careless in failing to stop and reassess whether deadly force was still needed.

Think about how small that window is. A few seconds. The space between one decision and the next.

The jury came back with a finding that tried to hold two truths at the same time. They found Sergeant Bohanon and Officer Walford twenty-five percent negligent. And they found Keith Childress himself seventy-five percent responsible for what had happened to him. Then they put a number to it. One hundred and fifty thousand dollars.

For the family, that number meant something. It was an acknowledgment, however partial, that the officers had not done everything right in those final seconds. But Nevada law had one more step left to take.

Nevada follows what is called a modified comparative-fault rule. When the person harmed is found more than half responsible for their own injury, they recover nothing at all. Childress had been found seventy-five percent at fault. And so, in the court's own flat and final words:

Plaintiffs’ award was reduced to $0.

Twenty-five percent of the blame. And zero dollars in the end. The jury had spoken, and the statute had quietly erased what they said. That is the arithmetic this chapter takes its name from. Twenty-five percent of nothing. Three children who had lost their father would go home with exactly what they came in with.

And yet, the case did not end there. Because woven all the way through that negligence claim was a problem the courts had never cleanly solved.

Remember the old principle the defense had leaned on. That willfulness and negligence are opposites. That a deliberate act, a chosen shot, cannot also be a careless one. If that principle held, then a negligence claim built on an officer's intentional use of force might not be a weak claim. It might be no claim at all. A contradiction in terms.

The Ninth Circuit looked hard at this question and admitted, plainly, that it could not answer it. Not because the judges lacked the will, but because the answer did not belong to them. It was a matter of Nevada's own law, and Nevada's highest court had never squarely decided it.

So the panel did something that happens only rarely. It held the negligence claim in abeyance. It paused. And it sent the question down to the Supreme Court of Nevada, framed in a single, careful sentence:

Under Nevada law, can a plaintiff raise a theory of negligence based on an officer’s intentional use of force?

Everything the family has left now rides on the answer to that one question.

So where does that leave the case of Keith Childress, Junior. The federal claims are finished. On the Fourth Amendment, and on the Fourteenth, the officers prevailed. The law looked at the deadly mistake through the eyes of a reasonable officer in that single moment, an officer who believed he was facing a gun, and the law called what he did reasonable.

And the fact at the very center of it all has never changed. There was no gun. There was a black phone, in a pocket, on a man who was running.

What remains is that one unanswered question, sitting now with the justices of Nevada. Whether a use of force the Constitution forgives can still, under state law, be something the officers owed for. Whether a split-second mistake can be both reasonable, and somehow, still accountable.

The law is built to give clean answers. Guilty, or not. Liable, or not. Reasonable, or not. But this case lives in the space between those answers. Where a thing can be lawful and still be a tragedy. Where a jury can assign blame, and a statute can take it away.

Keith Childress, Junior, died on the last day of 2015. He left three children behind. And the question of what, if anything, the law owes them is, for now, still waiting to be answered.
