In December of 2024, a court in northern Ohio decided an appeal brought by a man named Brian Jury. He argued it himself, from prison, without a lawyer. More than a decade had passed since a jury found him guilty.

In all those years, Jury never stopped insisting that the encounter at the center of his case had been consensual. It was a claim that every court to hear it had already rejected.

Still, he came back. And the opinion that answered him opens by stating, plainly, what he was asking for. The court wrote:

Appellant, Brian Jury, pro se, appeals from two judgments of the Erie County Court of Common Pleas which, respectively, denied his postconviction motions for DNA testing, request for records, and motion for dismissal of his indictment.

Underneath the motions and the deadlines sits a harder question. After a jury has spoken, and every appeal is spent, how many more times does the law owe a man a hearing? That was what the court in Ohio had to decide.

To understand everything that follows, you have to start with a single fixed point. August of 2014. A courtroom in Erie County, Ohio. A jury had finished its work.

The verdict was not narrow, and it left little room for doubt about what the jurors had concluded. They found Brian Jury guilty on two counts of rape, two counts of abduction, and one count of felonious assault. The court imposed its sentence, and Brian Jury went to prison.

Now, a verdict like that is, in the eyes of the law, a finding of fact. The jurors heard the evidence and concluded that the State had proven its case beyond a reasonable doubt. Brian Jury has never accepted that conclusion. From the day it was read, he has insisted that he was innocent. But the conviction became the fixed star of his life, and for the next ten years, everything he did would orbit around it.

Two years later, in the spring of 2016, the Sixth District Court of Appeals reviewed that conviction and affirmed it. Under the ordinary rhythm of the law, that is usually where a story ends. The trial happens, the appeal is heard, the judgment becomes final and settled. But for Brian Jury, 2016 was not an ending. It was closer to a beginning.

To understand why he could not let it rest, you have to see the fault line running underneath the entire case. It is the same fault line you find in a great many of these prosecutions. The question was never really whether the two of them had been together. The question was how to read what happened between them.

The woman at the center of the case, whom the court never names, and whom we will not name either, testified that she was raped. The jurors believed her. Her account, weighed against the other evidence the State put forward, was enough to convict.

Brian Jury told a different story. And I want to be precise about whose story this is. This is his account, contested at trial and rejected by every court since. He claimed that he and the woman had a prior arrangement, a history, as he framed it, of sex for hire, and that whatever happened that day had been consensual. That was the theory the defense put to the jury. The jury did not accept it. No court that has examined it since has accepted it either.

And here is what makes that kind of disagreement so difficult. When the dispute is not whether two people had contact, but whether that contact was consensual, physical evidence often cannot settle the matter. DNA can tell you that two people were together. It usually cannot tell you whether one of them said no. Hold onto that idea, because it sits at the very center of what Brian Jury would spend the next decade demanding.

Through nearly all of it, Brian Jury represented himself. Pro se, the law calls it, Latin for, on one's own behalf. No attorney drafting his motions, no advocate standing between him and the machinery of the court. Just a man in a prison law library, composing his own arguments, in the particular cadence of someone who has read a great many statutes and learned to speak their language.

When his case reached the court of appeals, this is how he framed the very first argument, his first assignment of error. He wrote, and these are his exact words:

The Trial Court erred, abused its discretion, when it prematurely denied Defendant's/Appellant's “Application for DNA Testing” without giving consideration to construe his “Application …” as a motion to gain evidence to support his Crim.R. 33(B) filing (now pending appeal); or, to allow him to find the financial means to pay for it.

Read past the legal phrasing, and you can hear what he is actually asking for. He is saying, do not throw this out on a technicality. If I filed my application for DNA testing under the wrong rule, then read it as something else. Read it as my attempt to gather evidence for the new-trial motion I already have pending. And if you will not do that, then at least give me time, time to find the money to pay for the testing myself. Underneath the citations, it is a plea for one more chance to be heard.

That request, modest as it sounds, ran straight into a wall. A wall that had been rising, filing by filing, for nearly ten years. And to understand the wall, we have to walk the years that built it.

To understand what the Sixth District faced in 2024, you have to understand the ten years that came before it. Because by the time these motions reached the appellate court, Brian Jury had been writing to the courts for nearly a decade. And the story of that decade is, in its own quiet way, the story of a door closing one inch at a time.

It began in the spring of 2015. Less than a year after the verdict, Jury filed a petition for habeas corpus in Ohio's Seventh District, challenging the very indictment that had brought him to trial. The court dismissed it.

That August, he tried again. A petition for postconviction relief this time, raising questions of jurisdiction, of evidence he said was illegally obtained, of a lawyer he believed had failed him, of proof he claimed the state had hidden. The trial court denied it, and it did so on a doctrine that would come to define the rest of his fight. Res judicata.

Res judicata. A thing already adjudicated. It is one of the oldest ideas in the law, and one of the most unforgiving. The principle is simple. At some point the argument is over. Not because the answer is certainly right, but because there must, eventually, be an end.

In April of 2016, the Sixth District affirmed Jury's conviction and sentence on direct appeal. In the eyes of the law, that was the moment the verdict became settled. Two months later, Jury filed an application to reopen that appeal, arguing his lawyer on appeal had failed him too. That was denied as well.

Then came the phone records. Starting in 2018, Jury began filing motions about cell-phone data, his own and the victim's. Location records, text messages, cell-site location information. He called it newly discovered evidence. The trial court denied those motions in early 2022, treating them as successive postconviction petitions arriving years too late. The Sixth District affirmed that December, finding no evidence anything had been concealed, and finding the claims, in its word, speculative.

And still he filed. In March of 2023, a motion for leave to bring a delayed motion for a new trial. Denied again, on res judicata. By now a pattern had hardened into something almost architectural. Each new motion met the same wall, built from the same stones. The matter has been decided, or it could have been, and so it is closed.

That is the ground on which the appeal we are following was argued. Not a fresh trial. Not a clean slate. A man on the far side of a conviction, a direct appeal, a reopening, two postconviction petitions, and a string of motions, asking the courts, one more time, to look again.

And here we reach a piece of legal machinery that almost no one outside a courtroom thinks about, but which quietly decides cases like this one every day. The standard of review. When an appellate court examines what a trial judge did with a motion like Jury's, it does not ask the question you might expect. It does not ask whether it would have ruled the same way.

Instead it asks something far narrower. Did the trial court abuse its discretion? And abuse of discretion is not a loose phrase. It is a term of art, with a meaning the courts have spelled out precisely. The Sixth District set it down plainly.

An abuse of discretion implies that the trial court’s attitude is unreasonable, arbitrary, or unconscionable.

Sit with how high that bar sits. It is not enough for the trial court to have been wrong. It is not even enough that a reasonable judge might have decided differently. The ruling has to be unreasonable, arbitrary, or unconscionable. Which means a claim can be sympathetic, can tug at something in you, and still fail. Not because it was answered unfairly, but because the judge stayed inside the wide bounds the law allows.

This is the lens through which everything that follows has to be read. The appellate court was not asked whether Brian Jury was guilty. That question had been answered in 2014 and affirmed in 2016. It was asked only whether the trial court had crossed a line in refusing to reopen the case. And that is a very different question.

But there is a moment in this opinion where a careful reader has to slow down. Because the court does something here that courts do not always do. It concedes a point. It acknowledges, in the open, a weakness in the case that sent Jury to prison.

Jury's central claim had always come back to the victim's credibility. He argued her account could not be trusted, and he pointed to a record that, in his telling, was full of contradictions. The remarkable thing is that the court did not simply wave this away. Weighing his claim against the whole of the trial record, it wrote, quoting now directly from the opinion.

the other testimony presented at trial—which included sources other than the victim, who had a host of credibility issues—this is insufficient to exonerate Jury.

Read that again, because it is doing two things at once. The court grants that the victim, the central witness against him, had, in its own words, a host of credibility issues. And in the same breath it holds that this is not enough. Not enough to exonerate him. Because the case did not rest on her testimony alone. There were other sources, other evidence, put before the jury.

This is the thoughtful heart of the whole matter, so it is worth being precise about what it means and what it does not. A witness can have real credibility problems, and a conviction can still stand, when other evidence supports it. The law does not promise a perfect witness. It requires proof beyond a reasonable doubt, and that proof can come from many places at once.

And so the concession that might have felt, to Jury, like the first crack in the wall, turned out to be no opening at all. The court could agree that the victim was an imperfect witness and still hold, without contradiction, that the verdict was sound. Jury went on insisting the encounter had been consensual. Every court that reviewed his case rejected that account. And here, the concession changed nothing.

By the autumn of 2023, Brian Jury had been fighting his conviction, on paper, for the better part of a decade. And that November, he filed again. Not a single motion this time, but four of them, all at once.

The four were a coordinated push. An application for DNA testing of the evidence. A request for the records in his case file. A request for the transcript of the grand jury that had indicted him back in 2013. And a motion, under Criminal Rule 48, to throw out that indictment altogether. Together they amounted to a single, simple demand. Open the file back up, and let me prove what I have been saying all along.

The trial court answered in two days. On November 28th, it denied the application for DNA testing under the Ohio statute that governs it. The next day, it dealt with everything else, the records, the grand jury transcript, the motion to dismiss, by treating them as what it concluded they really were. Another postconviction petition. The latest in a long line. And barred, like the others, by res judicata.

Now, the DNA request deserves a moment, because at first it sounds like the most powerful tool a wrongly convicted person could ever ask for. But consider what Jury's own theory actually was. He had never claimed that some stranger was present, or that the physical evidence belonged to someone else. His claim, contested at every turn and rejected by the jury, was that the encounter had been consensual.

And that is the difficulty buried inside a consent defense. DNA can tell you whose body was in the room. It cannot tell you whether what happened there was a crime. Ohio law will only order this kind of testing when the result could actually change the outcome of a case. If Jury conceded the contact and disputed only the consent, then a test confirming his own DNA would prove nothing the jury had not already assumed.

The court had made this very point before, in an earlier ruling, and here it simply quoted itself. The line is short. It is also, in its way, the whole story of these ten years compressed into a single sentence. The court wrote:

To allow Jury to search for materials in support of claims that he is barred from raising would be pointless.

Pointless. It is a striking word to find in an appellate opinion, where the language usually runs cautious and gray. But it captures the cold arithmetic of finality. Res judicata does not say that Jury is lying. It says that the time to make this argument has passed, and that a court will not reopen the evidence file to fuel a claim a defendant no longer has the right to raise.

Res judicata. The thing already decided. It is one of the oldest ideas in the law, and one of the least comfortable. The doctrine assumes that at some point a dispute must simply end, not because we are certain the result was perfect, but because a system that never closes a case is a system that never closes any case. Every reopened file is a promise to every other defendant that their file, too, can be pried back open, forever. The law trades the possibility of one corrected error for the finality of all the rest.

And so, on December 27th, 2024, a three-judge panel of Ohio's Sixth District Court of Appeals issued its decision. It consolidated the appeals, weighed each of the trial court's rulings in turn, and affirmed all three. The DNA denial. The records denial. The dismissal of the motion to dismiss. Every door, closed in a single document.

Brian Jury was not exonerated. He was not granted a new trial. His 2014 conviction stood exactly where it had stood for ten years, and he remained, as he had been throughout, a man insisting to every court that would no longer hear him that the case against him was wrong. The law had given him its answer. He simply did not accept it.

On December twenty-seventh, two thousand twenty-four, the Sixth District consolidated Brian Jury's three appeals and answered them the same way, three times over. Affirmed. The application for DNA testing, the request for records, the motion to dismiss the indictment. The conviction a jury had returned more than ten years earlier still stood, untouched.

Brian Jury was not exonerated. No court found him innocent. He still maintains the encounter was consensual, and every court that has examined that claim has declined to reopen it.

Underneath almost every one of those denials sat a single phrase. Res judicata. The thing has been decided. It is the law's way of saying that a question, once fully and fairly litigated, is closed. Not because the answer is certainly right, but because the asking has to end somewhere.

And that is the hard bargain at the center of this case. A system that never reached finality would let every verdict be fought forever. But a system that closes its doors also closes them on the rare person who was telling the truth, and was never believed.

Brian Jury's argument, underneath all the motions, was that he had never truly been heard. The law's answer, returned to him across a decade, was quieter and harder than that. He had been heard. A jury listened, and did not believe him.

After a jury has spoken, and every appeal is spent, the law owes you process. It does not promise you a different answer.
