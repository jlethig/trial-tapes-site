That something is called a standing filing bar. And it is worth slowing down to explain what one actually is, because it sits at the center of everything that follows. A filing bar is an order, issued by a court, that restricts a person's ability to file new lawsuits. Not a single case. All of them. It is the legal system's version of a gate, lowered in advance, against a particular litigant.

Courts do not impose these orders lightly. The right to bring a grievance before a judge is close to sacred in American law. But that right is not unlimited. When a court concludes that a litigant is filing case after case that it deems to lack legal merit, consuming the time of judges and clerks and opposing parties, it has the power to say, enough. The gate comes down. And from that point forward, the litigant must satisfy specific conditions before the court will even look at anything new he files.

And here is the thing to hold onto. A filing bar is not a verdict. It says nothing about whether any particular lawsuit a person might bring is right or wrong, true or false. That checkpoint lives in two statutes. The first, Section 1915(e)(2) of Title 28, governs anyone proceeding in forma pauperis, the legal term for litigating as a pauper, without the usual fees. The second, Section 1915A, is narrower, and pointed directly at cases like this one.
