Bill Charles V Donna Mcqueen

YouTube RSS Feed

Full Transcript

In the fall of 2020, a homeowner in a planned community outside Nashville left a one-star review on Google. A few sentences. Nothing most of us would read twice.

That review would travel, over the next three and a half years, all the way to the Supreme Court of Tennessee. The man it named sued the woman who wrote it for defamation.

But the case never really turned on what she wrote. It turned on who he was. A public figure carries a far heavier burden than a private citizen, and Justice Sarah Campbell framed that burden in a single line.

This is a steep hill to climb, so determining whether the plaintiff is a public figure is a crucial inquiry in any defamation case.

And so the court had to decide which Bill Charles it was looking at. A private citizen who had been wronged. Or a public figure who had to prove Donna McQueen lied on purpose.

In 2017, Donna McQueen went looking for a place to put down roots, and she found it in a master-planned community called Durham Farms, just outside Hendersonville, Tennessee. Like a lot of buyers, she didn't only fall for the house. She fell for the plan.

And that plan made specific promises. It limited how many lots could be built. It called for estate-sized lots. And it drew one line in particular that would matter enormously later: there would be no units that were, in the language of the plan, rental only. McQueen would say she relied on those restrictions when she signed.

Durham Farms sold itself as a place built around community and connectedness. That was the pitch. And when you buy into a pitch like that, you are trusting that the people behind it will keep the place looking like the brochure. Hold onto that idea, because it is the thing that breaks.

Now move forward to September of 2020. Freehold Communities, the developer behind Durham Farms, announced that it wanted to amend the master plan. The change would add more than a hundred and sixty new units to the community. And those units would be rental only. The exact thing the original plan had ruled out.

The residents revolted, and they organized fast. They started a Facebook group with a name that left no doubt about the mission. They called it, roughly, Help Stop Developer's New Rental Only Section in Durham Farms. And the group urged its members to do something direct. Email the developer, Bill Charles, themselves.

So who was Bill Charles? Freehold had hired him, a local real estate professional, to be its presence on the ground. He led the meetings with residents. He stood up and explained the company's decisions. To the people of Durham Farms, he was not a faceless corporation buried in a filing. He was the man at the front of the room. And now he was the man they were being told to write to.

The pressure built through that September. Local newspapers picked up the story. Residents gathered signatures, eventually close to six hundred and eighty of them, on a petition opposing the plan. And some of them carried their anger online, posting one-star reviews of Freehold Communities on Google.

Most of those reviews were aimed at the company. They were furious at Freehold, at the idea of a bait and switch, at watching the community they had bought into change underneath them. The reviews were a release valve for a neighborhood that felt it had been promised one thing and handed another.

But one review was different from all the others. It was the only one that named a single human being, and it put Bill Charles at the center of the accusation. Donna McQueen wrote it. And in full, it read:

Buyer Beware! Freehold Communities is nothing but bait and switch. I have lived here three years with multiple changes to our development which is supposed to be all about community and connectedness. Bill Charles, especially, uses misleading tactics to lure in home buyers only to deceive them. A rental section within our community is not what any of us signed up for. Zero star rating for Freehold.

Notice the sentence where it turns personal. Bill Charles, especially, uses misleading tactics to lure in home buyers only to deceive them. That is a serious charge to level at a named man. But here is the thing you have to carry with you for the rest of this story. That sentence is Donna McQueen's accusation. It is her account of what she believed happened. No court has ever ruled that it was true.

In fact, years later, the Tennessee Supreme Court would go out of its way to say it was not deciding whether her words were true or false. Because the lawsuit Bill Charles was about to file would not turn on the truth of the review at all. It would turn on a single question about who, in the eyes of the law, Bill Charles actually was. And about four months after McQueen hit post, he sued her.

For about four months, that review just sat there. Public, searchable, attached to the name of a company and the name of a man. And then, in January of 2021, Bill Charles went to court.

He filed suit in the Chancery Court for Williamson County, Tennessee, and he brought two claims. The first was defamation. The second was its quieter cousin, a claim called false light, the idea that McQueen had cast him before the public in a way that was both false and unflattering. He wanted the review answered, and he wanted a judge to do the answering.

And that is where the case found its first real fault line. Of all the residents who were furious that fall, of all the negative reviews Freehold collected, McQueen's was the only one that named a person. So the question, the one this whole dispute would eventually turn on, was almost childishly simple. Why him?

McQueen's answer was that, to her, Bill Charles was Freehold. He was the one who stood at the front of the residents' meetings. He was the one who explained the company's decisions to the people who lived with them. When the neighborhood pictured the developer, the face it saw was his.

In the way McQueen saw it, Charles was, quote,

the public face of Freehold

But there was more behind her view than the meetings. McQueen had spoken with a local official, a Hendersonville alderman named Eddie Roberson. Roberson told her that Charles had attended a city meeting about the rental proposal, and that the city itself was against it. And from that one conversation, McQueen drew a conclusion. She came away believing that Charles was not merely present for the rental plan. She believed he was behind it.

Now, that belief is the thing to hold carefully. It was McQueen's impression, an inference she pulled from a single conversation. It was never something a court found to be true. And Charles, when his turn came, told a very different story.

He did not deny that he had been involved. He acknowledged that he facilitated the meetings, that he helped bring Freehold and the city's officials into the same room. A year earlier, in 2019, he had stood in front of Durham Farms residents to explain and defend the company's choice to shrink the minimum lot sizes in one section. By his own account, he was the man in the room.

But there was a line he drew, and he drew it sharply. He said that at the meetings he facilitated, he did not, quote,

participate in any substantive discussion of the rental unit issue

And he went further than that. Charles claimed the very opposite of what McQueen believed. He said that he had privately opposed the rental units himself. In his telling, he was a facilitator who kept his hands off the substance, not an advocate pushing an unwanted plan onto the community that trusted him.

So there they were, two accounts laid against each other. McQueen saw the public face of a developer who, she believed, was steering the very change the neighborhood feared. Charles saw a man who opened a door and then stepped back from whatever happened on the other side of it. Two people, looking at the same meetings, describing two entirely different men.

And here is the strange part. The case that grew out of all this would not be settled by deciding which of them was right. It would be settled by a question that sounds almost beside the point. Not whether Charles backed the rental units, but whether Bill Charles, simply by stepping into that room, had become something the law treats very differently. A public figure.

To understand how a Google review climbed all the way to a state Supreme Court, you have to understand a law Tennessee passed in 2019. It's called the Public Participation Act, and it belongs to a family of statutes known by an unlovely acronym. SLAPP. A strategic lawsuit against public participation.

The idea behind a SLAPP is simple, and a little cynical. You don't sue someone because you expect to win. You sue because the lawsuit itself is the punishment. The legal fees, the depositions, the years of uncertainty. The quiet message to the next critic is this. Speak up about me, and you could be next.

Anti-SLAPP laws exist to short-circuit that. Tennessee's version lets a defendant who is sued over protected speech ask the court to dismiss the case early, before the expense of a full trial. Donna McQueen invoked it. And that one decision reshaped the entire fight.

Here's the crucial part. Once McQueen showed her review concerned a matter of public interest, the burden shifted to Bill Charles. To keep his case alive, he had to make a preliminary showing, what the law calls a prima facie case, that he could actually prove the elements of his claim. Not at some distant trial. Right then, at the threshold.

And that threshold is where everything turned on a single question of identity. In American defamation law, not all plaintiffs are treated the same. A private citizen who is defamed generally has to show the speaker was careless with the truth. A public figure has to show something far harder.

That something harder has a name. Actual malice. And it does not mean spite or ill will, the way the word malice sounds. It means the speaker either knew what she said was false, or spoke with reckless disregard for whether it was true at all. Proving what someone knew, inside their own head, at the moment they hit publish.

So the case no longer hinged on whether McQueen's review was fair, or even whether it was accurate. It hinged on a classification. If Bill Charles was an ordinary private citizen, he had a path. If he was a public figure, he faced that steep climb, and he had to prove McQueen had lied on purpose, or something close to it.

To answer that, the courts looked at what Charles had actually done in the community. Back in 2019, he had stood before residents to explain and defend Freehold's decision to shrink the minimum lot sizes in one section of the development. He was, in those moments, the man at the front of the room.

Then came the private meetings with Hendersonville's city officials over the rental proposal. Charles acknowledges he facilitated them, but he insists he stayed out of the substance of the rental fight. McQueen saw it differently. That dispute, over how involved he really was, sits at the very heart of this case.

And there was one more fact, a quieter one. At the homeowners' association meeting in October of 2020, just weeks after the review appeared, Charles declined to comment on the rental issue at all. A small moment, but the kind of detail the courts would weigh when deciding who he really was in this story.

Two courts looked at those same facts. They reached opposite conclusions.

The trial court came first. Chancellor Michael Binkley, sitting in Williamson County, granted McQueen's petition and dismissed both of Charles's claims. His reasoning. Charles was a limited-purpose public figure, and he had failed to prove actual malice. The Chancellor also ordered Charles to pay McQueen's attorney's fees.

For Charles, that was a defeat on every front. So he appealed. And in September of 2022, the Tennessee Court of Appeals handed him something that looked like a lifeline.

The appeals court disagreed with the Chancellor on the central point. Bill Charles, it held, was not a public figure. He was a private citizen. And that mattered enormously, because it meant his defamation claim no longer required proof of actual malice. It could proceed under the lower, negligence standard. His case was alive again.

But the lifeline came with a catch. The same court ruled that his second claim, the one for false light, still failed, because that claim did require actual malice, and Charles could not show it. So the score, after two courts, stood split. One had called him a public figure. The other had called him a private one. The same man, the same review, two opposite answers.

Which meant the question was no longer whether Bill Charles had been wronged. It was who, in the eyes of the law, Bill Charles actually was. And there was only one court left in Tennessee with the authority to settle it.

After the Court of Appeals split the difference, the case climbed one last step. In March of 2023, the Tennessee Supreme Court agreed to take it up, heard oral argument that October, and then went quiet for the better part of a year. The question waiting for an answer was the same one that had divided every court below. Was Bill Charles a private citizen, or a public figure?

On July 3rd, 2024, Justice Sarah Campbell delivered the opinion of the court. Her very first holding was the one that would decide everything else. She wrote, and I am quoting the court directly here:

We hold that Charles is a limited-purpose public figure given the voluntary and prominent role he played in a controversy concerning changes to the Durham Farms development plan.

A limited-purpose public figure. Not a celebrity, not a politician, but someone who steps voluntarily into a particular public controversy and, for that controversy alone, takes on the higher burden. The court pointed to the meetings Charles hosted, to his role as the development's public-facing representative, and to the choices he made to speak, and at times not to speak, on the very dispute at issue. On that point, the Supreme Court reversed the Court of Appeals.

The court paused on something larger than this one subdivision. It noted that the very idea of a public figure has grown slippery in the age of the internet, citing a recent opinion from the United States Supreme Court. There, a justice had warned that the old categories were straining, that:

private citizens can become ‘public figures’ on social media overnight

Think about what that means. The same forces that let a homeowner broadcast a one-star review to the world can also pull the person she is reviewing across the line into public-figure territory, and with it, the steep burden of proving a lie was told on purpose.

And that burden is where Bill Charles's case finally came apart. Because he was a public figure, he could not win simply by showing McQueen got something wrong. He had to make a threshold showing of what the law calls actual malice. That she knew her review was false, or that she published it with reckless disregard for whether it was true. The court held he had not made that showing.

With that, both of his claims fell. The defamation claim and the false-light claim alike. Charles raised a further argument about whether he should have to pay McQueen's appellate fees, and the court rejected it. It sent the case back to the trial court, including for the question of McQueen's attorney's fees.

But here is the line the court was careful to draw. It did not rule that McQueen's review was true. It did not rule that Charles deceived anyone. The justices expressly declined to decide whether her statements were accurate or defamatory at all. They held only this. That Charles, as a public figure, had not cleared the actual-malice bar the law set in front of him.

So who won? On the merits, Donna McQueen. Both of Bill Charles's claims failed, and the case returned to the lower court so it could sort out the fees he would owe her. A one-star Google review, posted in the heat of a neighborhood fight, had traveled three and a half years and outlasted a defamation suit from the man it named.

And yet the review was never declared true. That is the quiet, important thing to carry out of this case. McQueen did not prevail because a court found that Charles deceived home buyers. She prevailed because the law asked Charles to prove she lied knowingly, and he could not.

What is left is a line. The one between a private citizen and a public figure. And a court's reminder that the line is thinner than it used to be. Step far enough into a public fight, lead the meetings, become the face of the thing, and the law may decide you have to take the criticism the way public figures do.

Bill Charles wanted to be treated as a private man who had been wronged. The Tennessee Supreme Court decided he had become something else, at least for this one controversy, in this one community, on this one question. In the social-media age, the court suggested, more of us may find ourselves on that same uncertain ground than we ever expected.
