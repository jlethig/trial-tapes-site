In the spring of 2026, a welder named Leonel David Torres Basanta sat in federal detention. He had thirteen years at his trade, a wife, and a young child who was an American citizen. He had not been charged with any crime.

He was being held because the government had moved to end the protected status that allowed people of his nationality to remain. When a federal judge in Vermont reviewed the case, he set down his findings plainly. He wrote, quote.

Mr. Basanta has no criminal record. There is no reason to believe that he presents a threat of any kind to the community. The evidence is to the contrary.

And so the case came down to one question. A man the court found posed no threat to anyone was being held by the United States, and whether he walked free turned on a single federal judge.

To understand why a welder with no criminal record ended up behind the walls of a federal detention center, you have to leave the courtroom for a moment, and go back to a decision made in Washington, more than a year before anyone in Vermont had heard his name. In February of 2025, the Administration published a formal action in the Federal Register. Its stated purpose was to bring an end to a program called Temporary Protected Status, for nationals of Venezuela.

Temporary Protected Status, or TPS, is a kind of legal shelter. When a country becomes too dangerous to return to, because of war, or disaster, or upheaval, the United States can designate its nationals as protected. They are allowed to stay. They are allowed to work. They are not, for the duration, subject to removal. For many Venezuelans living in America, that status was the thread their whole lives hung from.

And so the move to end it did not go unanswered. A federal district court in California blocked the termination, holding that the program could not simply be switched off. But the matter did not stop there. It climbed, as these things do, toward the Supreme Court, and the Supreme Court stayed the lower court's orders. I want to be precise about what that means, because it matters. A stay is not a final answer. It is a pause. The question of whether this protected status could lawfully be terminated was left open. Unresolved. Suspended somewhere above the heads of everyone it touched.

Think about that for a moment. Hundreds of thousands of people, whose right to remain in the country rested entirely on the outcome of a legal fight they had no part in, and could not control. The machinery of that fight was already in motion. And it was moving toward them.

One of the people standing in its path was a man named Leonel David Torres Basanta. He was not a litigant in the great constitutional argument. He was not a public figure. He was a tradesman. He had a family. A wife, whose own request for asylum was still pending, still waiting on a decision. And a young child, a citizen of the United States by birth. Habeas corpus is one of the oldest protections in the Anglo-American legal tradition. The Latin means, roughly, that you shall have the body. It is the mechanism by which a person held by the state can force the government into open court to justify the detention. It does not ask whether the prisoner is a good man or a bad one. It asks something narrower, and older, and harder to wave away. By what authority are you holding him?

So the caption of the case read the way these cases tend to read. The individual on one side. The machinery of the federal government on the other. And the first respondent named, the order put it this way.

DONALD J. TRUMP, in his official capacity as President of the United States, et al, Respondents.

In a habeas case, you name the officials responsible for the chain of custody that keeps you confined, and you follow that chain as far up as it goes.
