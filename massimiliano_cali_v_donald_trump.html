Massimiliano Cali V Donald Trump

In May of 2026, a single federal trial judge in Washington did something a trial judge almost never does. He took a sanction the President had imposed on a United Nations investigator, and he froze it. Not just for the family who had sued. He blocked it, in his own words, in full.

Nine days later, a federal appeals court pulled that order back. The government could begin enforcing its sanction again, the panel said, with a single exception. It could not touch one family. The investigator's husband, an Italian citizen, and her American-born daughter.

Because that husband and that daughter were the reason any of this reached a courtroom. The investigator herself never sued. Her family did.

And so the court was left with two questions it had not yet decided. Whether a foreign citizen, speaking from outside the United States, can claim the protection of the American Constitution at all. And whether one judge, reaching to shield one family, had frozen far too much.

To understand how a frozen bank account collided with the power of the presidency, you have to start with a woman who never set foot in this lawsuit. Her name is Francesca Albanese. She is not a plaintiff, not a defendant, not a witness. She is the person the sanctions were aimed at.

Albanese is an Italian lawyer. In 2022, the United Nations Human Rights Council appointed her to a position with a long and bureaucratic title: Special Rapporteur on the situation of human rights in the Palestinian territory. A Special Rapporteur is an independent investigator. The job is to look, to document, and to report back to the United Nations. It comes with a platform, and very little else.

And report she did. In October of 2024, she submitted a document to the U.N. General Assembly titled "Genocide as Colonial Erasure." The following July, she released another, this one called "From Economy of Occupation to Economy of Genocide." That second report did something that would matter enormously to this case. It named American companies. Lockheed Martin. Caterpillar. Chevron.

Now, what those reports actually were depends entirely on who you ask. The United States government would come to describe her work as a threat to American foreign policy, a campaign of, in their word, lawfare, against U.S. companies and allied officials. The family who eventually sued would describe the very same work as protected advocacy. Speech. The kind of thing a human rights investigator is supposed to do.

Hold onto that disagreement, because the entire case sits on top of it. One side sees a national security threat. The other sees a person exercising the freedom to speak. The court was being asked, in effect, to decide which lens the Constitution requires.

The machinery that would freeze Albanese's life began turning in February of 2025. That month, President Trump signed Executive Order 14203. The order declared a national emergency, this one centered on the International Criminal Court, the tribunal in The Hague that investigates war crimes.

The order did something specific. It authorized sanctions, under a federal law called the International Emergency Economic Powers Act, against people who helped the I.C.C. pursue what the order called protected persons. In plain terms, the President was reaching for one of the most powerful financial tools the executive branch has, and pointing it at those who assisted the court's investigations.

On July 9th, 2025, Secretary of State Marco Rubio used that tool. He designated Francesca Albanese. The State Department's stated reason, and I want to be careful to frame this as the government's position rather than settled fact, was that she had directly engaged with the I.C.C. in its efforts against the United States and Israel.

The same day, the Treasury Department's Office of Foreign Assets Control added her name to a list. The list has a clinical name: the Specially Designated Nationals list, the S.D.N. list. But landing on it is anything but clinical. It freezes any property within U.S. jurisdiction. It bars, with few exceptions, anyone subject to U.S. law from doing business with you.

Think about what that means for an ordinary financial life. A bank account. A mortgage. A wire transfer to a family member. All of it, potentially, walled off. The government did issue what is called a wind-down license, a narrow permission to unwind certain existing dealings. But the core of the designation did exactly what it was designed to do. It froze her out of the American financial system.

And here is the turn that makes this case strange. When the lawsuit finally came, it was not filed by Francesca Albanese. She is not a plaintiff. The people who walked into federal court were her family.

The first is her husband, Massimiliano Cali. He is an Italian citizen, an economist who once worked for the World Bank. Years earlier, that job had brought the family to Washington. They had opened an American bank account. They had bought a condominium in the District of Columbia. Then the work moved them on, to Indonesia, then to Tunisia, and they had not lived in the United States since 2015.

The second plaintiff is their daughter. She was born in the United States in 2013, which makes her an American citizen. She is a minor, and the court records refer to her only by her initials. She appears in this case as something close to a legal abstraction, an American child whose citizenship her parents argued the sanctions had quietly trapped.

The injuries they described were concrete, at least as the district court would later credit them. The proceeds from that D.C. condominium, they said, would have to flow into a blocked account. Their U.S.-citizen daughter, they argued, was effectively unable to travel, because the parents she would travel with were now barred from U.S. transactions. There were even concerns that the child could face liability simply for ordinary dealings with her own mother.

The government saw these harms very differently. It called them speculative and unsubstantiated. It pointed out that the plaintiffs had filed no sworn declarations, and that O.F.A.C. had issued licenses covering the very things they worried about, the condominium, the care of the child. Whether these injuries were real and serious or thin and hypothetical was, itself, one of the fights.

The case landed before Judge Richard Leon, a veteran of the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia. The family asked him for a preliminary injunction, an order to halt the sanction now, before the slow machinery of a full trial could grind toward a final answer.

On May 13th, 2026, he gave them one. Judge Leon found that the family had standing, the legal right to bring the case. And he found that their First Amendment claim was likely to succeed on the merits. In the language of these motions, likely to succeed is a powerful phrase. It means the judge looked at the constitutional argument and concluded it would probably win.

But it was the scope of his order that would become the heart of the appeal. Judge Leon did not simply protect this family. He enjoined Albanese's designation, in his words, in full. Not just as to Cali. Not just as to the daughter. The entire sanction, blocked, for everyone, everywhere.

It is worth pausing on what this was, and what it was not. This was a preliminary ruling, an early and provisional decision, not a final judgment about anyone's rights. And it was about to be appealed, hard and fast.

The government's response was an emergency. Its lawyers went straight to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit and asked it to put Judge Leon's order on hold. The motion they filed opened not with dry procedure, but with a single sentence built to frame the entire dispute.

Their motion began with a sentence that reads less like a legal filing and more like an opening argument. I am quoting its first line directly.

The district court issued a sweeping injunction that categorically blocks the sanctions that the President and Secretary of State imposed on Francesca Albanese, a United Nations “Special Rapporteur” who has threatened U.S. foreign policy by pushing to investigate and prosecute U.S. companies and allied officials.

Read that again, because every word is doing work. The injunction is sweeping, and categorical. The sanctions belong to the President and the Secretary of State, the political branches. And Albanese is cast as someone who threatened American foreign policy. That is the government's story, told the government's way. The family had not yet filed their answer to it. But the questions underneath that one sentence, whether speech can be sanctioned, and whether one judge can block a presidential order for the whole world, were now headed to a higher court.

To understand what happens next, you have to understand that the appeal turned on a question that comes before the First Amendment. Before the sanctions. Before any of it. Not whether Francesca Albanese's speech was protected. But whether she had any First Amendment rights to assert in the first place.

Remember who Albanese is. An Italian citizen. A United Nations investigator. A woman whose permanent home, by 2026, was abroad, in Tunisia. She had lived in the United States once, years earlier. But she did not live there now. And the government's position was that this single fact changed everything.

The district court had found that the plaintiffs were likely to win on the merits, that the sanctions looked like punishment for protected speech. But the government's first move on appeal was not to argue about the speech at all. It was to argue that the court had no business asking the question, because a foreign citizen standing on foreign soil simply does not carry the Constitution with her.

And you can see why the government would want to start there. If you can win on the threshold, you never have to defend the sanctions themselves. You never have to explain why a report criticizing American companies counts as a national security threat. You simply say the court reached a question it had no power to reach.

The government built that claim on a single proposition, one it lifted from a Supreme Court decision and quoted word for word. The line is short, and it is absolute.

[I]t is long settled as a matter of American constitutional law that foreign citizens outside U.S. territory do not possess rights under the U.S. Constitution.

Do not possess rights. Not limited rights. Not conditional rights. None. If that is true, then the entire case collapses at the threshold, because you cannot violate a right that was never there to begin with. That is the government's argument, and the Supreme Court language behind it is real.

But here is the wrinkle the government had to work around. Albanese was not a stranger to the United States. She had once lived there. She and her husband still owned a condominium in Washington. Their daughter was born on American soil, a United States citizen. Whether those ties are enough to pull a foreign national back under the Constitution's protection is exactly the question no court in this case has actually answered yet.

And to show the court just how dangerous it thought the trial judge's logic was, the government reached back to 1950, to a case about enemy fighters held overseas after the Second World War. If foreign nationals abroad could claim constitutional rights, the government warned, there would be no logical place to stop. It quoted the older decision directly.

enemy elements … could require the American Judiciary to assure them freedoms of speech, press, and assembly as in the First Amendment.

That is the slippery slope, drawn in the government's own ink. Extend the First Amendment to a U.N. investigator in Tunisia today, and tomorrow you may be extending it to a wartime adversary. It is an argument by precedent, by analogy, by warning. It is not, it should be said, anything the appeals court had actually adopted. It was the government telling the court where it believed the road would lead.

Now, think about that for a moment, because there is a thread running underneath all of this that the government's framing tends to pull tight and hide. This lawsuit was never filed by Francesca Albanese. Her name is on the sanctions. It is not on the complaint.

The people who sued were her husband, Massimiliano Cali, and their daughter, a child who is an American citizen. And that distinction matters, because the district court did not rest its order on Albanese's rights alone. It credited injuries the family said were their own. Money from the sale of a Washington condominium, frozen. A child, the court found, effectively unable to travel, because the parents who would travel with her were barred.

The government's answer was that these harms were speculative and unsubstantiated, that the family had filed no sworn declarations, and that the Treasury had already issued licenses covering the very transactions they complained about. Which set of facts is true is part of what remains unresolved. But it reframes the threshold question in a way the government would rather you not dwell on. Because if the people actually in court are an American citizen and her father, then the tidy rule, no rights abroad, does not so cleanly dispose of the case.

And that gap, between the foreign official the government wanted to talk about and the American child the plaintiffs put at the center, is where the whole fight was about to turn.

There were really two fights inside this appeal. The first was the one we just heard, the question of whether Francesca Albanese had any constitutional rights to invoke from abroad. But the government had a second argument, and in some ways it was the more practical one. Even if her family could sue, even if their frozen condominium and their daughter's tangled travel gave them a foot in the courthouse door, why had a single trial judge blocked the entire sanction, for the whole world?

Remember what Judge Leon actually did on the thirteenth of May. He didn't simply carve out a protection for Massimiliano Cali and his daughter. He enjoined Albanese's designation, in his words, in full. The sanction the President and the Secretary of State had imposed, gone, at least for now, top to bottom. And when the government went looking for his reasoning on that point, the explanation they found sat in a single footnote. Footnote five.

[i]t is unclear how defendants could limit the impact of Albanese’s designation as to plaintiffs while otherwise enforcing the designation.

That was it. One sentence, deciding that the relief could not be surgical, that you could not shield the family without lifting the whole designation. The government would build a substantial part of its appeal on tearing that single sentence apart. Whether the trial judge was right that the remedy could not be narrowed is one of the questions the appeals court has not yet answered.

Because here was the government's framing. The family's actual stake in all of this, they argued, was small. A set of proceeds from a Washington condominium. A child's ability to travel with her parents. A handful of specific transactions, most of which, the government pointed out, OFAC had already licensed. And from that narrow dispute, a nationwide order had grown. The motion put it in a single image.

The court thus let the tail wag the dog, using a highly narrow challenge predicated on a few potential transactions to upend the entire designation.

The tail wagging the dog. It's the kind of phrase that survives a hundred briefs because it does real work. The government's point was that a remedy should fit the injury, that a few blocked transactions did not justify dismantling a national-security sanction in its entirety. The plaintiffs, of course, saw it differently, and the trial judge had sided with them. Which side is correct, the breadth of the remedy, is precisely what the higher court now had to weigh.

And it had to weigh it fast. The government had first asked Judge Leon himself to pause his own order while they appealed. On the twentieth of May, he refused. So they went over his head, to the United States Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit, with an emergency motion, asking for an immediate stay. The clock was now measured in hours.

On the twenty-second of May, the panel answered. Three judges, Henderson, Katsas, and Childs, acting together, per curiam, with no single name attached. And what they did was neither what the government wanted nor what the family wanted. They split the difference, almost down the middle. Their order, when it came, read in part:

appellants may implement and enforce the designation of Francesca Albanese as a designated foreign national under Section 1(a)(ii)(A) of Executive Order 14203, except that appellants may not take any action that violates the licenses regarding Massimiliano Cali and L.C. issued by the Office of Foreign Assets Control.

Read it slowly and you can see the compromise. The sanction on Albanese could go back into force. The government could enforce it again, broadly, against nearly everyone it touched. With one exception. The specific licenses that OFAC had issued to protect Cali and his daughter would stay in place. The tail and the dog, finally separated. The dog let loose, the tail kept safe.

But the panel was careful about one thing, and it said so plainly. This was not a verdict. It was a holding action, a way to keep things steady while the court studied the real questions. The order, the panel wrote, in its own words,

should not be construed in any way as a ruling on the merits.

In other words, nobody had won. Not yet. The government had bought back most of its sanction for the moment. The family had kept the narrow shield that mattered to them. And the two enormous questions underneath, whether a foreign citizen abroad can reach for the First Amendment, and whether one judge's order had swept too far, were left exactly where they started. Open. Waiting for a fuller hearing, on a clock the court had just started.

As of late May 2026, here is where the fight stands. The partial stay holds. The government may again enforce its sanctions against Francesca Albanese, the United Nations investigator whose reports it has called a threat to American foreign policy, everywhere except one narrow place. That place is the set of Treasury licenses shielding a single family.

But the appeals court was careful to say what its order did not mean. The three judges, Henderson, Katsas, and Childs, wrote that their decision, and I am quoting them directly,

should not be construed in any way as a ruling on the merits.

So two questions remain open underneath this case. The first is constitutional. Can a foreign citizen, speaking from outside the United States, claim the First Amendment at all? The government says that was settled generations ago, and that the answer is no. The trial judge was not so sure.

The second is about the reach of one judge. Did the district court have the power to freeze a presidential sanction for the whole world, when the only people before it were Massimiliano Cali and his American daughter, caught in the gears of an order aimed at someone else? The government calls that the tail wagging the dog.

What comes next is a clock. The family has until the afternoon of May 28 to answer the government's motion, and the government replies by June 2. Then the same three judges decide whether the sanctions stay in force, or freeze again. Whose assets stay frozen, and whose words the Constitution reaches, this court has not yet said.
