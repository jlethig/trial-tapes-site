For more than two centuries, no former president of the United States had ever sat as a criminal defendant. Not for anything done while holding the office. That line had never once been crossed, until this case.

When the question reached the Supreme Court, the Chief Justice opened the opinion of the Court with the plain fact of it. He wrote, quoting directly:

This case is the first criminal prosecution in our Nation’s history of a former President for actions taken during his Presidency.

But the Court had not gathered to weigh his guilt. It had gathered to answer something deeper. Whether the office of the presidency itself placed a president's official acts beyond a prosecutor's reach.

And so the Court was caught between two dangers. Try a former president for the acts of his office, and little stops his rivals from prosecuting those who follow. Shield him completely, and the promise that no one is above the law begins to mean nothing.

To understand what the Supreme Court was asked to decide, you have to start with the man at the center of it, and the office he once held. From January of 2017 until January of 2021, Donald J. Trump served as President of the United States. Four years in the most powerful office the Constitution creates.

Then, in November of 2020, he ran for a second term, and he lost. The election went to Joseph R. Biden, Jr. That much is settled history. What happened in the weeks that followed is where the story turns, and where the law gets difficult.

On August 1st, 2023, a federal grand jury returned an indictment. Four counts, all of them tied to what prosecutors described as a coordinated effort to overturn the result of the 2020 election. The defendant named in that indictment was a former President of the United States.

Think about that for a moment. In nearly two and a half centuries, it had never happened. No former president had ever stood criminally accused for things he was alleged to have done while sitting in the Oval Office. There was no playbook. No precedent. The country was, in a very literal sense, in uncharted constitutional territory.

Now, before we go any further, a word about how I am going to tell this part. Everything I am about to describe comes from the indictment. These are allegations. They were never tested at a trial, never weighed by a jury, never proven. Mr. Trump is presumed innocent of every one of them. So when I say the indictment alleges, I mean exactly that, and nothing more.

The charges centered on a single date. January 6th, 2021. On that day, Congress had gathered in a joint session to do something the Constitution treats as largely ceremonial. To count the electoral votes, and to certify the winner of the presidential election.

According to the indictment, on the morning of that session, Trump addressed a crowd of his supporters. The indictment alleges that he repeated claims of election fraud that were false, and that he told the assembled crowd the Vice President had the power to alter the outcome of the certification.

The indictment further alleges that he then directed those supporters toward the Capitol. And it alleges, in its own words, that a large and angry crowd violently attacked the Capitol and halted the proceeding.

I want to be precise here, because the precision matters. None of that was ever adjudicated. A grand jury's indictment is an accusation, not a finding of guilt. Whether any of it could be proven, and whether a former president could even be tried for it, was the very thing now headed for the courts.

Trump's lawyers had a response, and it was sweeping. They argued that a former president cannot be prosecuted at all for his official acts. That immunity, they said, was absolute. The first court to hear that argument did not accept it.

On December 1st, 2023, the District Court denied Trump's motion to dismiss. It held that former presidents enjoy no absolute immunity from criminal prosecution. The office, in that court's view, did not place a former president beyond the reach of the criminal law.

Trump appealed, and the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals affirmed. It issued its ruling per curiam, a decision in the name of the court as a whole rather than any single judge, and it reached the same conclusion. There was no structural immunity shielding him from these charges.

And so the case climbed, as the hardest cases do, all the way to the Supreme Court of the United States. But notice what had happened along the way. What began as a criminal docket, four counts against one defendant, had quietly become something much larger. The justices were no longer being asked only about Donald Trump.

They were being asked a question about the presidency itself. Can the criminal law reach inside that office and hold the person who held it to account? Or does the Constitution build a wall around a president's official acts that no prosecutor may cross? That was the question now waiting on the Court's desk.

On July 1, 2024, the Chief Justice of the United States, John Roberts, delivered the opinion of the Court. And the question in front of him was not really about Donald Trump at all. Not about whether he was a good president or a bad one, guilty or innocent. It was about the office he had once held.

Think about the structure the Constitution sets up. A single person, elected by the whole nation, vested with what Article Two calls the executive power. The Court's concern was not the man in the chair. It was the chair itself, and what would happen to it if every decision a president made could later be turned into a criminal charge by the people who replaced him.

Because here is the worry the majority kept circling back to. If a former president can be prosecuted for the way he wielded power, then every future president governs with one eye on the next administration's prosecutors. Bold action becomes legal risk. And the presidency, the Court reasoned, was never meant to be that fragile.

And yet. The same shield that lets a president govern without flinching is a shield that could, in other hands, cover a great deal of wrongdoing. The Court understood this perfectly well. Much of the opinion is an effort to hold those two truths in the same hand at the same time.

So the Court began to build a wall. Not around the whole presidency, but around its center. There are certain powers the Constitution hands to the president and to no one else. The power to pardon. The command of the armed forces. The authority to remove executive officers, to recognize foreign governments, to direct the work of the Justice Department. Powers that Congress cannot regulate and courts cannot second-guess.

For that innermost circle of power, the Court's conclusion was not cautious, and not hedged. It was absolute. And Roberts stated it in a single sentence. He wrote, quote:

We thus conclude that the President is absolutely immune from criminal prosecution for conduct within his exclusive sphere of constitutional authority.

Absolutely immune. Not presumed innocent. Not entitled to a defense. Immune. For those core powers, a former president cannot be charged at all. Not for his motives, not for what a prosecutor believes he was really trying to accomplish. The motive never enters the room.

The logic is that these powers belong to the president alone. If a court could put him on trial for how he used them, then the court, or the prosecutor bringing the case, would in effect be governing in his place. And that, the majority held, is something the Constitution does not permit.

But a president does a great many things that are not core constitutional powers. He gives speeches. He meets with advisers. He talks with state officials, with members of Congress, with the public. Most of what fills a president's day lives in this broader, blurrier territory. And so the Court drew not one line, but a map with three regions.

The first region we have already crossed. Core constitutional powers, absolute immunity. The second region is everything else a president does in his official capacity, the ordinary lawful business of the office. For those official acts, the Court held that a president is entitled to at least presumptive immunity.

Presumptive is a careful word. It means immunity is the starting point, the default setting, but that in theory it can be overcome. To prosecute an official act, the government would have to show that bringing the charge poses no danger to the authority and functioning of the executive branch. A very high bar, set there on purpose. But not an impossible one.

And then there is the third region. The things a president does that are not presidential at all. Acts he takes as a candidate for re-election, or as the leader of his party, or as a private citizen who happens to live in the White House. Here the Court drew its outer boundary, and on this point it left no room for doubt.

On the far side of that boundary, where the president is no longer acting as president, the rule was as plain as a rule can be. Roberts wrote, quote:

As for a President's unofficial acts, there is no immunity.

So now there is a map. Absolute immunity at the core. Presumptive immunity for the official acts that surround it. And no immunity at all for the unofficial ones. On paper, it is almost elegant. Three regions, three rules, clean lines drawn across the entire range of what a president can do.

But a map is only as good as your ability to read it on the ground. Because everything now turned on one question, a question the Court had just made the most important in the entire case. When a president acts, how do you know which region he is standing in? Where exactly does an official act end, and an unofficial one begin? That was the line the Court would have to draw next, and it would have to draw it against the specific things this president was alleged to have done.

Now the Court had its framework. Three tiers, a sorting machine for presidential conduct. But a framework decides nothing on its own. The hard work of this case was never the abstraction. It was the application. Taking each thing the indictment accused Donald Trump of doing, and asking which tier it belonged in.

Start with the Justice Department. The indictment alleged that after the election, Trump met with the Acting Attorney General and senior officials at the Department of Justice and the White House, and pressed them to pursue claims of election fraud and to send a letter to certain states. And it alleged that when the Acting Attorney General resisted, Trump repeatedly threatened to replace him.

Now, these were allegations, and only allegations. Trump was never tried on them. He is presumed innocent of every one. But for the question in front of the Court, none of that mattered yet. The justices did not need to decide whether any of it was true. They needed to decide only what category the conduct fell into.

And here the answer followed almost mechanically from the rule the Court had just written. The President's authority over the Justice Department, his power to direct investigations, to shape prosecutions, to choose who runs the department, sits at the very center of the executive power the Constitution gives to him alone. This was the exclusive sphere. The core. The tier where immunity is absolute.

So when the Court fed these allegations into its own framework, the conclusion was not a close call. Roberts wrote, quote.

Trump is therefore absolutely immune from prosecution for the alleged conduct involving his discussions with Justice Department officials.

Think about what that holding does. It is not a finding that Trump was innocent of these allegations. It is something different, and in some ways larger. The Court placed this conduct entirely beyond a prosecutor's reach. Absolutely immune. It could not be charged. It could not be proven. The Court would say it could not even be shown to a jury as evidence. For this slice of the indictment, the trial was over before it began.

But most of the indictment did not sort that cleanly. And this is where the opinion slowed down, because the remaining allegations sat in the harder middle of the framework. Official acts, the Court suggested, but not at the absolute core. The tier that carried at least presumptive immunity, the protection that could, in theory, be overcome.

Take the Vice President. The indictment alleged that in a series of conversations, Trump pressured the Vice President to reject states' electoral votes, or to send them back to the state legislatures, during the ceremonial role the Vice President plays when Congress meets to count the votes.

The Court treated this as official conduct. A President and a Vice President discussing how the certification should proceed is, on its face, the business of the executive branch. So presumptive immunity attached. And the question became whether the government could overcome that presumption, whether putting this on trial would intrude on the functions of the executive. The Court did not answer it. It sent it back.

Then the alternate slates of electors. The indictment alleged that after failing to persuade state officials, Trump and others organized competing slates of electors in seven states, who met, and signed certificates, and sent them to Washington. The indictment called those slates fraudulent. But whether that conduct was official or unofficial, whether it belonged in the tier with immunity or the tier without, the Court, once again, did not decide.

And here, the record we have runs out. In the text of the opinion available to us, the Court's analysis of the elector allegations breaks off mid-section. What survives is the Court's refusal to draw that line itself, handing the official-versus-unofficial sorting back down to the trial court to do first.

It would be easy, reading these holdings, to hear only the immunity. Absolute here, presumptive there, a President shielded at nearly every turn. But the opinion was careful to set down a counterweight. A single line the majority insisted it had not abandoned, no matter how far the immunity reached.

Against the whole architecture of immunity it had just built, the Court wrote, quote.

The President, charged with enforcing federal criminal laws, is not above them.

Both things, the Court said, were true at the same time. The President is not above the law. And large parts of what a President does cannot be prosecuted. The entire opinion is an attempt to hold those two ideas in a single hand.

And so the Court did not end the case. It did not acquit, and it did not convict. It built the framework, applied it where the answer was clear, and remanded the rest, sending the official-versus-unofficial line-drawing back down to the District Court to resolve first.

Which left the central question of the case suspended in the air. Not whether a President is immune. The Court had answered that, in tiers. But where, exactly, the line falls. Where an official act ends, and an unofficial one begins. That question went back to a trial court, on a record that, for these charges, would never be finished.

So what did the Supreme Court actually decide that July morning in 2024? It is easier to begin with what it did not. It did not decide whether Donald Trump was guilty of anything. It returned no verdict, and it convicted no one.

What it decided was a question of structure. A former president, the Court held, carries an immunity that belongs to the office itself. Absolute, for the powers the Constitution grants him alone. At least presumptive, for the rest of his official conduct. And none at all for his purely private acts.

Then it handed the case back. The work of sorting the alleged conduct into those categories, official or unofficial, shielded or exposed, returned to the District Court where it started. The underlying charges were never tried. Under our law, Donald Trump remains presumed innocent of every one of them.

And so the question the country carried to the Court came back transformed. Not simply whether a former president can be prosecuted, but how much of what he did in office a prosecutor is even permitted to reach.

Yet the Court left the hardest line of all unmarked. Where exactly does an official act end, and an unofficial one begin? On this record, within the opinion itself, that question went home unanswered. And until someone draws it, we will not know where the office finally ends, and the man begins.
