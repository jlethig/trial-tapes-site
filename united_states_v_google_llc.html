United States V Google Llc

In the late summer of 2024, in a federal courthouse in Washington, a judge named Amit Mehta finished a ruling years in the making. It was the largest monopoly case the United States had brought since it took on Microsoft. And he opened with a single sentence. He wrote, quote,

Google is a monopolist, and it has acted as one to maintain its monopoly.

Eleven words. To reach them, the court had heard from dozens of witnesses across weeks of trial. And the defendant was not some obscure firm buried in regulatory filings. It was Google. The company that, in all likelihood, is how you found your way to this story.

But naming a monopolist is one thing. Forcing open a market one company had held for more than a decade is something else entirely. And so the question now fell to a single judge. Whether he could pry that market back open without overreaching, and whether he could do it before artificial intelligence rewrote what the word search even means.

On the twentieth of October, 2020, the United States Department of Justice walked into a federal courthouse in Washington and filed a complaint. Eleven states joined it. The defendant was Google. The charge was monopoly.

To feel the weight of that, reach back more than twenty years, to the last time the government took a technology giant to court on a charge this size. That defendant was Microsoft. In the decades between, Washington had largely left the great software companies alone. This was the dam breaking.

The law the government invoked was Section Two of the Sherman Act, written in 1890, when the monopolies in question moved oil and steel and railroad cars. And here is a subtlety that matters. Section Two does not make it illegal to be a monopoly. What it forbids is keeping one through conduct that shuts your competitors out. That, the government said, was what Google had done to the market for internet search.

So how does a company monopolize search? The government's answer turned on a single, almost mundane word. Default.

Take a new phone out of its box, or open a fresh browser, and there is already a search engine waiting for you. You did not choose it. Someone chose it for you. And the evidence at trial showed something we might not want to admit about ourselves. Most people never change it. The default is the destination.

The conduct the court would later find anticompetitive was this. Google paid to be that default. It struck agreements to sit in the search box of Apple's Safari browser, and across the vast world of Android phones. The exact terms, the dollars that changed hands, are sealed in the record, redacted from the public opinions, so I won't put a number to them. But the shape of it was plain. Google had bought the one piece of real estate that mattered most. The place where a search begins.

The liability trial ran for nine weeks in the fall of 2023. Dozens of witnesses. Then, for the better part of a year, silence, while the judge wrote. On the fifth of August, 2024, Judge Amit Mehta delivered his answer. And the very first line of his ruling left no room for interpretation. He wrote, and I am quoting it exactly,

Google is a monopolist, and it has acted as one to maintain its monopoly.

Eleven words, sitting at the top of an opinion that ran for hundreds of pages. Now, one caution, and it matters. That finding is not the final word. Google has appealed it to the D.C. Circuit, and a higher court could affirm it, narrow it, or set it aside completely. For now, Google's guilt is settled only in the trial court, and only for now.

But here is the thing about a ruling like this. Declaring that a company broke the law is, in a strange way, the easier part. You are looking backward, at conduct that has already happened. The hard part comes next. Because once you have named the harm, you have to fix it. And fixing it means looking forward, into a future no one can see.

Judge Mehta knew this, and he said so, with a candor you rarely find in a federal opinion. He wrote, quote,

here the court is asked to gaze into a crystal ball and look to the future. Not exactly a judge’s forte.

Think about what he is admitting. A judge is trained to weigh what happened, to apply settled rules to fixed facts. He is not trained to be a prophet of markets. And yet the remedy he was about to design would have to anticipate where search itself was heading, in an industry that reinvents itself every few years.

That tension, between naming a wrong and engineering its cure, would come to define everything that followed. Months later, when the judge sat down to write the final judgment, the document that would actually bind Google, he opened it not with thunder, but with a small, almost rueful confession about the work itself. He wrote, quote,

The age-old saying “the devil is in the details” may not have been devised with the drafting of an antitrust remedies judgment in mind, but it sure does fit.

The devil is in the details. It is the kind of line you might skim right past. But hold onto it, because it may be the truest sentence in this whole story. The fight over what to do about Google would not be won in grand declarations about monopoly and competition. It would be won in the fine print. In definitions. In single words. And that fight is where we turn next.

By the summer of 2024, the question of guilt was, for the moment, settled. Google had been found liable. But a finding of liability is only the first half of an antitrust case. The second half, the half that would go on to consume more than a year of hearings, was deceptively simple to state and almost impossible to answer. What do you do about it?

There is a common assumption that the dramatic moment in any trial is the verdict. In antitrust law, that is rarely true. A verdict tells you that a law was broken. A remedy has to rebuild the thing the law was meant to protect, a competitive market, and rebuilding is always harder than assigning blame. The judge had named the disease. Now he had to write the cure.

In April of 2025, the courtroom filled again. Over roughly three weeks, the court heard from nearly fifty witnesses. Executives, economists, engineers, rivals. This was not a trial about what Google had done. That had been decided. This was a trial about the future, and about how far a court should reach into it.

The plaintiffs wanted something dramatic. At its most aggressive, the government's theory pointed toward a structural remedy, breaking pieces off of Google, including its Chrome browser. Google argued for something far narrower, a set of behavioral rules it could live with. Between those two poles sat Judge Mehta, holding a question that no amount of testimony could fully settle. How much surgery does a market actually need?

And then the ground began to move under everyone's feet. Because while the lawyers argued about the search engine of the past decade, the witnesses kept describing a different product, one that was changing in real time. The most striking version of that came from Google's own chief executive, Sundar Pichai.

Pichai testified about artificial intelligence, and what it would mean for the company's flagship product. He did not hedge. According to the court's findings of fact, he told the court, in plain terms, that

AI technology is going to deeply transform Google Search

Think about what that does to a courtroom. The judge is being asked to design rules for a market, and the company's own leader is testifying that the central product in that market is about to become something else entirely. A remedy written for yesterday's search engine might be obsolete before the ink dries. The cure had to be aimed not at the thing in front of the court, but at a thing still taking shape.

There was another complication, and it came from a company that was not even on trial. Apple. Apple was not a defendant in this case. It had no verdict against it, no liability to answer for. But Apple had a great deal at stake, because for years it had been on the receiving end of the very deals at the heart of the case.

Before the remedies hearing had fully gotten underway, Apple tried to step in. Having been denied the right to formally intervene, it filed an emergency motion to stay the proceedings, arguing that it could not adequately defend its own right to contract with Google. On February 2nd, 2025, the court denied that motion. The remedies phase would go forward, and a non-party's contracts would be reshaped by a case it could only watch from the gallery.

It is worth pausing on that. One of the quieter truths of antitrust law is that when a court restructures a dominant firm, it inevitably reaches the partners who built their businesses around it. The deal was between Google and Apple. But the remedy would touch them both, and Apple would have to absorb it largely as a bystander.

As the two sides moved from broad principles to the actual words of the judgment, the fight grew narrower and sharper. And one dispute in particular captured how much can turn on a single alleged conversation. It concerned something the lawyers called conditioning.

The plaintiffs told the court that Google had, in a meeting, confirmed an intention to do something specific. As the plaintiffs described it, Google would be willing to conditionally refuse to license its Play Store to device makers, depending on whether those makers distributed Google's search-related products. If true, it was exactly the kind of leverage the whole case was meant to prohibit.

Google's response was not a clarification. It was a flat denial. Google's counsel told the court that the company had never made any such representation, that the plaintiffs had simply gotten it wrong. So the court was left with a genuine he-said, she-said, two officers of the court describing the same meeting in irreconcilable ways, with no transcript to settle it.

Now, here is the elegant part of how the judge handled it. He did not have to decide who was telling the truth about the meeting. He only had to decide what the decree should say going forward. And Google had given him an opening, because Google insisted, repeatedly, that it would never engage in conditioning of that kind.

If that was genuinely true, the judge reasoned, then Google should have no objection to writing the prohibition down in black and white. A company that swears it will never do a thing loses nothing by promising, on paper, never to do it. The only party who fears a clear rule is the party who wants room to maneuver.

So the judge called the bluff, and he did it in a single, almost conversational sentence. Writing of Google's promise that it would never engage in the conduct, he answered it directly. Quote.

Then it should have no problem with a decree that expressly prohibits “any conditioning of the type.”

It is a small line, but it captures the whole posture of the remedies phase. The court was no longer interested in promises about intentions. It wanted the prohibitions written plainly enough that good faith would cost nothing and bad faith would have nowhere to hide. The cure was being drafted, clause by clause. And the hardest clauses were still to come.

By the late summer of 2025, the central question in this case had changed. It was no longer whether Google had broken the law. The court had already answered that the previous August, in a ruling Google would later carry up on appeal. The new question was harder, and in some ways stranger. Having found the violation, what was the court supposed to do about it?

There were, broadly, two roads. The first was structural. The government had asked the court to break off a piece of Google itself, to force the company to sell its Chrome browser and pry the empire apart at the seams. The second road was behavioral. Leave the company intact, but bind its conduct with a set of rules, and watch it closely for years.

On September the second, 2025, the court chose the second road. In its Remedies Opinion, it declined to order a breakup. There would be no forced sale of Chrome. Instead, the court reached for behavioral remedies, chief among them a requirement that Google share some of the very data that had made its search engine so hard to challenge.

And the court did something else, something that revealed just how far it was willing to look. It folded generative artificial intelligence into the decree. The remedy would reach not only the search engine of today, but the AI products beginning to reshape what search even means. The court was, in effect, writing rules for a market that had not finished being born.

The terms hardened into a Final Judgment on December the fifth, 2025. It ran on data sharing and syndication, the obligation to make certain search results and data available to qualified competitors. It created a Technical Committee to police compliance. And it set a clock. The decree would govern Google's conduct for six years.

But notice what the judgment did not contain. There was no breakup. Google would keep Chrome. It would keep Android. The most dramatic weapon the government had asked for, dismantling the company, the court had set down. This was a remedy meant to open a door, not to demolish the house.

And here is where the story refuses to end cleanly. A final judgment, despite its name, is rarely the final word. On January the sixteenth, 2026, Google filed its notice of appeal to the D.C. Circuit. The company that had been branded a monopolist was taking the fight to a higher court.

Then, on February the third, the plaintiffs filed cross-appeals of their own, unsatisfied that the remedy had not gone further. That same day, the Final Judgment became effective. So consider the strange position everyone now occupied. The judgment was live, binding, in force. And it was being challenged from both sides at once.

Which means nothing you have heard tonight is settled. The liability finding, the remedy, the six-year decree, all of it now sits before the appeals court. It could be affirmed. It could be reversed. It could be sent back down for the trial court to do over. The last word belongs to judges who have not yet spoken.

While that appeal was pending, Google asked the trial court for something narrow but urgent. It wanted a partial stay, an order pausing the data-sharing and syndication remedies until the appeal could be decided. And its argument rested on a single, unforgiving idea: that some harms cannot be undone.

Money lost can be repaid. A contract torn up can be rewritten. But confidential data, once handed to a competitor, can never be pulled back. You cannot un-share a secret. Google's lawyers reduced all of it to one homely, almost folksy image. In their reply brief, they wrote that once the data goes out the door, quote,

the cat is out of the bag

It is a small phrase carrying an enormous claim. If Google was right, then sharing the data even once would inflict a wound that no appeals court could ever heal, no matter how the case came out. The remedy itself, Google argued, might do irreversible damage before anyone could decide whether it was lawful in the first place.

On May the seventh, 2026, the court answered. It declined to pause the remedies. The feared harm, the judge concluded, was not yet certain, and not yet imminent. The data sharing was not about to begin tomorrow, and a danger that has not arrived is not a reason to halt a judgment already in force.

But the judge did not slam the door. He denied the request without prejudice, a phrase that carries a quiet invitation. It meant Google was free to come back and ask again, later, when the remedies drew closer to actually taking effect and the harm looked less like a fear and more like a fact.

And so, as of the most recent word in this case, the machinery grinds on. The judgment stands, effective but unfinished. The appeal waits to be heard. The remedies inch toward a market that artificial intelligence is rewriting faster than any court can rule. The story of whether one judge can pry open a market is, for now, still being written.

In the end, a single federal judge looked at the most-used product on the internet and concluded that Google had not simply won the search market. It had broken the law to keep it.

But naming the problem and fixing it proved to be two different things. The court declined to break the company apart. Instead, it ordered Google to share the data behind its dominance, to open its results to rivals, and to live under six years of oversight.

And none of it is settled. Google has appealed. The plaintiffs have cross-appealed. The judgment is in effect, but it is not the final word, and the court above could uphold it, narrow it, or send the entire case back to begin again.

Beneath the legal fight sits a harder problem the judge named himself. He compared his task to gazing into a crystal ball, predicting the future of a market that kept shifting while he wrote. By the time the remedies took hold, artificial intelligence was already rewriting what the word search even means.

So the case ends where it began, on one unresolved question. Whether a court can force a market open without overreaching, and before that market becomes something no one recognizes, remains unanswered. That answer now belongs to the D.C. Circuit, which must decide whether a judge gazing into the future got it right.
