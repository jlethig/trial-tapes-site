The Second Amendment is twenty-seven words long. It was ratified in 1791. And for the next two hundred and seventeen years, the Supreme Court of the United States never squarely decided what those twenty-seven words actually protected.

A well-regulated militia, necessary to the security of a free State. The right of the people to keep and bear Arms. Two clauses, stitched together in a single sentence, separated by a comma. Generations of justices had found ways not to choose between them.

Then, on June 26th, 2008, Justice Antonin Scalia opened the majority opinion in District of Columbia versus Heller, and reduced two centuries of argument to a single sentence. He wrote, quote.

We consider whether a District of Columbia prohibition on the possession of usable handguns in the home violates the Second Amendment to the Constitution.

Not muskets. Not militias. Handguns, in the home. Five justices would say that prohibition violated the Constitution. Four would say it did not. If the answer was supposed to be obvious from the text, then it is worth asking why it took more than two centuries to arrive.

And why, in the end, it came down to a single vote.

To understand what brought Dick Anthony Heller to the Supreme Court of the United States, you have to start with the city he lived in. In 2001, the District of Columbia codified one of the most restrictive firearms regimes in the country. Not a ban on a particular kind of weapon. A near-total ban on the most common one.

Under the D.C. Code, the city would not issue you a registration certificate for a handgun. And without that certificate, simply possessing the weapon was a crime. You could not legally carry a pistol in public without a license, and the city did not, as a practical matter, issue those licenses. Even a long gun, owned lawfully, had to sit inside your home unloaded and disassembled, or bound at the trigger by a lock.

The statute did not draw a line between the dangerous and the responsible. It did not separate the sober from the violent, the police officer from the felon. Inside a private home in the nation's capital, the law's answer to the question of whether you could keep a loaded handgun to defend yourself was, simply, no.

Which brings us to Dick Anthony Heller. The opinion the Supreme Court would eventually issue introduces him in a single sentence, almost in passing. The Justice writing for the Court put it this way.

Respondent Dick Heller is a D. C. special police officer.

By day, that meant Heller stood post at the Federal Judicial Center, the federal courts' administrative office, blocks from the Capitol. The District trusted him to walk into that building every morning with a loaded sidearm on his hip. The District gave him the gun. The District authorized him to use it, if it came to it, in defense of the federal judiciary.

In 2003, Heller decided he would also like to keep a handgun at his home. Not the duty weapon. A handgun of his own, in his own apartment, for the reason most people who keep one keep one. He filled out the District's paperwork. The District refused.

The man the District trusted to protect federal judges was, in his own living room, forbidden by that same District from owning the kind of weapon he carried at work. So he sued.

The case moved the way constitutional cases tend to move. Slowly. And with detours.

In 2004, the United States District Court for the District of Columbia dismissed Heller's complaint. The lower court read the Second Amendment the way most federal courts had read it for decades. As a guarantee tied to militia service. Not as a personal right an ordinary citizen could invoke to strike down a city's gun law.

Three years later, in 2007, the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals reversed. The panel held that the Second Amendment did protect an individual right, and that the District's handgun ban and its trigger-lock rule could not survive that reading. For the first time in modern American history, a federal appellate court had struck down a major gun law on Second Amendment grounds.

The District of Columbia asked the Supreme Court to take the case. And the Court, in the dry shorthand the United States Reports uses for what would become the most consequential decision of its term, said yes. The order simply records, quote.

We granted certiorari.

For the first time since the Second Amendment was ratified in seventeen ninety-one, the Supreme Court of the United States was going to sit down and decide, on the merits, what those twenty-seven words meant. Two hundred and seventeen years of constitutional silence were about to end.

The Second Amendment is twenty-seven words long. The Supreme Court had spent more than two centuries declining to say, in full, what those twenty-seven words protected. Now, in the spring of 2008, a five-justice majority sat down to do exactly that.

Here is the sentence, in full. A well regulated Militia, being necessary to the security of a free State, the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed. One sentence. Two commas. And two ideas pressed up against each other so tightly that for two hundred years, readers had been arguing about which one controlled.

Justice Scalia's first move, writing for the majority, is structural. He cuts the sentence in half. The opening half, about a well regulated militia, he calls the prefatory clause. The closing half, about the right of the people to keep and bear arms, he calls the operative clause. Then he announces a rule of grammar that will do most of the work in this opinion. A prefatory clause, he says, can announce a purpose. It cannot limit a right.

Think about that for a moment. Two centuries of argument over whether the militia language narrows the right, and the majority's answer is essentially this. The militia clause tells you why the right is protected. It does not tell you who gets to exercise it. The operative clause does that. And the operative clause says, the people.

From there, Scalia walks the reader, slowly, through every other place the Constitution uses the phrase the people. The First Amendment's right of the people to peaceably assemble. The Fourth Amendment's right of the people to be secure in their persons. In every other context, he argues, that phrase refers to individuals, not to a state institution. He closes the section with a sentence that the rest of the opinion will lean on.

We start therefore with a strong presumption that the Second Amendment right is exercised individually and belongs to all Americans.

A strong presumption. Exercised individually. Belongs to all Americans. That single sentence is the doctrinal hinge of the entire case. Once you accept it, the rest of the opinion is essentially a long defense of why the prefatory clause does not pull that presumption back down to a militia-bounded right.

But a right to do what, exactly. The text says, to keep and bear Arms. And so the majority turns next to the word Arms. This is where the opinion begins to bite.

The District of Columbia, defending its ban, had argued that the Second Amendment, if it protects anything, protects only the weapons in common use in 1791. Muskets. Flintlocks. Things you load down the barrel. Modern handguns, the argument went, were simply not what the framers had in mind. Scalia takes that argument and dispatches it in two sentences. And in the process, he uses a word you almost never see in a published Supreme Court opinion.

Some have made the argument, bordering on the frivolous, that only those arms in existence in the 18th century are protected by the Second Amendment. We do not interpret constitutional rights that way.

Bordering on the frivolous. That is not the temperate language of judicial restraint. That is a justice saying, on the record, that an argument made in good faith by a city government, joined by amici, briefed in full, is not even worth taking seriously. He compares it, in the surrounding sentences, to the suggestion that the First Amendment does not reach the internet because the printing press of 1791 did not produce websites. And then he moves on.

Which brings the opinion to the hardest piece of text in the entire Amendment. The verb. To keep and bear Arms.

The District, and Justice Stevens in dissent, had pressed an elegant reading. To keep and bear arms, they argued, was a single military idiom. Not two separate verbs. One unified phrase, meaning, in the language of the founding era, to serve as a soldier. Under that reading, the right belongs only to those bearing arms in militia service.

Scalia's response is, in places, almost playful. He goes through founding-era dictionaries. He goes through state constitutional analogues. He concedes that bear arms, in some military contexts, did carry that idiomatic weight. But to read the phrase as a single unit, he says, you have to do violence to the word that comes before it. You have to pretend that keep was just along for the ride. And then he reaches for an analogy that has, in the eighteen years since, been quoted more often than almost anything else he wrote.

It would be rather like saying “He filled and kicked the bucket” to mean “He filled the bucket and died.” Grotesque.

Grotesque. One word. A complete sentence. Sitting on the page of a United States Supreme Court opinion, directed at a reading of the Constitution offered, in good faith, by four colleagues sitting at the same bench. It is the most quoted rhetorical beat of the entire case, and it tells you something about how the majority is operating here. They are not just announcing a holding. They are dismantling, in public, a reading they consider so strained that the only honest response, in the Justice's view, is mockery. Whether you find that bracing or unbecoming probably depends on what you already thought about the Second Amendment when you sat down to read.

To understand why Justice Scalia thought the text already answered the question, you have to follow him backward. Across an ocean. Across more than a century. The majority opinion grounds itself, in part, in English history. And the story it tells goes something like this.

In the late sixteen hundreds, two Stuart kings, Charles the Second and James the Second, used hand-picked militias to disarm their Protestant enemies. By the time Parliament forced James off the throne in sixteen eighty-eight, the right to keep arms for self-defense had become, in the majority's reading of that history, one of the wrongs that needed correcting.

The English Bill of Rights, enacted the following year, included a guarantee that Protestants, and I am quoting the language directly,

may have arms for their defense.

Scalia treats that line as the direct ancestor of the Second Amendment. The right to be armed, in his telling, was already, in seventeenth-century England, a right of individuals against a government that might one day try to take it away.

Now, a careful note here. This is the majority's reading of the historical record. Justice Stevens and Justice Breyer, in their dissents, read the same English history differently. They see a guarantee tied to militia service, not to private self-defense in the home. The history, in other words, is not as quiet as Scalia's prose makes it sound.

From England, the opinion moves to the founding. And here Scalia describes the militia not as a select unit, not as a uniformed force, but as, quote,

all males physically capable of acting in concert for the common defense.

He is borrowing that phrase from an earlier Supreme Court decision, United States versus Miller, decided in nineteen thirty-nine. The militia, in this reading, is not the army. It is the people themselves, expected to show up with their own weapons when the moment demanded it.

And then the opinion does something worth pausing on. It cites the first federal Militia Act, passed in seventeen ninety-two. That statute, the opinion notes, required, quote,

only whites enroll.

Free, able-bodied, white male citizens between the ages of eighteen and forty-five. The founding-era militia, in other words, was not all the people. It was a subset of the people, defined by race and sex and age. The opinion cites this without dwelling on it. We should not skip past it. The historical record the majority leans on is also a record of who, at the founding, was kept outside the door.

By the time Scalia has finished walking through the English background and the American practice, he is ready to deliver two things, almost back to back. A final rhetorical jab at the dissent. And the holding of the case.

The jab, tucked into a footnote, takes the dissent's reading of the Second Amendment and disposes of it with a literary reference. Scalia writes, quote,

The right "to carry arms in the militia for the purpose of killing game" is worthy of the mad hatter.

It is a strange line to find in a Supreme Court opinion. Lewis Carroll, dragged into a constitutional decision to make the dissent sound like nonsense out of Wonderland. It tells you something about the temperature of the disagreement on the Court that summer.

And then, after more than fifty pages of text and history, comes the conclusion that becomes the rule of the case. Scalia writes,

There seems to us no doubt, on the basis of both text and history, that the Second Amendment conferred an individual right to keep and bear arms.

Twenty-seven words in the Constitution. Two hundred and seventeen years of judicial silence. And now, this sentence.

But there were four justices who looked at the same text, and the same history, and arrived somewhere very different.

Justice John Paul Stevens, writing for the dissent, read the prefatory clause about a well-regulated militia not as a mere announcement of purpose, but as a limit on the right itself. In his telling, the Second Amendment protected the right of citizens to bear arms in the service of the militia, and no more than that. Whatever right to private self-defense exists in this country, in Stevens's view, comes from somewhere else. Not from those twenty-seven words.

Justice Stephen Breyer wrote separately. His objection was different. Even if you grant the individual right, Breyer argued, the majority leaves judges with no workable way to weigh a gun law against it. No standard. No test. Only history.

Scalia's opinion takes note of Breyer's framing and dismisses it. The majority refers to, quote,

Justice Breyer's assertion that individual self-defense is merely a "subsidiary interest"

of the right to keep and bear arms. A subsidiary interest. As though self-defense in the home were a side concern. The two justices, in other words, were not even agreeing on what the right was for. Self-defense at home, or service in a militia. The same twenty-seven words. Two completely different rights.

Heller, in the end, came down five to four. Five justices saw an individual right hiding in plain sight, anchored in centuries of text and history. Four justices saw a different right entirely, bounded by militia service, and warned that the majority was rewriting the rules of judicial review along with the rules of the Second Amendment.

Both readings were authored by people who had spent their lives reading the Constitution. Both readings were defended at length, in print, by people who were, by any honest measure, serious. The Second Amendment, it turned out, was not as quiet as it looked.

Here is what the Court actually decided that day in June of 2008. One thing. One narrow thing. The Second Amendment protected an individual right. And under that right, the District of Columbia could not forbid an ordinary citizen from keeping a handgun, loaded, in his own home.

That was the holding. Long guns. Concealed carry. Assault weapon bans. Red flag laws. Prohibitions on felons. Prohibitions on the mentally ill. Heller did not resolve any of it. The opinion explicitly left most of that for another day.

Those days came. In 2010, in McDonald against Chicago, the Court extended the individual right against the states. And then in 2022, in Bruen, the Court told the lower courts to stop balancing public safety against the individual right, and instead to test gun laws against the historical tradition of firearms regulation in America.

Which is how, fifteen years after Scalia warned that the Constitution did not protect only those arms in existence in the eighteenth century, federal judges across the country found themselves doing something very close to that. Reading old statutes. Hunting for analogues. Arguing about the founding.
