Gideon V Wainwright

Bay County, Florida. Nineteen sixty-one. A man named Clarence Earl Gideon stands at the defense table in the Fourteenth Judicial Circuit court. He has no money. He has no lawyer. And he has just asked the judge to appoint him one.

The judge, by every indication preserved in the record, sounds sorry about what he is about to say. He tells Gideon this.

Mr. Gideon, I am sorry, but I cannot appoint Counsel to represent you in this case. Under the laws of the State of Florida, the only time the Court can appoint Counsel to represent a Defendant is when that person is charged with a capital offense. I am sorry, but I will have to deny your request to appoint Counsel to defend you in this case.

Gideon, standing alone at the defense table, answers with a single sentence. One sentence, on the record, that will become the spine of his case.

The United States Supreme Court says I am entitled to be represented by Counsel.

He was a drifter facing a state felony charge in a courtroom that had every reason to ignore him. And he had just named the highest court in the country as his authority. The question that followed him out of that Florida courtroom, all the way to Washington, was whether he was right. Whether the United States Supreme Court had actually said what he claimed it had.

Before we go to the Supreme Court, we have to go to Panama City, Florida. To the Fourteenth Judicial Circuit Court, in Bay County. To a courtroom in the summer of 1961.

The defendant's name is Clarence Earl Gideon. He is fifty-one years old. He has spent most of his adult life in and out of state prisons. He drinks. He has been, off and on, homeless. And the State of Florida has filed an information against him, alleging that he broke into a poolroom in the early morning hours, intending to commit a misdemeanor once he got inside. Under Florida law, that intent turns the break-in into a felony.

Gideon has no money. He has no lawyer. And on the morning the case is called for trial, he stands up before the bench, and he asks the court to appoint one.

The transcript of that exchange survives. It is short. The judge speaks first. And what he says is recorded, word for word, in the United States Reports. I'm going to read it to you exactly as he said it.

Mr. Gideon, I am sorry, but I cannot appoint Counsel to represent you in this case. Under the laws of the State of Florida, the only time the Court can appoint Counsel to represent a Defendant is when that person is charged with a capital offense. I am sorry, but I will have to deny your request to appoint Counsel to defend you in this case.

Read that one again in your head. The judge is not being cruel. He is being accurate. Florida law in 1961 said exactly what he said it said. A defendant facing a felony, but not a capital felony, had no right to a court-appointed lawyer, no matter how poor he was. The judge was applying the rule as it existed. And, you can hear it in the transcript, he was sorry about it.

What Gideon says next is one sentence. Eleven words. The Supreme Court would later set it off on its own page. It traveled, eventually, from a Bay County courtroom to Washington, D.C.

The United States Supreme Court says I am entitled to be represented by Counsel.

He was wrong. At least, in 1961, he was wrong as a matter of black-letter constitutional law. The Supreme Court had said no such thing about state felony defendants. Nineteen years earlier, in a case called Betts v. Brady, the Court had said almost the opposite. But Clarence Earl Gideon, standing alone at a defense table in the state of Florida, believed otherwise. And he said it out loud. And the court reporter wrote it down.

And then the trial began.

Here is what Clarence Earl Gideon did, without a lawyer, in a felony jury trial in the state of Florida. He made an opening statement to the jury. He cross-examined the State's witnesses as they came to the stand. He called witnesses of his own in his defense. He chose not to testify himself. And when the evidence was in, he stood up and gave a short closing argument, in which he emphasized his innocence.

The Supreme Court would later note all of this, in a single short paragraph in Justice Black's opinion. The Court does not tell us what the witnesses said. It does not describe the State's evidence. It does not tell us what Gideon argued to the jury. It tells us only that he did these things, the way a lawyer would have done them, except for the one detail that he was not a lawyer.

The jury was out for a short time. They came back with a verdict of guilty. The judge sentenced Clarence Earl Gideon to five years in state prison.

And that should have been the end of the story. A poor man, tried for a poolroom break-in, convicted by a jury of his peers, sentenced to five years. The kind of case that moves through American criminal courts every single day, and disappears into a docket number. Nobody outside Bay County would have heard of Clarence Earl Gideon. Nobody outside Bay County had any reason to.

Except that, in the Florida state prison at Raiford, Clarence Earl Gideon sat down with a pencil. And a piece of prison stationery. And he started to write.

After the jury returned its verdict, Clarence Earl Gideon was sent to Florida State Prison to serve out the five years the judge had given him. And it was there, in his cell, that the case truly began.

Gideon had no lawyer in prison either. What he had was time, and a prison library, and a body of law he was teaching himself. He read about habeas corpus, the old common-law writ a prisoner can use to ask a court whether his confinement is lawful. He read about the Bill of Rights. He read about the Fourteenth Amendment, the one passed after the Civil War that binds the states to certain federal guarantees.

And then he sat down and wrote a petition to the Supreme Court of Florida. He wrote it by hand. He wrote it on the kind of printed form a prison makes available to inmates who want to file with the courts. And in the body of it, he made a single claim. The United States Supreme Court would later preserve that claim, word for word, in a footnote to its own opinion. Here, in Gideon's own handwriting, is what he wrote.

I, Clarence Earl Gideon, claim that I was denied the rights of the 4th, 5th and 14th amendments of the Bill of Rights.

That sentence is the only fragment of Gideon's handwritten petition that the Supreme Court would later reproduce in its opinion. Twenty-one words. A man with no legal training, reaching for amendments he had read about in a prison library, asking the highest court in his state to recognize that something had gone wrong in that Bay County courtroom.

The Florida Supreme Court considered the petition. And then, without writing an opinion, without explaining itself in a single line, it denied all relief.

There was no reasoning to argue with. No legal analysis to challenge. The state court simply, silently, said no, and that should, by every ordinary expectation, have been the end of it.

To understand why Florida's silence carried such weight, you have to understand what was sitting on top of Gideon's petition like a stone. A 1942 Supreme Court case called Betts v. Brady.

In Betts, a divided Court had decided that the Sixth Amendment's guarantee of the right to counsel did not, by its own force, apply to the states. The Sixth Amendment, by its terms, bound only the federal government. For state defendants, the question was governed by the Fourteenth Amendment's due process clause, and the Betts Court held that, as a general matter, the failure to appoint a lawyer to a poor state defendant did not, on its own, violate due process. It might violate due process in some special circumstance. But not automatically. Not as a constitutional rule.

That was the doctrine. That was the wall. For twenty-one years, state courts and lower federal courts had cited Betts when they declined to appoint counsel for poor defendants in noncapital cases. The Florida trial judge, when he apologized to Gideon and refused his request, had not been freelancing. He had been following the law. The law that the Supreme Court of the United States had itself written.

So when Gideon's handwritten petition reached Washington, it was not asking the Court for a small favor. It was asking the Court to tear down its own wall.

And the Court agreed to hear him.

It granted certiorari. It treated the pauper's petition from a Florida prison cell with the same dignity it would have treated any other. And then it did something that, by itself, signaled what kind of question this was about to become. It appointed a lawyer for him. The very thing Florida had refused to do, the United States Supreme Court did, before a single argument had been heard.

And the Court told the parties exactly what it wanted them to brief. Not just whether Gideon's particular trial had been fair under the existing rule. Not just whether some special circumstance in Bay County in 1961 made his case an exception. The Court wanted to know something much larger. The Court wanted to know whether Betts v. Brady should be reconsidered.

That is what a Supreme Court does when it is thinking about overruling itself.

March 18, 1963. The Supreme Court of the United States is ready to deliver its decision in the matter of Gideon versus Wainwright.

Justice Hugo Black holds the opinion in his hands. He is seventy-seven years old. Twenty-one years earlier, when this same Court decided Betts versus Brady, Black had written the dissent. He had argued, then, that the Sixth Amendment's right to counsel should apply to the states. He had lost.

Now, two decades later, with a unanimous Court behind him, Justice Black sits down to write the opinion that will finish the argument he started in 1942.

The opinion is methodical. It traces the history of the right to counsel. It walks through Powell versus Alabama, through Johnson versus Zerbst, through Betts itself. And then, somewhere near the middle, Black writes a sentence so plain that, if you read it too quickly, you might miss what it does.

That government hires lawyers to prosecute and defendants who have the money hire lawyers to defend are the strongest indications of the widespread belief that lawyers in criminal courts are necessities, not luxuries.

Consider what Justice Black is doing there. He is not citing a treatise. He is not invoking natural law. He is making an observation about how the system already behaves. Prosecutors are paid by the state. Wealthy defendants pay their own. Every actor in the criminal courtroom who has a choice in the matter chooses to be represented. The poor defendant, alone, is asked to do without.

From this observation, Black draws the conclusion. And here is where Betts versus Brady ends.

Not only these precedents, but also reason and reflection, require us to recognize that, in our adversary system of criminal justice, any person haled into court, who is too poor to hire a lawyer, cannot be assured a fair trial unless counsel is provided for him. This seems to us to be an obvious truth.

An obvious truth. Think about the weight a Justice has to put on those three words to make them carry the result. It is obvious, Black is saying, that you cannot have a fair trial without a lawyer. It is obvious that a poor man without counsel is not standing on equal ground. It is obvious that the Constitution, properly read, has been saying so all along.

And to show that he is not inventing this principle, Black reaches back to 1932, to Powell versus Alabama, the Scottsboro case. Justice Sutherland, writing then, had explained why even a capable man cannot defend himself in court.

Even the intelligent and educated layman has small and sometimes no skill in the science of law.

Sutherland had called counsel the guiding hand at every step in the proceedings. Black, thirty-one years later, is pulling that lineage forward. The point is not that the Court is creating a new right. The point is that the right has been here the whole time, and the Court is finally finishing the work of recognizing it.

The opinion is unanimous. But three Justices, agreeing with the result, want to add something of their own. The concurrences begin.

Justice William O. Douglas writes briefly, and largely to insist on the broader proposition that the Fourteenth Amendment makes the protections of the Bill of Rights applicable to the states. For Douglas, this case is one more step on a road he has been walking for years.

Justice Tom Clark writes next, and he writes to close a door. Under Betts, counsel had been required in capital cases but not always in noncapital ones. Clark refuses to live with that distinction any longer. He writes, and these are his words,

I must conclude here, as in Kinsella, supra, that the Constitution makes no distinction between capital and noncapital cases.

And then Justice John Marshall Harlan adds his voice. Harlan is the Court's most careful conservative, the Justice most reluctant to overturn precedent. So when Harlan writes that Betts has been weakened by every later case, when he says the rule had become a fiction the Court was no longer applying, his words carry a particular kind of weight.

To continue a rule which is honored by this Court only with lip service is not a healthy thing, and, in the long run, will do disservice to the federal system.

Three Justices, three different arguments, all arriving at the same place. The right to a lawyer in a state criminal trial is not a privilege the wealthy purchase. It is not a kindness the state may extend. It is, the Court has now said, a fundamental requirement of due process. And it belongs, by way of the Fourteenth Amendment, to every defendant in every state, capital or not, rich or poor.

On March eighteenth, nineteen sixty-three, the judgment against Clarence Earl Gideon was reversed. The cause was remanded. And Betts versus Brady, twenty-one years of constitutional doctrine, was overruled.

What the Court had done, in plain language, was this. The right to be represented by counsel in a serious criminal case, long established in federal courts, was now anchored in the Fourteenth Amendment, and obligatory upon the states. Every defendant too poor to hire a lawyer was now, as a matter of constitutional law, entitled to one.

Gideon's own case was sent back to Florida. What happened on remand sits outside the record this episode draws from. What we can say is what the Supreme Court said. A fair trial, in an adversary system, is not possible without a lawyer.

Justice Black had called it an obvious truth. He meant that in nineteen sixty-three, with the precedents lined up and the country watching. But for the twenty-one years before he wrote those words, this truth had not been obvious enough to be the law. It had taken a handwritten petition, from a prison cell, to make it so.

That is the case of Gideon versus Wainwright.
