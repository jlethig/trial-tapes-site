In February 2025, a newly confirmed Secretary of Homeland Security signed two documents, days apart. Together, they were meant to undo legal protection the government had already granted to hundreds of thousands of Venezuelans living in the United States.

The program is called Temporary Protected Status. It had existed for thirty-five years. And in all that time, under administrations of both parties, no one had tried what Secretary Kristi Noem was now attempting: to reach back and erase protections a predecessor had already granted.

When the case reached the federal courts, the judges put the point plainly. They wrote, and these are their words exactly:

No administration has attempted to vacate an existing temporary protection status designation in the thirty-five years in which the program has existed.

Which left one question beneath everything that followed. Not whether the secretary had acted, but whether she could. Whether one Cabinet official can undo what another has granted, or whether Congress had placed that power beyond her reach. That was what the courts would decide.

To understand this case, you first have to understand a quiet piece of immigration law that Congress wrote in 1990. It is called Temporary Protected Status, or TPS. The idea is simple. When a country becomes too dangerous to send people back to, because of war, or a natural disaster, or some other extraordinary crisis, the Secretary of Homeland Security can designate that country. Its nationals already living in the United States are then allowed to stay, and to work, lawfully, for as long as the designation holds.

Venezuela became one of those countries. On January 19th, 2021, in the final hours of his first term, President Trump granted Venezuelans a related protection called Deferred Enforced Departure. In doing so, he described conditions in Venezuela as, quote, the worst humanitarian crisis in the Western Hemisphere in recent memory.

Weeks later, a new administration took the next step. On March 9th, 2021, Secretary of Homeland Security Alejandro Mayorkas formally designated Venezuela for Temporary Protected Status, an eighteen-month period of protection. With that single notice, roughly three hundred and twenty-three thousand people became eligible to stay.

Over the next two years, that protection was renewed and then widened. In 2022, Mayorkas extended it for another eighteen months. Then in October 2023, he did something larger. He extended the designation and redesignated Venezuela at the same time, opening eligibility to roughly four hundred and seventy-two thousand more people. The result was a two-track system, one group protected under the original 2021 designation, a second, larger group under the new 2023 one.

And then, in the last days of the Biden administration, Mayorkas tied it all together. On January 17th, 2025, he extended the 2023 designation through October 2nd, 2026, and consolidated the two filing tracks into one. So as power was about to change hands in Washington, Venezuelans living under TPS had protection on paper that stretched nearly two more years into the future.

Three days later, everything changed. On January 20th, 2025, President Trump returned to office and signed Executive Order 14159, titled Protecting the American People Against Invasion. At the Department of Homeland Security, there was a new secretary, newly confirmed: Kristi Noem.

What happened next moved quickly. On February 3rd, just seventeen days into her tenure, Secretary Noem issued what the government called a Vacatur Notice, an order purporting to undo Mayorkas's January extension. Two days after that, on February 5th, she issued a second notice, a Termination Notice, ending the 2023 Venezuela designation altogether.

Now, the second notice, the termination, was an exercise of authority the statute plainly gives the secretary. But the first one, the vacatur, was something else. According to the challengers who would soon sue, and according to the courts that later reviewed it, no administration had ever attempted to vacate an existing TPS designation in the program's entire history. Whether the law actually allowed it is the question this case exists to answer, and it is a question that, as we will see, is not yet finally resolved.

Set aside the legal machinery for a moment and consider what these two notices meant on the ground. Hundreds of thousands of people had built lives here under a status the government had granted and repeatedly renewed. Almost overnight, that footing was thrown into doubt.

When the Ninth Circuit panel later described the human consequences, Judge Wardlaw, writing for the court, put it in unadorned terms. She wrote, and I am quoting the opinion directly.

Hundreds of thousands of TPS holders, who had been living in the United States and relying on their legal immigration status for years, could become targets for detention and deportation.

Targets for detention and deportation. People who, the day before, had been here lawfully. That is what was at stake. And so the case narrowed to a single question: whether one Cabinet secretary could erase what another had granted, or whether Congress had placed that power beyond her reach.

Strip away the notices and the filing tracks, and the case came down to a single question of power. When one Secretary of Homeland Security grants Temporary Protected Status, can the next Secretary simply erase it? Not let it expire. Erase it, mid-term, as if it had never been granted at all.

To answer that, the court had to read the statute Congress wrote back in 1990. That law lays out exactly when a country can be designated, how long a designation lasts, and what a Secretary must do to end one. The plaintiffs argued the statute gives a Secretary the tools to extend or terminate on a fixed schedule, but says nothing, anywhere, about reaching back to undo a designation a predecessor had already locked in.

The Government read the same statute differently. In its view, a new Secretary inherits the full discretion of the office, including the discretion to revisit and withdraw a recent decision she believed was wrong. And Secretary Noem did believe the 2023 designation was wrong.

Her reasoning was set out in the Termination Notice itself, published in the Federal Register. It is worth hearing in her own words, because that published rationale is the official record of why she acted. She wrote, and I am quoting the notice directly,

[c]ontinuing to permit Venezuelans under the 2023 TPS designation to remain in the United States does not champion core American interests or put American interests first.

That was the justification. Whether it was a lawful basis to dismantle protections that hundreds of thousands of people had built their lives around, that was precisely what the plaintiffs disputed, and what the courts would have to decide. Their theory was simple. No matter how a Secretary framed her reasons, the statute did not give her the power to vacate a designation already in force.

When the Ninth Circuit panel looked at the structure of that statute, it found a design, and a deliberate one. Congress, the court said, had not built this program to swing with each election. It had built it to hold steady. Judge Wardlaw put the principle plainly. The panel wrote,

In enacting the TPS statute, Congress designed a system of temporary status that was predictable, dependable, and insulated from electoral politics.

Predictable. Dependable. Insulated from electoral politics. Those three ideas carried the weight of the panel's reasoning. The whole point of the program, as the court understood it, was that a person granted protection could rely on it for the period they were promised, without watching it dissolve the moment a new administration took office.

And that reading shaped everything that followed. If Congress had insulated the system from politics on purpose, then a mid-term vacatur arriving with a change in administration was not just unusual. It cut against the very thing the statute was built to protect. That was the lens through which the panel viewed the Secretary's action.

The legal challenge had moved fast. The National TPS Alliance and seven individual TPS holders sued in federal court in Northern California in February of 2025. By the thirty-first of March, District Judge Edward Chen had already ruled.

Judge Chen reached for a specific tool in the Administrative Procedure Act, Section 705. It lets a court postpone the effective date of an agency action while a lawsuit plays out. He found the plaintiffs were likely to succeed on the merits, and that they faced irreparable harm if the terminations took effect. So he froze the Secretary's notices in place.

The Government appealed straight to the Ninth Circuit. And on the twenty-ninth of August, 2025, the panel affirmed. In the decision that came to be known as NTPSA One, the court held that the plaintiffs were likely to prevail, that a Secretary likely could not vacate a predecessor's designation in this way.

But it is worth being precise about what that ruling was, and what it was not. The panel had affirmed preliminary relief. A finding that the plaintiffs were likely to win, not a final judgment that they had. The deeper fight, over whether this decision would stand at all, was only beginning.

The Ninth Circuit had ruled. But in a case like this one, a ruling is not the same as an ending. What followed was a procedural maze, and to see how it closes, you have to follow how the two sides moved through it.

Twice, the Supreme Court stepped in. The first time came in May of 2025, before the panel had even heard argument. The justices granted a stay, freezing the relief that shielded the TPS holders while the appeal played out, and while any petition to the Supreme Court itself remained possible.

Then, in October, the Court did it again. A second emergency stay, so that nothing on the ground would change while the litigation continued. No removals, no terminations taking effect, the whole question simply held in suspension.

In between those two stays, something stranger happened. In the fall of 2025, the federal government shut down. Appropriations lapsed. And the Department of Justice, defending Secretary Noem's decision, asked the courts to pause the case until funding returned.

The plaintiffs objected, and the shape of their objection mattered. Even with the government shut down, they argued, immigration enforcement had not stopped. Detentions continued. Removals continued. The government was still spending money to act against TPS holders, they said, while asking the court to halt the one proceeding meant to test whether those actions were lawful.

The government's answer rested on the law of lapsed funding. With no appropriations, its lawyers said, the work of defending the case simply had to wait. Which version you found persuasive depended a great deal on where you already stood.

And here the maze turns back on itself. The Government had asked the Supreme Court to freeze the case. But while the appeal sat frozen, the district court kept working, and in September of 2025 it reached a final judgment, setting aside Secretary Noem's notices outright.

That created a problem, at least as the Government saw it. Once the district court ruled for good, the earlier appeal, the one behind the August opinion, arguably no longer mattered. The question had gone moot. And under an old doctrine from a 1950 case called Munsingwear, a court will often wipe a decision off the books when the case goes moot on appeal, so it binds no one.

So the Government moved to vacate the August opinion. To erase it. The argument rested on fairness, and the Government's lawyer, Drew Ensign, put it plainly in the motion. He wrote, and I am quoting from the filing now.

it is in the public interest for litigants to have the opportunity to fully exhaust appellate review and not be bound in ongoing litigation by a precedent that the litigant was prevented from challenging through no fault of its own

Through no fault of its own. That phrase carried the whole argument. The Government was saying it had been trapped, denied full review by the ordinary churn of litigation, and should not be bound by a precedent it never got to test. The plaintiffs, and the panel, saw the cause of that trap very differently.

When the panel answered, it traced the difficulty to its source. The Government complained it had lost its chance at full review, that the case slipped into mootness before the highest court could hear it. But who set those events in motion? The Government had asked for the stays. It had shaped the very posture it now called unfair. As for the circumstances that left the appeal stranded, the court wrote, quote.

those circumstances were, at least in part, a creature of the Government's own making.

A creature of its own making. The doctrine the Government invoked was built to protect a party that loses its appeal through no fault of its own. But the panel had just found the fault lay, at least in part, with the side asking for the favor. And that pointed straight at the last question the court had to answer. Whether to forget what it had already decided.

So the Government had taken the most unusual path left to it. It no longer asked the Ninth Circuit to reverse the August opinion. By now it couldn't. Instead it asked the court to erase that opinion, to vacate it, to treat the words as if they had never been written. And the plaintiffs, the ones who had won that opinion, urged the court to leave it exactly where it stood.

In their brief opposing the motion to vacate, the lead lawyer for the TPS holders, Ahilan Arulanantham, did not open with procedure. He opened with scale. With what he believed was actually riding on the outcome for the more than one million people whose protected status was wrapped up in this fight.

He began the argument with a single sentence. He wrote, and I'm quoting him directly:

It is difficult to overstate the importance of the issues in this case.

Now, that is a lawyer's line. Advocates always say their case matters. But the numbers underneath it were not rhetoric. The 2023 designation alone had made roughly four hundred and seventy-two thousand Venezuelans eligible. Add the earlier grants, and you have hundreds of thousands of people with jobs, families, and years of life built on a single promise. That the status would hold.

On March eighteenth, 2026, the panel gave its answer. The question, the judges said, came down to one thing. The public interest. Whether the public was better served by erasing a published opinion, or by letting it stand. And on that question, the court did not hesitate.

Judge Wardlaw, writing for the panel, put it in a single sentence. She wrote, quote:

Because the public interest weighs heavily against the vacatur of NTPSA I, we decline to vacate our decision.

The opinion would stand. Everything the August panel had said, about the limits on a secretary's power, about a program Congress had built to be insulated from politics, all of it remained good law in the Ninth Circuit. The Government had asked the court to forget. The court declined to forget.

But here the story has to slow down. Because standing law is not the same as a final answer, and it would be easy to walk away from this case believing more was decided than actually was.

Twice, the Supreme Court had stepped in. In May of 2025, and again that October, the justices stayed the relief and froze the terminations in place. The practical effect was a kind of stillness. Nothing changed on the ground. No one covered by the designation was being removed because of Secretary Noem's notices, and no one's status was being stripped while the litigation ground on.

And the Ninth Circuit had affirmed something narrower than it might first appear. It had held that the plaintiffs were likely to succeed. That their challenge to the secretary's action would probably win. Likely. Probably. That is the language of preliminary relief, not the language of a final judgment declaring that Secretary Noem broke the law.

The Supreme Court had signaled one more thing. In staying the case, the justices indicated they were, in their own phrasing, reasonably likely to take it up. Which means the central question, whether one Cabinet secretary can erase the protections another had granted, may not be settled in the Ninth Circuit at all. It may be settled somewhere higher.

So where does that leave the hundreds of thousands of Venezuelans at the center of all this? For now, in suspension. Twice the Supreme Court froze everything in place, which means that as of this telling, no one has been deported under the terminations, and nothing has been finally decided.

The Ninth Circuit has said only that the plaintiffs are likely to be right. Not that they have won. The deeper question, the one this entire fight was always about, whether a single secretary can undo what an earlier one granted, or whether Congress placed that power beyond her reach, is still open.

And it may yet land back at the Supreme Court itself, the one place that has so far only pressed pause. The justices have already called it reasonably likely that they will hear it. If they do, this preliminary fight, with all its motions and stays and dueling readings of a thirty-five-year-old statute, could finally become a definitive answer. Until then, the protections hold, and the question waits.
