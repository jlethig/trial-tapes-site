Lizzie Borden 1893

Full Transcript

On a hot Thursday morning in August of 1892, in a tidy house on Second Street in Fall River, Massachusetts, two people were killed with a hatchet. Andrew Borden, and his wife, Abby. The only other person known to be home that morning was Andrew's grown daughter. A Sunday school teacher named Lizzie.

She would be arrested, indicted, and tried for both killings. And after a trial that gripped the whole country, the jury would deliberate for roughly ninety minutes, and set her free. The town of Fall River never did. For the rest of her life, her neighbors treated the verdict as a mistake the court had made.

So the question left to the court in New Bedford is the same one that has never quite been laid to rest. Whether a churchgoing daughter took a hatchet to her own father and stepmother in the full light of morning, or whether a frightened mill town built its case against the wrong woman, and called it justice.

Fall River, Massachusetts. The summer of 1892. A city built on cotton and cold profit, where the mills ran day and night and the men who owned them counted every dollar twice. On a narrow street near the center of town stood a plain wooden house, and inside it lived a family that had everything money could buy, and almost nothing money could not.

The patriarch was Andrew Jackson Borden. He had made his fortune the careful way, in property and furniture, and for a time in the undertaking trade, fitting the dead of Fall River for their coffins. He was worth a great deal, enough to live among the wealthy on the hill. He chose instead to stay on Second Street, in a house without modern plumbing, where the doors were kept locked from the inside.

Under that roof lived five people. Andrew. His second wife, Abby, whom he had married years after the death of his first. His two grown daughters from that first marriage, Emma and Lizzie. And a young Irish servant named Bridget Sullivan, whom the family, for reasons of their own, called Maggie.

Lizzie was thirty-two years old. She taught Sunday school. She kept company with the respectable women of the city's churches and charitable societies. She was, by every outward measure, exactly the sort of woman a town like Fall River trusted without a second thought.

But the house on Second Street was not a happy one. The daughters and their stepmother lived as near strangers, taking their meals at separate hours, keeping to separate parts of the house. Lizzie, it was said, had stopped calling Abby mother some years before. She called her Mrs. Borden instead.

In the months before that summer, the friction in the house had begun to harden. That spring, Andrew found pigeons roosting in the barn out back. Lizzie, it was said, had grown fond of them. Andrew killed them with a hatchet. And the story would later be told that it troubled her deeply.

In July came a quarrel serious enough that the two sisters left town together for an extended stay away from home. When Lizzie returned, she took a room in a boarding house for a few days before coming back to Second Street. Small things. But after everything that followed, no small thing stayed small.

Then, in the first days of August, sickness came to the house. For several days the whole family was violently ill, gripped by a vomiting that Abby herself feared was poison. The prosecution would later suggest that someone in the household had reason to fear something worse than spoiled food. But that reading came long after, and the jury would weigh it for themselves.

It was during those days that, a druggist later testified, a woman he identified as Lizzie Borden came into his shop asking to buy prussic acid, a deadly poison, saying she needed it to clean a sealskin cape. He refused her, he said, for want of a prescription. Lizzie denied she had ever gone, and the jury at trial would never hear the claim. But that summer, the story spread through Fall River like fever.

On the third of August, a relative arrived. John Vinnicum Morse, an uncle by the first marriage, came to stay and slept in the guest room that night. The next morning he would account for his whereabouts in great and precise detail. Some found that precision reassuring. Others found it strange.

Thursday, the fourth of August. The morning came in hot, the kind of heat that sits heavy in a closed house. Andrew rose early and went out on his usual rounds through the business district, collecting on his properties and his debts. Bridget Sullivan was set to washing the outside of the windows. And Abby went upstairs to make up the guest room.

What investigators reconstructed about that guest room is difficult to tell. Sometime in the middle of the morning, while Andrew was still out and Bridget was at the windows, Abby Borden was attacked there. She was facing her killer when the first blow fell, struck above the ear. She dropped between the bed and the wall. And then, the medical examiners found, she was struck again and again, more than a dozen times, after she had already fallen.

No one in the house, by every account given afterward, heard a thing. Bridget went on with the windows. Whoever did this moved through the house unseen, and Abby Borden lay where she fell for the better part of two hours, while the ordinary business of the morning went on below her.

A little before eleven, Andrew came home. His key would not work the front lock, and Bridget came to let him in. As she struggled with the bolt, she would later testify, she heard a laugh from somewhere above her on the stairs. Andrew, worn down by the heat, lay down on the sitting-room sofa to rest. He did not get up again.

Bridget climbed to her own room on the third floor to lie down in the heat. It could not have been long. Perhaps ten minutes. And then, from the foot of the stairs, she heard Lizzie calling up to her.

Maggie, come quick! Father's dead. Somebody came in and killed him.

Bridget came down. Lizzie stopped her at the sitting-room door and would not let her go in, sending her instead across the street for Dr. Bowen. But Bridget had seen enough. Andrew Borden lay on the sofa where he had settled to rest, and someone had struck him in the face and head with a sharp blade, again and again, while he slept. The blows, the examiners would conclude, had fallen from close above him.

Two people were dead inside a locked house, in the middle of a summer morning, on a respectable street, in a city that prided itself on order. And the only adults known to have been anywhere near them were a servant washing windows on the outside, and Andrew Borden's churchgoing daughter. The question that would consume Fall River, and then the whole country, had already been asked in those first frantic minutes. Who came in. And whether anyone had come in at all.

Three days after the murders, the house on Second Street had become the most watched address in Fall River. Reporters loitered at the fence. Police came and went. And inside, the two Borden sisters waited, with a friend named Alice Russell staying close, the way friends do when a household has been broken open.

On the morning of Sunday, August seventh, Alice Russell walked into the kitchen and found Lizzie standing at the stove. In her hands was a dress. Russell would later testify that Lizzie was tearing it apart, feeding it piece by piece toward the fire.

Russell asked her what she was doing. And by the account she later gave under oath, Lizzie said she was, quote,

going to burn this old thing up; it is covered with paint.

Paint. The dress, Lizzie said, had been ruined by paint. And here is where two readings of the same small act split apart and never come back together.

To the prosecution, this was a woman destroying evidence in plain sight, burning a garment that might have carried the spatter of two killings. To the defense, it was exactly what Lizzie said it was, a soiled dress fed to a stove in a house with no shortage of grief and no shortage of paint. The defense never bothered to challenge the explanation at trial. And the jury, in the end, would not treat the burning as proof of anything.

But that was later. In the days right after the killings, the law moved quickly. On August eighth, an inquest opened, and Lizzie Borden was called to testify.

She testified without her family lawyer at her side. And she testified under the influence of morphine, a dose Dr. Bowen had prescribed to steady her nerves in the aftermath. Picture the position. A grieving woman, sedated, alone before a prosecutor, asked to account for her every movement on the morning her father and stepmother were hacked to death.

And her account would not hold still. Asked where she was when her father came home that morning, she placed herself in the kitchen. Then in the dining room. Then on the stairs. The story shifted under questioning, each version quietly contradicting the last.

Now, this matters, and you should hold onto it. Every one of those contradictions, the shifting rooms, the tangled timeline, was ruled inadmissible at her trial. The jury that would decide whether Lizzie Borden lived or died never heard a word of her inquest testimony. The most damaging thing she ever said was sealed away before twelve men were sworn in.

The jury did not hear it. But Fall River did. The inquest may have been closed to the public, but its substance leaked into the newspapers, and the papers carried Lizzie's contradictions out into the streets.

And the effect was immediate. One contemporary dispatch reported that her testimony had, quote,

caused a change of opinion among her friends who have heretofore strongly maintained her innocence.

Read that again. Her friends. The people who knew her best, who had stood by the Sunday school teacher, the respectable daughter of a respectable house, were beginning to wonder. In a town the size of Fall River, that shift in feeling was as good as a sentence.

On August eleventh, one week after the bodies were found, Lizzie Borden was served with a warrant and taken to jail.

The machinery of the law ground on through the autumn. In November, a grand jury began hearing evidence. On the second of December, eighteen ninety-two, it handed down an indictment. Lizzie Borden would stand trial for the murders of her father and her stepmother.

And so a churchgoing woman of thirty-two, who months earlier had taught children their scripture, spent the winter and spring in a cell, waiting for a courtroom in New Bedford to open its doors. When it finally did, the town would get its reckoning. And the State would learn just how little of its case it could actually prove.

By the summer of 1893, the question of who killed Andrew and Abby Borden had outgrown Fall River. It belonged now to a courthouse in New Bedford, where, on the fifth of June, the trial of their daughter began. The town that had buried its dead came to watch the law decide what the gossip already had.

The prosecution did not deal only in words. On that opening day, they brought the victims into the room. The skulls of Andrew and Abby Borden, stripped clean during the autopsy, were carried in and entered into evidence, set out before the jury as exhibits in the case.

At the sight of them, Lizzie Borden fainted. Whatever the jury made of that, the woman they were trying had just collapsed at the sight of what was left of her father and stepmother.

And there was something else in the air that June, something the jury could not entirely shut out. Days before the trial opened, a woman named Bertha Manchester had been found hacked to death in her own Fall River home, another body, another hatchet. Lizzie Borden was already in jail when it happened. The parallel was impossible to ignore, and it raised a quiet, dangerous question. If a killer with a hatchet was still loose in Fall River, had they arrested the wrong person?

The defense built its case on a single, stubborn fact. To have killed her father, Lizzie Borden had to have been in the house. And her account placed her somewhere else entirely.

She said that in the minutes around Andrew's return, she had gone out to the barn, climbed to the loft, and stayed there looking for sinkers to go fishing. Pressed on exactly how long she had been out there, away from the house, she gave the figure her lawyers would build everything around.

twenty minutes or possibly a half an hour

Twenty minutes, perhaps thirty. Long enough, if it were true, that she could not have been the one standing over Andrew Borden when the blows fell. The prosecution found it hard to believe. The loft was stifling in the August heat, the dust on the floor undisturbed, and no clear reason to linger there so long. But disbelief is not proof, and the state could not put her in the sitting room.

Then the defense produced a witness the prosecution had not accounted for. Hyman Lubinsky, an ice cream peddler passing the house, testified that he had seen a woman walking from the direction of the barn toward the side door at about three minutes past eleven, in the very window Lizzie described. It was not certainty. But it was a stranger, with no stake in the family, putting her where she said she was.

When the evidence closed, the case passed to the judge. And here the story turns on a detail that has troubled it ever since. The man who would instruct the jury, Justice Justin Dewey, had been appointed to the bench by George Robinson, back when Robinson was governor of Massachusetts. George Robinson was now sitting at the defense table, leading Lizzie Borden's defense.

Justice Dewey then delivered a charge to the jury so long, and so favorable to the accused, that observers described it less as a neutral instruction than as a second closing argument for the defense. From the bench, the judge gave the jury every reason to doubt.

The jury withdrew to decide whether Lizzie Borden would live or hang. They were gone for roughly an hour and a half.

Ninety minutes. They came back not guilty.

Outside the courthouse, a free woman now, Lizzie Borden was met by reporters who wanted to know how she felt, after ten months in a cell and a trial for her life. She told them she was something she had not been allowed to be in a long time.

the happiest woman in the world

The law had given its answer in ninety minutes. The town would take the rest of her life to give its own. Acquitted in a courtroom, Lizzie Borden walked back into a Fall River that had already made up its mind, and it would never let her forget it.

For ten months, Lizzie Borden had been a prisoner of the Commonwealth. Then, on the twentieth of June, 1893, after a jury deliberated for roughly ninety minutes, she became something she had not been since the previous August. She was free.

Outside the New Bedford courthouse, the reporters were waiting. The same reporters who had filled their columns with her name for the better part of a year. They asked her how she felt, now that it was over. And she told them she was, quote,

the happiest woman in the world

The happiest woman in the world. It is the kind of line that ought to close a story. A wrongful accusation answered, an innocent woman set right, the courthouse doors swinging open onto the rest of a quiet life. But that is not how Fall River heard it. And it is not how the rest of her life would go.

The jury had taken ninety minutes. The town had taken no time at all. Fall River had made up its mind long before the verdict was read, and the verdict did not change it. The acquittal made Lizzie Borden free. It did not make her welcome.

She had money now. Andrew Borden's estate passed to his two daughters, and Lizzie did not leave Fall River. She moved to a finer house on a better hill, and she lived out her years as something the town could neither forgive nor be rid of. A wealthy woman and an outcast, in the same breath.

And the question at the heart of it was never answered. No one else was ever arrested for the killings of Andrew and Abby Borden. No one else was ever brought to trial. The case remains open to this day. For more than a century, the writers and the historians have lined up on either side of it, unable to agree on whether the law had freed an innocent woman, or let a guilty one walk out into the summer afternoon.

Lizzie Borden died in Fall River in 1927, of pneumonia, at the age of sixty-six. Her sister Emma, who had stood beside her through the trial, died that same year. They left behind the town that had tried one of them, and never once stopped talking about her.

The court had given its verdict in a single afternoon. Two words. Not guilty. But a verdict is only what the law decides. It is not always what the world believes. And in the long quarrel between the two, the name Lizzie Borden became its own kind of sentence. A judgment the law itself never handed down. And never could.
